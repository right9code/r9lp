## SECTION A - GRAMMAR
**Changed Gender Passage:**
She owned a bull (or steer/bullock), two stallions, a colt and had even got a doe recently. She was a noted authoress (or author) and had married a professor. She was also an advisor to a viscount who had settled in Almora many years ago. The gentleman was loved by all. Her aunt trusted him implicitly and made him the executor of her will.
### Explanations:
 * **He \rightarrow She:** Change from masculine to feminine pronoun.
 * **heifer \rightarrow bull (or steer/bullock):** A heifer is a young female cow that has not borne a calf; its masculine counterpart is a young bull or steer.
 * **mares \rightarrow stallions:** A mare is a female adult horse; a stallion is an uncastrated male adult horse.
 * **filly \rightarrow colt:** A filly is a young female horse; a colt is a young male horse.
 * **buck \rightarrow doe:** A buck is a male deer, rabbit, or goat; a doe is the female counterpart.
 * **author \rightarrow authoress (or author):** Historically, "authoress" is the feminine form, though "author" is widely used as a gender-neutral term today.
 * **professor (or professoress) \rightarrow professor:** In modern English grammar, "professor" is gender-neutral. If changing the underlying gender of the spouse, it becomes a male professor.
 * **viscountess \rightarrow viscount:** A viscountess is a noblewoman holding the rank; a viscount is the nobleman.
 * **The lady \rightarrow The gentleman:** "Lady" refers to the viscountess; since it changed to viscount, the term becomes "gentleman" (or "lord").
 * **His uncle \rightarrow Her aunt:** "His" changes to "Her" to match the main character's new gender. "Uncle" changes to the feminine "aunt".
 * **trusted her \rightarrow trusted him:** The pronoun changes to masculine to refer back to the viscount/gentleman.
 * **executrix \rightarrow executor:** An executrix is a female appointed to carry out a will; an executor is the male counterpart.
 * **his will \rightarrow her will:** The possessive pronoun changes to feminine to match the aunt.

### 2. Summary of "Somebody’s Mother"
"Somebody’s Mother" by Mary Dow Brine is a touching poem that highlights the virtues of compassion, respect for the elderly, and the golden rule of kindness. The poem describes a cold, winter day where an old, frail woman stands at a busy street crossing, completely ignored by the rushing crowd. A group of schoolboys passes by, laughing and playing, but one cheerful boy notices her distress. He pauses, offers her his assistance, and safely guides her across the slippery street. Later, he tells his friends that he helped her because she is "somebody's mother," hoping that someday a stranger will similarly help his own mother when she is old and gray. The poem leaves us with a powerful moral message about empathy across generations.
