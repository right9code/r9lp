# A Concrete Example - Class 8 Comprehensive Notes

**Poet:** Reginald Arkell

## Complete Poem Text

My next-door neighbour, Mrs. Jones,
has got a garden full of stones:
A crazy path, a lily pond,
a rockery and, just beyond
A sundial with a strange device,
which Mrs. Jones thinks rather nice.

My next-door neighbour, Mrs. Jones,
puts little plants between the stones
They are so delicate and small,
they don't mean anything at all.
I can't think how she gets them in,
unless she plants them with a pin.

My next-door neighbour, Mrs. Jones,
once asked me round to see her stones.
We stood and talked about a flower
for quite a quarter of an hour.
"Where is this lovely thing?" I cried.
"You're standing on it," she replied.

**— Reginald Arkell**

---

## Detailed Summary

"A Concrete Example" by Reginald Arkell is a humorous and light-hearted poem that explores the difference between two people's perspectives on what makes a "garden." It focuses on the speaker's neighbor, Mrs. Jones, and her very unusual, stony garden.

### Stanza-wise Overview

*   **The Garden of Stones:** The speaker introduces his neighbor, Mrs. Jones, whose garden is remarkably different from a typical one—it is "full of stones." Instead of sprawling flower beds, it features a "crazy path," a "lily pond," a "rockery," and a "sundial." While these features might seem odd or barren to the speaker, Mrs. Jones finds them "rather nice," showing her pride in her unique creation.
*   **The Microscopic Plants:** Mrs. Jones does plant things, but they are so "delicate and small" that they are tucked into the tiny gaps "between the stones." To the speaker, these plants are so insignificant that they "don't mean anything at all." He humorously wonders how she even manages to get them into the ground, suggesting she might have to use a "pin" to plant them. This highlights the speaker's skepticism and amusement at Mrs. Jones’ gardening style.
*   **The Irony at the End:** Mrs. Jones invites the speaker into her garden to show him her treasures. They spend fifteen minutes ("quite a quarter of an hour") discussing a specific "flower" that Mrs. Jones clearly values. However, the speaker is so focused on the "stones" and the lack of visible greenery that he cannot even see the plant they are talking about. When he finally asks where this "lovely thing" is, the poem ends with a punchline: he is accidentally "standing on it." This concludes the poem with a humorous and "concrete" example of the speaker's failure to see what Mrs. Jones loves.

## Central Themes

### 1. Different Perspectives and Subjectivity
The poem highlights that beauty and value are subjective. What the speaker sees as just "stones" and "nothing at all," Mrs. Jones sees as a beautiful, "nice," and "lovely" garden. It suggests that people can look at the same "concrete" reality and see completely different things.

### 2. The Beauty of the Small and Subtle
Mrs. Jones represents a person who appreciates the minute, delicate details of nature. She finds joy in tiny plants that others might overlook or even step on. The poem encourages us to look closer and be more mindful of the small wonders around us.

### 3. Humour and Irony
The poet uses a light-hearted, conversational tone to poke fun at the speaker's lack of observation. The irony of the speaker standing on the very object he is searching for serves as a humorous lesson in paying attention.

## Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

**Rhyme Scheme:** AABBCC (couplets) — *Jones/stones, pond/beyond, device/nice*. The simple, bouncy rhyme scheme adds to the poem's light-hearted and humorous character.

**1. Pun (Wordplay)**
*   **The Title: "A Concrete Example"** — This is a clever pun. Literally, "concrete" refers to the hard, stony nature of Mrs. Jones’ garden (concrete is made from stones). Symbolically, the poem provides a "concrete" (solid/clear) example of how perspectives differ and how the speaker failed to see the beauty Mrs. Jones saw.

**2. Irony (Situational)**
*   The most significant irony occurs in the final lines. The speaker and Mrs. Jones spend fifteen minutes talking about a "lovely" flower, but the speaker cannot even see it. The irony is that the flower is so small (due to Mrs. Jones' style) and the speaker is so unobservant that he is actually crushing the very thing he is trying to admire.

**3. Hyperbole (Exaggeration)**
*   *"unless she plants them with a pin"* — The speaker exaggerates the smallness of the plants to emphasize how insignificant they seem to him. It creates a funny image and underscores his disbelief in her gardening methods.

**4. Alliteration**
*   ***s***o delicate and ***s***mall
*   ***s***undial... ***s***trange
*   ***p***lants... ***p***in
*   **Effect:** Creates a musical and rhythmic quality to the verse.

**5. Refrain**
*   *"My next-door neighbour, Mrs. Jones"* — This line opens every stanza, grounding the poem in the relationship between the two neighbors and establishing a repetitive, storytelling rhythm.

**6. Tone**
*   The tone is **humorous, light-hearted, and conversational.** The speaker isn't angry at Mrs. Jones; he is simply amused and slightly baffled by her choices, making the poem feel like a friendly anecdote.

## Textbook Questions and Answers

### II. Analytical and Reflective Questions

**1. How does Mrs. Jones feel about her garden? Support your answer with evidence from the poem.**
**Ans:** Mrs. Jones feels a deep sense of pride, affection, and genuine satisfaction regarding her unconventional garden, viewing it as a place of subtle beauty and "nice" features. The poem provides direct evidence for this when it states she thinks the sundial is "rather nice" and when she enthusiastically invites her neighbor "round to see her stones." Her willingness to spend "quite a quarter of an hour" talking about a single, tiny flower proves that she finds her garden deeply interesting and worth her time and focused attention. She uses positive, admiring words like "lovely thing" to describe her plants, showing that she doesn't see them as "insignificant" as the speaker does. Overall, her attitude is one of a dedicated and happy gardener who has created a world that perfectly suits her own unique perspective, regardless of how "strange" it might appear to others.

**2. Why do you think the speaker describes the plants as being so small that they could be planted with a pin?**
**Ans:** The speaker uses this humorous hyperbole to emphasize how extremely delicate and "insignificant" the plants appear from his own skeptical and unobservant perspective. By suggesting she "plants them with a pin," he is playfully mocking the scale of Mrs. Jones’ gardening, implying that her plants are so microscopic they are almost invisible and therefore "don't mean anything at all" to a casual observer. This description serves to highlight the stark contrast between the "hard" reality of the large stones and the "fragile" reality of the life tucked between them. It also brilliantly sets up the final irony of the poem: the plants are indeed so small and the speaker is so focused on the larger "stones" that he literally cannot see the flower and ends up accidentally standing on it. The "pin" imagery thus underscores the speaker's failure to appreciate the tiny, detailed beauty that Mrs. Jones treasures.

**3. What do we get to know about Mrs. Jones—based on her gardening style and her interaction with the speaker?**
**Ans:** Based on her gardening style, we learn that Mrs. Jones is an unconventional, patient, and highly observant person who finds beauty in subtle and "delicate" details that most people would completely ignore. Her "garden full of stones" suggests she is someone who doesn't follow the crowd and has a unique, perhaps more "architectural" or "zen-like," sense of aesthetics. In her interaction with the speaker, she comes across as a friendly, enthusiastic, and proud individual who is eager to share her passions, even if her neighbor doesn't quite "get" them. The fact that she can talk about a single flower for fifteen minutes shows she has a deep capacity for focus, mindfulness, and appreciation. Finally, her simple, factual reply—"You're standing on it"—at the end of the poem suggests she is observant enough to see exactly what the speaker is doing, and perhaps possesses a dry sense of humor about his clumsiness.

**4. The poem portrays Mrs. Jones in a positive light. Support this statement.**
**Ans:** The poem portrays Mrs. Jones in a highly positive light by presenting her as a person who finds happiness and beauty in her own unique way, without needing validation or approval from others. Although the speaker is slightly mocking and skeptical, the reader is actually encouraged to admire her "tenacity" in planting delicate life "between the stones" and her ability to find "lovely things" in a place that others might casually dismiss as barren. She is shown as a welcoming, open neighbor who invites people in, and her enthusiasm for her "rockery" and "sundial" is infectious and charming. By the end of the poem, the joke is squarely on the speaker, not on Mrs. Jones, because she is the one who truly "sees" and values the flower while he is the one clumsily and blindly crushing it. This suggests that her mindful "vision" of the world is much more accurate and meaningful than the speaker's superficial and unobservant one.

**5. What does the poem tell us about the way people think differently about the world around them?**
**Ans:** The poem is a "concrete example" of how subjectivity and personal perspective profoundly shape our experience of the world, teaching us that two people can look at the exact same physical space and see two completely different realities. For the speaker, the "stones" are an obstacle to beauty and the plants are meaningless, while for Mrs. Jones, the stones are the very "canvas" upon which her intricate garden is built. This tells us that our "mental lenses"—our expectations, biases, and levels of observation—determine what we value and what we ignore. The speaker’s failure to see the flower, despite talking about it for fifteen minutes, reveals how we can be completely "blind" to the truths and passions of others if we don't try to adopt their perspective. Ultimately, the poem suggests that the world is much richer than it appears on the surface, and that if we want to see the "lovely things," we must be willing to look past our own "stony" assumptions.
