# Somebody's Mother - Class 8 Comprehensive Notes

**Poet:** Mary Dow Brine
**(From the textbook *Poorvi*)**

---

## Complete Poem Text

The woman was old and ragged and grey
And bent with the chill of the Winter's day.
The street was wet with a recent snow
And the woman's feet were aged and slow.

She stood at the crossing and waited long,
Alone, uncared for, amid the throng
Of human beings who passed her by
Nor heeded the glance of her anxious eye.

Down the street, with laughter and shout,
Glad in the freedom of "school let out,"
Came the boys like a flock of sheep,
Hailing the snow piled white and deep.

Past the woman so old and grey
Hastened the children on their way,
Nor offered a helping hand to her—
So meek, so timid, afraid to stir

Lest the carriage wheels or the horses' feet
Should crowd her down in the slippery street.

At last came one of the merry troop,
The gayest laddie of all the group;
He paused beside her and whispered low,
"I'll help you cross, if you wish to go."

Her aged hand on his strong young arm
She placed, and so, without hurt or harm,
He guided the trembling feet along,
Proud that his own were firm and strong.

Then back again to his friends he went,
His young heart happy and well content.
"She's somebody's mother, boys, you know,
For all she's aged and poor and slow.

"And I hope some fellow will lend a hand
To help my mother, you understand,
If ever she's poor and old and grey,
When her own dear boy is far away."

And "somebody's mother" bowed low her head
In her home that night, and the prayer she said
Was, "God be kind to the noble boy,
Who is somebody's son and pride and joy!"

**— Mary Dow Brine**

## Detailed Summary

"Somebody's Mother" is a touching and moralistic poem by Mary Dow Brine that emphasizes empathy, kindness, and our shared humanity. It tells the story of an elderly woman and a young schoolboy who shows her unexpected compassion.

### Stanza-wise Overview

*   **The Helpless Old Woman:** The poem opens on a cold, bleak winter day. A busy street is covered in snow, making it slippery and dangerous. An elderly woman, described as "ragged and grey" and "bent with the chill," stands anxiously at the crossing. She is afraid to cross the street, fearing she might be trampled by the fast-moving horses, carriages, and the bustling crowd.
*   **The Indifferent Crowd:** Despite the heavy foot traffic, none of the hurried passersby stop to help or even acknowledge the frail old woman. They ignore her plight, focusing only on their own destinations.
*   **The Joyful Schoolboys:** A group of lively schoolboys comes rushing down the street, happy to be free from school. Like the adults, most of the boys pass the old woman without a second thought.
*   **An Act of Kindness:** One boy, however, notices her fear and hesitation. He pauses, steps out of his group, and gently offers the woman his arm. With his support and guidance, he helps her safely cross the treacherous, slippery road.
*   **The Boy's Reflection:** After guiding her across, the boy feels a deep sense of pride and happiness. When he rejoins his friends, he explains his actions: he says she is "somebody's mother." He expresses his hope that if his own mother is ever old, poor, and alone, some kind stranger will help her in the same way.
*   **A Mother's Prayer:** That night in her home, the old woman bows her head in prayer. She prays to God to bless the "noble boy" who showed her kindness, recognizing him as "somebody's son."

---

## Central Themes

### 1. Empathy and Compassion
The primary theme is the profound importance of noticing and addressing the needs of others, especially the vulnerable and elderly. The boy’s simple act of compassion contrasts sharply with the apathy of the rushing crowd.

### 2. Universal Humanity and "Paying it Forward"
The repeated phrase "somebody's mother" serves as a powerful reminder that every stranger is connected to someone who loves them. The boy helps the woman hoping that someone, somewhere, would do the same for his own mother—a beautiful example of the Golden Rule.

### 3. The Power of Small Acts
The poem demonstrates that an act of kindness does not have to be grand to be meaningful. A simple gesture, like helping someone across the street, can leave a lasting impact and bring immense gratitude.

## Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

**Rhyme Scheme:** AABB (couplets) throughout — *grey/day, snow/slow, long/throng, by/eye, shout/out, sheep/deep.* The consistent couplet rhyme creates a rhythmic, ballad-like flow perfect for storytelling.

**1. Imagery (Visual and Tactile)**
The poet paints vivid pictures of the winter scene:
*   *"The woman was old and ragged and grey / And bent with the chill of the Winter's day"* — visual and tactile imagery of cold, frailty, and age.
*   *"The street was wet with a recent snow"* — creates a cold, slippery, dangerous setting.
*   *"Her aged hand on his strong young arm"* — a powerful visual contrast between age and youth.

**2. Contrast / Antithesis**
The poem is built on sharp contrasts:
*   **Age vs. Youth:** *"trembling feet"* of the old woman vs. *"firm and strong"* feet of the boy.
*   **Indifference vs. Compassion:** The rushing crowd and other boys ignore her, while one boy stops.
*   **Weakness vs. Strength:** The frail, "meek, so timid" woman vs. the energetic, "gayest laddie."

**3. Simile**
*   *"Came the boys like a flock of sheep"* — compares the group of boys to a flock, emphasizing their herd mentality and carefree, thoughtless movement. Only one "sheep" breaks away to show kindness.

**4. Repetition / Refrain**
*   *"Somebody's mother"* — repeated multiple times as the poem's central motif.
*   *"old and grey," "aged and poor and slow"* — repeated descriptions reinforce the woman's vulnerability.
*   **Effect:** Transforms the anonymous old woman into someone universal — everyone's mother.

**5. Alliteration**
*   ***h***astened... ***h***elping ***h***and
*   ***s***o meek, ***s***o timid
*   ***p***aused... ***p***roud
*   **Effect:** Adds musical quality and emphasis.

**6. Personification**
*   *"His young heart happy and well content"* — the heart is given the ability to feel happiness independently.

**7. Irony**
*   The boys are described as *"glad in the freedom of 'school let out'"* — their freedom contrasts with the old woman's imprisonment (she is trapped, unable to cross). The irony highlights how people often celebrate their own joy while ignoring others' suffering.

**8. Tone**
The tone shifts through the poem:
*   **Opening:** Melancholic, bleak, empathetic toward the old woman.
*   **Middle:** Warm, hopeful, and admiring (when the boy acts).
*   **Ending:** Grateful, prayerful



## Textbook Questions and Answers

### II. Analytical and Reflective Questions

**1. What might be the old woman's fears as she waited for a long time at the crossing?**
**Ans:** As the old woman stood "anxious and timid" at the busy crossing, her primary fear was likely being knocked down and trampled by the horses, carriages, or the careless, rushing crowds. The winter street was described as "white with snow," making it dangerously slippery and difficult for her frail legs to navigate. Furthermore, she might have feared that her "aged hand" and weak frame would completely fail her in the middle of the street, leaving her stranded and vulnerable to the fast-moving traffic. The indifference of the passing crowd likely compounded her fear, making her feel invisible, abandoned, and terrified that no one would stop to help her if she fell or needed assistance.

**2. How does the poet show the helplessness of the old woman? Support your answer with evidence from the poem.**
**Ans:** The poet vividly portrays the old woman's helplessness through powerful descriptive language that highlights her physical frailty and emotional vulnerability. She is described as being "old and ragged and gray," bent over with the "chill of the winter’s day," which immediately establishes her physical weakness. Her helplessness is most evident in her hesitation at the crossing; the poem states she was "afraid to stir" because she feared the "carriage wheels or the horses' feet" would trample her. The lines "She stood at the crossing and waited long, / Alone, uncared for, amid the throng" perfectly capture her isolation and the heartbreaking reality that she is completely unable to cross the dangerous street without the assistance that the crowd is ignoring.

**3. Why does the boy decide to help the old woman? What does this tell us about him?**
**Ans:** The boy decides to help the old woman out of a profound sense of empathy, recognizing her vulnerability and seeing her not just as a stranger, but as "somebody's mother." He helps her because he hopes that, in the future, if his own mother is ever old, poor, and in need of assistance when he is far away, another kind stranger will step up to help her just as he did. This decision tells us that the boy possesses an exceptional level of emotional maturity, compassion, and moral vision compared to his peers. He understands the universal human connection—that we are all bound by ties of family and shared vulnerability—and he acts on the golden rule of treating others the way he would want his own loved ones to be treated.

**4. How was the boy's approach towards the old woman different from that of the other boys in the group?**
**Ans:** The boy's approach was radically different because it was characterized by gentle compassion and proactive kindness, whereas the other boys displayed thoughtless indifference and self-absorption. While the rest of the group passed by the old woman "like a flock of sheep," caught up in their own noise, laughter, and excitement without even glancing at her distress, this particular boy "paused beside her" and offered his strong, young arm to guide her. He did not mock her frailty or rush past her; instead, he softly whispered an offer to help her across. His actions demonstrated individual moral courage—the willingness to step away from the crowd and do the right thing—while the others simply followed the herd in ignoring someone in need.

**5. Justify the appropriateness of the title of the poem.**
**Ans:** The title "Somebody's Mother" is highly appropriate and central to the poem's core message because it humanizes the old woman, shifting her identity from a nameless, ragged stranger to a figure worthy of universal respect and care. It reflects the pivotal realization that motivates the young boy to help her: he understands that despite her current poverty and frailty, she is connected to a family and was once a nurturing mother to someone. By framing her in this relational context, the title appeals to the reader's own sense of family and empathy, suggesting that everyone we encounter is someone's loved one. It effectively encapsulates the poem's theme that our moral obligation to help the vulnerable is rooted in our shared human experience of love and family.

**6. What does the poem suggest about the role of empathy and humanity in society?**
**Ans:** The poem powerfully suggests that empathy and humanity are the essential bonds that hold society together and protect its most vulnerable members from cold indifference. It critiques a society where a crowd can walk past a terrified, elderly woman without offering aid, showing how easily people can become desensitized to the suffering of others in their own busy lives. However, through the actions of the young boy, the poem illustrates that a single act of empathy—born from the ability to see a stranger as "somebody's mother"—has the power to restore dignity, provide safety, and bring immense emotional comfort. Ultimately, it teaches that true humanity is not found in grand gestures, but in the quiet, everyday compassion we show to those who cannot help themselves, creating a ripple effect of kindness that we may one day need ourselves.
