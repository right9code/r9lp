# Harvest Hymn - Class 8 Comprehensive Notes

**Poet:** Sarojini Naidu
**(From the textbook *Poorvi*)**

## Complete Poem Text

**Men's Voices:**
Lord of the lotus, lord of the harvest,
Bright and munificent lord of the morn!
Thine is the bounty that prospered our sowing,
Thine is the bounty that nurtured our corn.
We bring thee our songs and our garlands for tribute,
The gold of our fields and the gold of our fruit;
O giver of mellowing radiance, we hail thee,
We praise thee, O Surya, with cymbal and flute.

**Women's Voices:**
Queen of the woodland, queen of the harvest,
Bird-winged and bountiful, mother of mirth!
Thine is the bosom that nourished our children,
Thine is the womb where our riches have birth.
We bring thee our love and our garlands for tribute,
With gifts of the opulent giving we come;
O source of our manifold gladness, we hail thee,
We praise thee, O Prithvi, with cymbal and drum.

**All Voices:**
Lord of the Universe, Lord of our being,
Father eternal, ineffable Om!
Thou art the Seed and the Scythe of our harvests,
Thou art our Hands and our Heart and our Home.

**— Sarojini Naidu**

---

## Detailed Summary

"Harvest Hymn" by Sarojini Naidu is a lyrical and deeply spiritual poem that serves as a song of gratitude and praise for a successful agricultural harvest. It beautifully captures the harmonious relationship between rural communities, the forces of nature, and the divine.

### Stanza-wise Overview

The poem is structured as a communal act of worship, divided into three distinct voices that represent different groups within the community giving thanks:

*   **Men’s Voices (Praising Sun and Rain):** The poem begins with the voices of the men, who direct their gratitude toward the heavens—specifically to Surya (the Sun God) and Varuna (the Rain God). They acknowledge that the Sun's "mellowing radiance" (gentle light) is what ripens the crops, and Varuna’s life-giving rain is what nurtures the fields. To show their profound appreciation, the men offer the "gold" of their fields (the ripened, harvested crops) as a tribute, celebrating with musical instruments like cymbals, flutes, and pipes.
*   **Women’s Voices (Praising the Earth):** The second part features the voices of the women, who address Prithvi (Mother Earth). They refer to the Earth as the "queen of the harvest" and an "omnipotent mother." The women recognize the Earth as the fertile, nurturing source that physically holds and feeds the seeds, eventually bringing forth prosperity. They offer their love, garlands, and gifts, praising the Earth's bounty with the rhythmic sounds of cymbals and drums.
*   **All Voices / Chorus (Praising the Supreme Divine):** In the final stanza, the voices of the men and women unite in a collective chorus. Together, they direct their ultimate praise to the Supreme Divine (the Lord of the Universe). They acknowledge the Divine as the ultimate creator and sustainer of all life, referring to Him as both the "Seed" (the beginning of life) and the "Scythe" (the completion of the harvest cycle).

## Central Themes

### 1. Gratitude and Thanksgiving
The core theme of the poem is deep, sincere gratitude. The farmers do not take their successful harvest for granted; instead, they recognize it as a blessing and actively offer thanks through songs, music, and the very crops they have grown.

### 2. Harmony Between Humans and Nature
The poem highlights that agriculture is not just human labor, but a sacred partnership. The farmers depend entirely on the Sun for light, the Rain for water, and the Earth for nourishment. The poem portrays nature not as an exploitable resource, but as a set of divine entities worthy of respect and worship.

### 3. Unity and Community Celebration
The structure of the poem—moving from men to women, and finally to all voices together—emphasizes that the harvest is a collective experience. The entire community is bound together by their shared labor, their shared joy in the harvest, and their shared faith.

## Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

**Rhyme Scheme:** Free / Loose — the poem does not follow strict end-rhyme. Instead, it relies on internal rhythm, parallelism, and musical diction to create its hymn-like quality (*corn/morn, fruit/flute, mirth/birth, come/drum/Om/Home*).

**1. Personification / Apostrophe**
Natural elements are addressed directly as living deities:
*   *"Lord of the lotus, lord of the harvest"* — the Sun (Surya) is addressed as a divine lord.
*   *"Queen of the woodland, queen of the harvest"* — the Earth (Prithvi) is addressed as a queen and mother.
*   *"Father eternal, ineffable Om!"* — the Supreme Divine is addressed as a father.
*   **Effect:** Creates a devotional, prayer-like atmosphere.

**2. Metaphor**
Multiple metaphors enrich the spiritual meaning:
*   *"The gold of our fields and the gold of our fruit"* — the harvest is compared to gold, emphasizing its immense value.
*   *"Thou art the Seed and the Scythe of our harvests"* — God is both the beginning (Seed = creation) and the end (Scythe = harvesting), encompassing the entire cycle of life.
*   *"mellowing radiance"* — sunlight is compared to a gentle, ripening force.

**3. Parallelism (Structural Repetition)**
The poem's structure mirrors itself across stanzas:
*   *"Lord of the lotus, lord of the harvest"* || *"Queen of the woodland, queen of the harvest"* — identical grammatical structure.
*   *"We bring thee our songs"* || *"We bring thee our love"* — parallel offerings.
*   *"We praise thee, O Surya, with cymbal and flute"* || *"We praise thee, O Prithvi, with cymbal and drum"* — mirrored praise.
*   **Effect:** Creates a chant-like, ritualistic rhythm perfect for a hymn.

**4. Imagery**
*   *Visual:* *"gold of our fields," "garlands for tribute," "bird-winged and bountiful"* — rich, warm harvest visuals.
*   *Auditory:* *"cymbal and flute," "cymbal and drum"* — the sounds of festive celebration.
*   *Tactile:* *"mellowing radiance"* — the warm, gentle touch of the sun.

**5. Alliteration**
*   ***b***right and ***b***ountiful, ***b***ounty, ***b***osom, ***b***ird-winged
*   ***m***ellowing... ***m***anifold... ***m***irth... ***m***other
*   ***g***old... ***g***arlands... ***g***iver... ***g***ladness
*   **Effect:** Creates a musical, rolling rhythm suited to a song of praise.

**6. Epithet**
Descriptive titles are used to honour the deities:
*   *"Bright and munificent lord of the morn"* — for Surya.
*   *"Bird-winged and bountiful, mother of mirth"* — for Prithvi.
*   *"Father eternal, ineffable Om"* — for the Supreme Divine.

**7. Allusion (Religious/Cultural)**
The poem is rich in Hindu cultural references:
*   **Surya** — the Sun God.
*   **Prithvi** — Mother Earth, revered as a goddess.
*   **Om** — the sacred, universal syllable in Hinduism.
*   **Seed and Scythe** — alludes to the cyclical nature of creation and destruction.

**8. Tone**
The tone is **devotional, reverent, and celebratory.** It progresses from specific gratitude (to the Sun, then the Earth) to universal praise (to the Supreme Divine), creating a spiritual crescendo.

## Textbook Questions and Answers

### II. Analytical and Reflective Questions

**1. Why do the farmers hail the God of rain?**
**Ans:** The farmers hail the God of rain, whom they address as Surya or Varuna depending on the tradition, because rain is the absolute lifeblood of their agricultural existence. In a rural economy dependent on the monsoon, timely and sufficient rainfall is the single most critical factor that determines whether seeds will sprout, crops will mature, and the community will survive the year. They hail the rain god out of a deep sense of gratitude, acknowledging that without the "showering" grace of the heavens, all their hard physical labor in plowing and planting would be completely futile, resulting in famine rather than a bountiful harvest.

**2. What is being referred to as ‘wealth of our valleys’? Why?**
**Ans:** The "wealth of our valleys" refers to the rich, abundant agricultural produce—the golden wheat, the ripe corn, the fruits, and the vegetables—that has just been successfully harvested. The poet uses the word "wealth" not in the modern, monetary sense of gold or currency, but in the most fundamental, life-sustaining sense. For the farmers, true wealth is food security; a full granary means that their families will not go hungry during the harsh winter months. It is the tangible reward of their year-long partnership with nature, representing physical survival, communal prosperity, and the joyous culmination of their shared labor in the fertile valleys.

**3. State one reason why the women refer to the Earth as a ‘source of manifold gladness’.**
**Ans:** The women refer to the Earth as a "source of manifold gladness" because they recognize the earth as a nurturing, maternal entity that continuously provides for all their complex needs in countless ("manifold") ways. It is not just the source of basic food; it is the source of the flowers that bring beauty to their homes, the cotton that provides clothing, the clay for their pots, and the very ground upon which their families live and play. They view the earth not as a dead resource to be exploited, but as a generous, living mother who responds to their care with an endless variety of gifts that bring joy and comfort to every aspect of their daily rural lives.

**4. Why does everyone in the poem offer their ‘labours for tribute’?**
**Ans:** Everyone in the poem offers their "labours for tribute" as an act of profound humility and devotion, recognizing that humans are merely co-creators in the grand scheme of nature. By offering the physical exhaustion, the sweat, and the effort it took to harvest the crops back to the divine forces (the sun, rain, and earth), they are acknowledging that human effort alone is useless without divine blessing. It is a way of saying "thank you" by dedicating the very act of their hard work to the gods, showing that they do not take the harvest for granted and that they view their agricultural labor as a sacred, spiritual duty rather than just a commercial enterprise.

**5. Why has the poet presented the poem as voices of men, women, and all voices?**
**Ans:** The poet, Sarojini Naidu, presents the poem through the distinct but harmonious voices of "Men," "Women," and "All Voices" to emphasize that the harvest is a deeply communal, inclusive event that requires the participation of the entire village. By giving men and women specific verses, she highlights their complementary roles in the agrarian lifestyle, showing that survival depends on gendered but equally valuable contributions to the labor. When the poem culminates in "All Voices," it beautifully symbolizes the unity of the community coming together in shared celebration and collective gratitude, demonstrating that the joy of the harvest erases individual differences and binds the society into a single, thankful chorus.

**6. How does the poet create the joyous atmosphere of rural India at harvesting time?**
**Ans:** The poet creates a joyous, vibrant atmosphere through the masterful use of sensory imagery, rhythmic meter, and exultant language that mimics the sounds and feelings of a rural festival. Words like "cymbal," "flute," "garlands," and "gladness" instantly evoke a scene of musical celebration, dancing, and vibrant colors. The repetitive, chanting rhythm of the poem ("Lord of the universe... Lord of the rainbow") mirrors the traditional, rhythmic singing of farmers working in the fields or celebrating around a fire. Furthermore, the imagery of "golden" crops and "shimmering" valleys paints a picture of glorious abundance, effectively transporting the reader into the heart of a sun-drenched, joyful Indian village at the peak of its agricultural triumph.

**7. Why is the poem presented as a hymn?**
**Ans:** The poem is presented as a "hymn"—a religious song or poem of praise to God—because the act of farming in traditional Indian culture is fundamentally intertwined with spirituality and faith. The harvest is not viewed merely as an economic transaction, but as a divine miracle where the forces of nature (Sun, Earth, Water) align to sustain human life. By structuring it as a hymn, complete with verses of praise, direct addresses to the divine ("O Lord"), and collective choruses of gratitude, the poet elevates the physical act of agriculture into a sacred ritual. It reflects the deeply ingrained belief that humanity is entirely dependent on the grace of the creator, making a song of worship the most natural and appropriate response to a successful harvest.
