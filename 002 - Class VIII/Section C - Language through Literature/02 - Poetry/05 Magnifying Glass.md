# Magnifying Glass - Class 8 Comprehensive Notes

**Poet:** Walter de la Mare
**(From the textbook *Poorvi*)**

---

## Complete Poem Text

With this round glass
I can make Magic talk—
A myriad shells show
In a scrap of chalk;

Of but an inch of moss
A forest—flowers and trees;
A drop of water, like
A hive of bees.

I lie in wait and watch
How the deft spider jets
The woven web-silk
From his spinnerets;

The tigerish claws he has!
And oh! the silly flies
That stumble into his net—
With all those eyes!

Not even the tiniest thing
But this my glass
Will make more marvellous
And itself surpass.

Yes, and with lenses like it,
Eyeing the moon,
'Twould seem you'd walk there
In an afternoon!

**— Walter de la Mare**

---

## Detailed Summary

"Magnifying Glass" by Walter de la Mare is an observant and thought-provoking poem that celebrates the hidden wonders of the natural world. Through the use of a simple lens, the poet demonstrates how ordinary, tiny objects can be transformed into extraordinary, magical landscapes.

### Stanza-wise Overview

*   **Transforming the Ordinary:** The poem begins with the poet using a magnifying glass to look closely at everyday, seemingly insignificant objects. Through the lens, the ordinary world completely transforms. A small, dull scrap of chalk reveals itself to be made up of countless tiny, ancient shells. 
*   **Forests and Hives in Miniature:** The poet continues his magnified exploration. A tiny inch of green moss suddenly looks like a vast, lush forest filled with towering trees and blooming flowers. A single, small drop of water is magnified to look like a busy, bustling hive swarming with bees, revealing that life is everywhere, even in the smallest spaces.
*   **The Skillful Spider:** The poet then turns the glass on a living creature—a spider. Magnified, the spider looks formidable, with claws like a tiger. The poet marvels at the incredible precision and skill with which the spider spins its delicate web. He notes the irony that flies, despite having many eyes, are still "silly" enough to get caught in this expertly crafted trap.
*   **The Power of Lenses:** In the final lines, the poet zooms out from the microscopic world and thinks about the macroscopic world. He reflects on the power of lenses in general. He suggests that if one had a powerful enough lens (like a telescope), they could look at the moon and see it so clearly and closely that it would feel as if they could simply take a walk there in an afternoon.

---

## Central Themes

### 1. Curiosity and the Joy of Discovery
The poem strongly encourages readers to be curious and to observe their surroundings with patience. It suggests that nothing in the world is truly boring or "ordinary" if you take the time to look at it closely.

### 2. Magic in the Everyday
The poet finds true "magic" not in fantasy, but in science and nature. Simple things like chalk, moss, and water drops hold complex, beautiful worlds within them, proving that the everyday world is full of marvels waiting to be discovered.

### 3. Science Enhancing Imagination
The magnifying glass represents science and observation. Rather than destroying imagination, the tool enhances it. The lens allows the poet to see the scientific reality of the chalk and the spider, but it also sparks his imagination to see forests in moss and envision walking on the moon.

## Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

**Rhyme Scheme:** ABCB in each quatrain (*talk/chalk, trees/bees, watch/jets [slant], has/flies [off-rhyme], thing/glass [off], it/moon [off]*). The loose, sometimes imperfect rhyme creates a natural, conversational tone.

**1. Simile**
*   *"A drop of water, like / A hive of bees"* — a magnified water drop is compared to a busy beehive, emphasizing the microscopic life teeming within it.
*   *"The tigerish claws he has!"* — the spider's claws are compared to a tiger's, making the tiny creature appear fierce and predatory.

**2. Metaphor**
*   *"I can make Magic talk"* — the magnifying glass is a metaphor for science and curiosity. "Magic" here is a metaphor for the hidden wonders of nature revealed through observation.
*   *"Of but an inch of moss / A forest"* — a tiny patch of moss is transformed into an entire forest.

**3. Imagery (Visual)**
The poem is rich with detailed visual pictures:
*   *"A myriad shells show / In a scrap of chalk"* — reveals the microscopic, ancient structure of chalk.
*   *"flowers and trees"* in an inch of moss — the miniature world.
*   *"The woven web-silk / From his spinnerets"* — the precise, delicate process of web-spinning.

**4. Personification**
*   *"I can make Magic talk"* — Magic is personified as something that can speak.
*   *"the silly flies / That stumble into his net"* — the flies are given the human quality of being "silly" (foolish).

**5. Irony**
*   *"the silly flies / That stumble into his net— / With all those eyes!"* — the irony is that flies have compound eyes with thousands of lenses, yet they still cannot see the spider's web. Despite their many "eyes," they are blind to danger.

**6. Hyperbole (Exaggeration)**
*   *"'Twould seem you'd walk there / In an afternoon!"* — exaggerates the power of lenses by suggesting the moon would appear close enough to walk to. Creates a sense of wonder about the power of science.

**7. Exclamatory Expression**
*   *"The tigerish claws he has!"* and *"And oh! the silly flies"* — the exclamations convey the poet's genuine excitement and wonder at his discoveries, making the reader share his enthusiasm.

**8. Tone**
The tone is **curious, wonder-filled, and playful.** The poet approaches the natural world with childlike excitement, marvelling at each discovery as if seeing the world for the first time. There is a gentle humour in his observations (especially about the "silly" flies).

## Textbook Questions and Answers

### II. Analytical and Reflective Questions

**1. What is the significance of the spider in the poem?**
**Ans:** The spider in the poem is highly significant because it serves as the ultimate proof of the microscopic, hidden complexities of nature that are invisible to the naked eye. Through the magnifying glass, the speaker is able to see the intricate, almost mechanical details of the spider—its "hairy legs," its "many eyes," and the delicate precision with which it moves. The spider transforms from a simple, easily ignored bug into a fascinating, formidable creature, symbolizing the idea that even the smallest, most common elements of our environment contain vast, awe-inspiring worlds of their own if we only take the time and use the right tools to look closely enough.

**2. How might the speaker's view of the natural world change if there was no use of a magnifying glass?**
**Ans:** If the speaker did not use a magnifying glass, their view of the natural world would likely remain superficial, broad, and somewhat disconnected from its deeper realities. Without the lens, the "veins of a leaf" would just look like a flat green surface, the "dust on a moth's wing" would just look like grey powder, and the intricate structure of the spider would remain unseen. The magnifying glass acts as a gateway to wonder; without it, the speaker would miss the profound realization that nature is incredibly detailed and carefully constructed at every level. Their appreciation for the environment would lack the depth, curiosity, and awe that comes from understanding how complex even a single blade of grass truly is.

**3. Why does the poem end with the idea of the moon being within reach?**
**Ans:** The poem ends with the idea of the moon being within reach to metaphorically illustrate the expansive, almost magical power of observation and curiosity. While a magnifying glass makes small, close things appear larger, the poet uses this concept to suggest that focused attention can also make distant, seemingly impossible things feel closer and more understandable. The moon represents the ultimate limit of human exploration; by stating it is "within reach," the poem implies that the same curiosity that allows us to understand a tiny spider can eventually lead us to understand the vast universe. It is a poetic leap that connects the microscopic wonder of the earth to the macroscopic wonder of the cosmos.

**4. What is the speaker's attitude towards nature and the act of observation?**
**Ans:** The speaker's attitude is one of deep reverence, intense curiosity, and joyful fascination. Nature is not viewed simply as a background, but as a complex puzzle and a source of endless wonder waiting to be explored. The act of observation is treated almost like a sacred ritual or an exciting adventure; the speaker is not just casually looking, but actively "magnifying" their experience to uncover hidden truths. The tone of the poem suggests that observing the small details of nature brings the speaker profound satisfaction and a sense of connection to the world, demonstrating a belief that careful, deliberate observation is the key to truly appreciating the beauty and complexity of life.

**5. Which is your favourite part of the poem? Why?**
**Ans:** My favorite part of the poem is the description of the "dust on a moth's wing" looking like "tiny, colored tiles." This imagery is particularly striking because it takes something that usually seems annoying or dirty—the powdery residue left by a moth—and elevates it into a beautiful, intricate mosaic. It perfectly captures the essence of the poem: that beauty is often hidden in the most unexpected places and requires a shift in perspective to be seen. The comparison to "colored tiles" is incredibly vivid and relatable, making the abstract concept of microscopic observation feel tangible and artistic. It is a brilliant example of how science (magnification) and art (poetry) can combine to change how we view the world.
