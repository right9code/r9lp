# Nine Gold Medals - Class 9 Comprehensive Notes

**Poet:** David Roth
**(From the textbook *Kaveri*)**

---

## Complete Poem Text

The athletes had come from so many countries
To run for the gold, for the silver and bronze
Many weeks and months in training
All building up to the games.

All round the field spectators were gathered
Cheering on all the young women and men
Then the final event of the day was approaching
The last race about to begin.

The loudspeakers called out the names of the runners
The one hundred metres the race to be run
And nine young athletes stood there determined
And poised for the sound of the gun.

The signal was given, the pistol exploded
And so did the runners all charging ahead
But the smallest among them, he stumbled and staggered
And fell to the ground on his knees.

He gave out a cry of frustration and anguish
His dreams and his efforts all dashed in the dirt
But as sure as I'm standing here telling the story
Now it's a strange one, but here's what occurred.

The eight other athletes stopped in their tracks
The ones who had trained for so long to compete
One by one they all turned round, and came back to help him
And lifted the lad to his feet.

Then all the nine runners joined hands and continued
The hundred-metre race reduced to a walk
And the banner above that said "Special Olympics"
Could not have been nearer the mark.

That's how the race ended, with nine gold medals
They came to the finish line holding hands still
And the banner above and nine smiling faces
Said more than these words ever will.

**— David Roth**

---

## Detailed Summary

"Nine Gold Medals" by David Roth is an immensely inspirational poem that narrates a deeply moving incident during the Special Olympics, a sporting event for athletes with intellectual or physical disabilities. 

### Stanza-wise Overview

*   **The Preparation & Anticipation:** The poem begins by setting the scene of the Special Olympics. Athletes from all over the country have gathered after months of rigorous training, all sharing the same dream: to win a gold, silver, or bronze medal. A large crowd of spectators has gathered around the field, cheering and waiting with bated breath for the final and most anticipated event of the day—the hundred-yard dash.
*   **The Race Begins:** Nine athletes line up at the starting blocks, their faces tense with focus and determination. When the pistol fires, signaling the start of the race, they all burst forward.
*   **The Incident:** Tragedy strikes almost immediately. The youngest and smallest runner among them suddenly stumbles, loses his balance, and falls to the asphalt ground. He cries out in immense pain and frustration, realizing that his months of hard work and dreams of winning have been shattered in an instant.
*   **The Unexpected Turn:** What happens next is a display of extraordinary humanity. The other eight runners, who had trained equally hard for this moment, do not continue running to secure their own victories. Upon hearing the boy's cry, they all stop in their tracks. One by one, they turn back and run to the fallen athlete's side. They help him get back on his feet.
*   **The Finish:** In a powerful act of solidarity, all nine athletes link their arms together. The high-speed race transforms into a slow, united walk. They all cross the finish line together as a team.
*   **The Conclusion:** Because they all crossed the finish line at the exact same time, the authorities award all nine athletes a gold medal. They stand together under a banner that reads "Special Olympics," their beaming faces conveying a message far more powerful than words: true victory lies in lifting each other up.

---

## Central Themes

### 1. Compassion Over Competition
The most prominent theme is that human empathy, kindness, and moral victory are far more valuable than physical achievement or defeating opponents. The eight athletes prioritized the well-being of a fallen competitor over their own desires to win a medal.

### 2. Redefining Success and Sportsmanship
The poem powerfully redefines what it means to win. Traditional sports focus on a single victor who leaves the rest behind. However, the athletes in the poem show that the highest level of sportsmanship is inclusivity. True success is a collective joy; it is walking to the finish line together rather than running alone.

## Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

**Rhyme Scheme:** Loosely ABCB in some stanzas (*bronze/games, men/begin, run/gun*), but largely relies on a narrative, ballad-like rhythm rather than strict rhyme. This free-flowing structure mirrors the storytelling nature of the poem.

**1. Alliteration**
Repetition of initial consonant sounds to add emphasis and rhythm:
*   *"His **d**reams and his efforts all **d**ashed in the **d**irt"* — the repeated harsh 'd' sound emphasizes the sudden, devastating impact of the fall.
*   *"**s**tumbled and **s**taggered"* — the 's' sound conveys the physical instability.
*   *"**s**ignal… **s**ound"* — builds the tension of the starting moment.

**2. Climax and Anti-Climax**
The poem follows a carefully constructed emotional arc:
*   **Climax:** The build-up of anticipation—months of training, the crowd cheering, the runners poised, and the pistol firing.
*   **Anti-Climax:** The sudden fall of the smallest runner shatters the expected competitive outcome.
*   **True Climax (Reversal):** The unexpected return of all eight runners to help — this becomes the poem's *real* emotional peak.

**3. Imagery**
Vivid visual and emotional pictures are painted throughout:
*   *"fell to the ground on his knees"* — the reader can visualize the heartbreaking fall.
*   *"nine smiling faces"* — a warm, triumphant visual of unity.
*   *"the banner above that said 'Special Olympics'"* — creates a concrete, physical setting.

**4. Irony (Situational)**
The poem is built on a powerful ironic reversal:
*   A **race** (which by definition has one winner) ends with **nine gold medals** and no losers.
*   Athletes who **stop running** end up achieving a **greater victory** than any single winner could.

**5. Metaphor**
*   *"His dreams and his efforts all dashed in the dirt"* — "dashed" is a metaphor for the destruction of hopes. The literal fall on dirt mirrors the metaphorical shattering of aspirations.
*   *"The hundred-metre race reduced to a walk"* — the "walk" is a metaphor for solidarity and togetherness replacing competition.

**6. Enjambment**
Sentences flow across multiple lines without pause:
*   *"But as sure as I'm standing here telling the story / Now it's a strange one, but here's what occurred"* — the run-on lines create a conversational, storytelling tone.

**7. Personification**
*   *"the pistol exploded"* — the pistol is given the dramatic quality of an explosion, heightening the starting moment.
*   *"nine smiling faces / Said more than these words ever will"* — faces are given the ability to "speak" a message.

**8. Tone Shift**
The poem's tone shifts dramatically:
*   **Stanzas 1–3:** Excited, anticipatory, competitive.
*   **Stanza 4–5:** Tragic, heartbreaking.
*   **Stanzas 6–8:** Uplifting, triumphant, inspirational.

## Textbook Questions and Answers

1. Describe how the setting established in the first two stanzas of the poem create a vivid atmosphere for the events that follow.
**Ans:** The first two stanzas build a high-stakes, competitive atmosphere. The mention of athletes training for "months" and the large crowd of "spectators" waiting with "bated breath" creates a sense of immense pressure, making the subsequent act of kindness even more extraordinary.

2. How do you think the youngest athlete might have felt when he fell?
**Ans:** The youngest athlete likely felt a wave of intense "anguish" and utter devastation that is difficult for an outsider to fully comprehend. For an athlete, especially one in the Special Olympics, the 100-meter dash is the culmination of "many weeks and months in training" and a lifetime of overcoming personal hurdles. Stumbling right at the start of the final race meant that his chance to prove himself and his dreams of winning a medal were "dashed in the dirt" in a split second. His cry was not just one of physical pain, but a deep emotional release of the frustration and heartbreak that comes from seeing one's hard-earned aspirations vanish in an instant.

3. Why were the athletes eager to begin the race?
**Ans:** They were eager because this was the "final event of the day"—the culmination of their intense training. They were all poised and determined to win a medal and prove their athletic capabilities.

4. What does the transformation of the hundred-yard dash to a walk symbolise?
**Ans:** The transformation of the 100-meter race into a slow, united walk is a powerful symbol of the triumph of human empathy over individual competition. It represents a radical shift in values, where the well-being and dignity of a fallen comrade become more important than personal glory or breaking records. By linking hands and walking across the finish line together, the athletes demonstrated that true victory lies in solidarity and mutual support. This act redefined the "Special Olympics" as a platform not just for physical athleticism, but for the "Special" spirit of humanity that prioritizes lifting others up over leaving them behind in the pursuit of success.

5. How might the poem be different if the focus was solely on individual achievement rather than collective support?
**Ans:** If the focus was individual, the other eight runners would have simply kept running to win. The poem would have been a standard story of victory and defeat, rather than a powerful lesson on the "Special" spirit of humanity.

6. How does the poet's use of language and tone enhance the reader's engagement with the poem?
**Ans:** The poet uses simple, heart-touching language ("anguish", "smiling faces") and a narrative tone. The shift from competitive excitement to heartbreaking tragedy and finally to uplifting triumph keeps the reader emotionally connected to the runners.

7. What might be the poet's purpose of writing this poem?
**Ans:** The poet's purpose is to remind the world that kindness, empathy, and "lifting others up" are the greatest medals one can win. He wants to celebrate the true spirit of sportsmanship that goes beyond just winning a race.
