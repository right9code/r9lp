**Poet:** Charles Swain

## Complete Poem Text

If words could satisfy the heart,
The heart might find less care;
But words, like summer birds, depart,
And leave but empty air.

The heart—a pilgrim upon earth—
Finds them, when needed, weeds;
And feels, in sorrow's hour of dearth,
That few are all it needs.

Hosts of words, which reach the head,
But never touch the heart;
With many a graceful sentence said,
Yet play a hollow part.

The voice that wins its sunny way,
A lonely home to cheer,
Hath of the fewest words to say,
But, oh! those few are dear!

If words could satisfy the chest,
The world might hold a feast;
But words, when summoned to the test,
Oft satisfy the least!

They make a gaudy show of leaves,
And bloom, and branches wide;
Yet, he who trusts their bloom, receives
No fruit to be his pride.

**— Charles Swain**

---

## Detailed Summary

"Words" by Charles Swain is a thought-provoking poem that explores the limitations of language. It emphasizes that the true value of communication lies in sincerity rather than the quantity or cleverness of the words used. 

The poem begins by pointing out the fleeting, often superficial nature of spoken words. The poet compares words to "summer birds" that appear for a short time and then fly away, leaving behind nothing but "empty air." This highlights how temporary speech can be when it lacks true meaning.

The core of the poem draws a sharp contrast between words that merely reach the "mind" and those that touch the "heart." Many words may sound pleasant, impressive, or clever to the mind, but if they are insincere, they fail to provide genuine comfort or emotional satisfaction. To illustrate this, the poet uses a powerful metaphor, comparing insincere, decorative speech to plants that produce beautiful flowers but bear no fruit. Just as a fruitless plant is ultimately unproductive, hollow words are useless when the heart is in genuine need of comfort. The poet concludes by describing the human heart as a "pilgrim" traveling through life, constantly searching for real connection, peace, and understanding—things that superficial words can never provide.

---

## Central Themes

### 1. Sincerity Over Quantity
The central message of the poem is that a few honest, simple, and heartfelt words are far more precious and effective than a large number of empty, showy words. Quality and emotional depth matter more than quantity.

### 2. Heart vs. Mind
The poem explores the disconnect between intellectual communication and emotional connection. Words that are cleverly constructed may impress the mind, but they are useless if they do not carry the genuine empathy required to touch and comfort the heart.

### 3. The Emptiness of Superficiality
The poem serves as a warning against superficial speech. It argues that words spoken without genuine feeling are as temporary as passing birds and as useless as weeds.

## Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

**Rhyme Scheme:** ABAB throughout (*heart/depart, care/air, earth/dearth, weeds/needs*). The consistent alternating rhyme creates a measured, hymn-like rhythm that reinforces the poem's moral, reflective tone.

**1. Simile**
*   *"words, like summer birds, depart"* — words are compared to summer birds using "like." Just as migrating birds appear for a short season and then fly away, words too are fleeting and temporary.
*   **Effect:** Emphasizes that words, however beautiful, are ultimately transient.

**2. Metaphor**
Multiple sustained metaphors enrich the poem:
*   *"The heart—a pilgrim upon earth"* — the heart is compared to a traveller on a lifelong journey, searching for genuine truth and emotional connection.
*   *"They make a gaudy show of leaves, / And bloom, and branches wide; / Yet, he who trusts their bloom, receives / No fruit to be his pride"* — insincere words are compared to a showy, fruitless tree. Beautiful on the outside, but producing nothing of real value.
*   *"sorrow's hour of dearth"* — sorrow is compared to a time of famine, where genuine comfort is scarce.

**3. Antithesis (Contrast)**
The poem is structured around contrasting ideas:
*   **Head vs. Heart:** *"Hosts of words, which reach the head, / But never touch the heart"* — intellectual speech versus emotional sincerity.
*   **Many vs. Few:** *"Hath of the fewest words to say, / But, oh! those few are dear!"* — quantity versus quality.
*   **Leaves vs. Fruit:** Beautiful appearance versus real substance.

**4. Personification**
*   *"The heart… finds them, when needed, weeds"* — the heart is given the ability to search, find, and judge.
*   *"The voice that wins its sunny way"* — the voice is personified as something that actively travels and succeeds.
*   *"words… depart"* — words are given the ability to leave, like living beings.

**5. Imagery**
*   *Visual:* *"a gaudy show of leaves, and bloom, and branches wide"* — creates a vivid picture of a showy but fruitless tree.
*   *Auditory:* *"graceful sentence said"* — one can almost hear the pleasant but hollow speech.
*   *Tactile:* *"touch the heart"* — the absence of physical contact emphasizes emotional disconnection.

**6. Exclamatory Expression**
*   *"But, oh! those few are dear!"* — the exclamation breaks the poem's measured tone to express genuine, deep emotion about the power of sincere words.

**7. Irony**
*   The poem itself is made of words, yet it argues against the power of words — this self-aware irony adds depth.
*   *"words, when summoned to the test, / Oft satisfy the least!"* — the very thing people rely on most in times of crisis often fails them.

**8. Tone**
The tone is **reflective, didactic (teaching), and gently cautionary.** The poet speaks from wisdom and experience, warning readers about the dangers of hollow speech while celebrating the rare beauty of sincere, heartfelt words.

## Textbook Questions and Answers

1. What is the comparison that the poet draws between words and ‘empty air’?
**Ans:** The poet compares insincere or hollow words to "summer birds" to emphasize their transient and unreliable nature. Just as migrating birds arrive for a brief season and then fly away when the weather changes, superficial words appear only when it is convenient or easy, failing to provide any lasting emotional support. When such words "depart," they leave behind nothing but "empty air," which symbolizes the hollow void and the lack of genuine connection that results from insincerity. This comparison serves as a warning that speech without the "weight" of truth is as temporary as a passing breeze and can never provide the stable comfort that the human heart requires in times of need.

2. According to the poet meaningful words are more precious than a lot of them. Explain.
**Ans:** Sincere words are "dear" because they provide true emotional connection. A "host of words" that only reach the head are "hollow" and useless. The heart needs the quality of truth, not the quantity of speech.

3. Do you agree that the poet presents contrasting ideas related to ‘words’ in the poem? If yes, why? If no, why not?
**Ans:** Yes, the poet contrasts the "head" (intellect) with the "heart" (emotion), and "leaves" (showy words) with "fruit" (sincere meaning). These contrasts highlight that words can either be superficial tools or profound links between people.

4. The theme of loneliness hovers over the poem. Support this statement with examples from the text.
**Ans:** The poet mentions a "lonely home" that needs a voice to cheer it and describes the heart as a "pilgrim upon earth." These phrases show that human life is inherently solitary and only sincere communication can bridge that gap.

5. How does the poet convey the superficial nature of words? What ought to be done to address this?
**Ans:** The poet conveys the superficial nature of words by comparing them to a tree that makes a "gaudy show of leaves" and "bloom" but ultimately fails to produce any "fruit." This metaphor highlights that many people focus on the outward beauty, cleverness, and volume of their speech rather than its actual substance or sincerity. To address this issue, we must undergo a shift in values, prioritizing the quality of our emotional depth over the quantity of our vocabulary. We ought to strive for "fruitful" communication—where our words are backed by genuine action and empathy—ensuring that we use language as a bridge for real connection rather than just a decorative tool for social impression.
