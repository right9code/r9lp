# How I Taught My Grandmother to Read - Class 9 Comprehensive Notes

**Author:** Sudha Murty

---

## Character Sketches

### 1. The Grandmother (Krishtakka)
*   **Determined & Resolute:** Despite her old age (62 years), she decides to learn how to read and write. She proves that there is no age bar for learning.
*   **Humble & Traditional:** She is deeply religious and respects the traditional roles. However, her humility is most evident when she touches the feet of her young granddaughter to show respect to her 'teacher'.
*   **Regretful yet Proactive:** She deeply regrets her inability to read independently when she cannot read the next episode of *Kashi Yatre*. Instead of merely crying, she takes immediate action to overcome her illiteracy.

### 2. The Granddaughter (The Narrator/Sudha Murty)
*   **Supportive & Patient:** As a 12-year-old girl, she takes on the responsibility of teaching her 62-year-old grandmother.
*   **Immature but Loving:** Initially, she finds her grandmother's desire to learn at 62 amusing and laughs at her. However, seeing her grandmother's tears and determination, she becomes a dedicated teacher.
*   **Respectful:** She understands the value of tradition and returns her grandmother's respect by gifting her a copy of *Kashi Yatre* and touching her feet.

---

## Detailed Summary

### Phase 1: The Weekly Ritual of *Karmaveera*
The story is set in a village in North Karnataka. The narrator, a young girl, lived with her grandparents. At that time, Triveni was a very popular writer in the Kannada language. One of her novels, *Kashi Yatre*, was appearing as a serial in the weekly magazine *Karmaveera*. The grandmother, Krishtakka, was illiterate, so the narrator would read the weekly episode to her. The grandmother identified deeply with the protagonist of the novel, an old lady who wished to visit Kashi but sacrificed her savings to help a young orphan girl get married.

### Phase 2: The Narrator's Absence
Once, the narrator went to a neighboring village for a wedding and stayed there for a week. During this time, the latest issue of *Karmaveera* arrived at the house. The grandmother eagerly opened it, but she could not read a single word. She felt utterly helpless and frustrated because she was entirely dependent on her granddaughter to know the story. She rubbed her hands over the pictures and text but could not understand the plot.

### Phase 3: The Grandmother's Realization and Decision
When the narrator returned, she found her grandmother crying. This shocked the young girl because Krishtakka was a strong woman who rarely cried. The grandmother confessed her sorrow about being illiterate. She explained that she lost her mother early, never went to school, and got married young. She realized that money is useless if one is not independent. She made a firm resolution to learn the Kannada alphabet, setting Saraswati Pooja (during Dasara) as her deadline to become independent.

### Phase 4: The Teaching Process
The grandmother asked her 12-year-old granddaughter to be her teacher. Initially, the narrator laughed at the idea of a 62-year-old woman wearing spectacles trying to read. However, Krishtakka was serious. She said, *"For learning, there is no age bar."* The grandmother was an ideal student. She did an amazing amount of homework, reading, repeating, writing, and reciting the alphabet tirelessly.

### Phase 5: The Touching Conclusion
On the day of Saraswati Pooja, the grandmother called the narrator to the pooja place and made her sit on a stool. She then gifted her a frock material. To the narrator's absolute shock, the 62-year-old grandmother bent down and touched the feet of her 12-year-old granddaughter. The narrator was horrified, as tradition dictates that youngsters touch the feet of elders. However, the grandmother explained that she was touching the feet of her *teacher*, not her granddaughter, to show respect for bestowing the gift of literacy upon her. The narrator then touched her grandmother's feet in return and gifted her a newly published, bound copy of the novel *Kashi Yatre*, which the grandmother read independently.

---

## Central Themes

### 1. Adult Literacy & Independence
The central theme is the importance of literacy. The grandmother realizes that relying on others for basic reading makes her dependent and helpless. Education brings true independence and self-respect.

### 2. No Age Bar for Learning
The story strongly conveys the message that age is just a number when it comes to acquiring knowledge. A strong willpower and dedication can overcome any physical or age-related barriers.

### 3. Reversal of Roles and True Respect
The story beautifully reverses traditional roles. A child becomes the teacher, and a grandmother becomes the student. The grandmother's gesture of touching the child's feet redefines 'respect'—it is given to knowledge and the one who imparts it, regardless of age.

## Textbook Questions and Answers

1. Why do you think the grandmother felt embarrassed to ask someone else to read to her while the narrator was away?
**Ans:** The grandmother was a well-off and respected lady in the village. She felt that her illiteracy was a sign of dependency that was embarrassing for someone of her age and status. Asking a stranger would have exposed this "poverty" of knowledge, which she found humiliating.

2. Why does the narrator initially laugh at her grandmother's determination to learn at the age of sixty-two?
**Ans:** The twelve-year-old narrator viewed sixty-two as an age meant for rest and retirement, not for starting the alphabet. She childishly focused on her grandmother's physical signs of aging—grey hair, wrinkled hands, and spectacles—believing it was too late for her to become a student.

3. What significance does the story of Kashi Yatre have in both the grandmother's life and the story?
**Ans:** In the grandmother's life, the novel mirrors her own religious devotion and her sense of sacrifice. In the broader story, it is the catalyst for her change; her inability to read the latest episode of her favorite novel makes her realize how helpless her illiteracy makes her, prompting her to learn.

4. What does the grandmother's desire to learn the Kannada alphabet reflect about her?
**Ans:** The grandmother's desire to learn reflects her extraordinary resilience and a deeply progressive mindset that refuses to be limited by tradition or age. While most people her age would have resigned themselves to their circumstances, she viewed her illiteracy as an obstacle that could be conquered through sheer willpower. Her willingness to humble herself before her young granddaughter and work tirelessly until the Dussehra deadline shows a person who values self-improvement above social pride. It demonstrates that she is a lifelong learner who believes that it is never too late to reclaim one's dignity through knowledge.

5. What lessons can we infer from the grandmother's action of touching the narrator's feet?
**Ans:** It teaches that knowledge and the source of that knowledge (the teacher) deserve absolute respect, regardless of age or gender. It honors the "Guru-Shishya" tradition and shows that true humility is necessary for real learning.

6. What does the following line tell us about the broader theme of the story?
'For a good cause if you are determined, you can overcome any obstacle.'
**Ans:** This line highlights the central theme of "Determination and Willpower." It suggests that when a goal is worthy (like literacy), the human spirit can transcend any physical or social barrier, including old age.

7. How effectively does the story highlight the value of education in supporting personal independence?
**Ans:** The story powerfully illustrates that education is the ultimate tool for personal emancipation. For the grandmother, her lack of literacy was not just an inability to read a book, but a loss of self-esteem and independence that made her feel "poverty-stricken" despite her wealth. By learning the alphabet at sixty-two, she broke the stereotype that age is a barrier to intellectual growth. Her final success proves that literacy provides a person with the dignity to navigate the world without being dependent on others, thus supporting the broader message that education is a lifelong necessity for individual freedom.
