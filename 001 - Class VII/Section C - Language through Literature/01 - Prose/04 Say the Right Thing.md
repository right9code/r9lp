# Say the Right Thing - Class 7 Comprehensive Notes

**Author:** G.C. Thornley
**Genre:** Play / Comedy
**(From the textbook *Poorvi*)**

---

## Detailed Summary

"Say the Right Thing" is a humorous and lighthearted play by G.C. Thornley that explores the importance of social etiquette, tactful communication, and the comical disasters that occur when one speaks without thinking.

### Detailed Main Events

*   **Preparing for the Guests:** The play opens with Mrs. Shaw frantically preparing to host two important guests: Mrs. Harding (the wife of a wealthy man who recently moved to the area) and her sister, Mrs. Lee. Mrs. Shaw is eager to make a strong, positive impression and build a friendship with the new neighbors.
*   **The Etiquette Lesson:** To ensure everything goes smoothly, Mrs. Shaw gives her daughter, Mary, strict instructions on how to behave. She tells Mary to be polite, kind, and to use specific social phrases to keep the conversation flowing. For example, she teaches Mary the polite phrase, "Must you go? Can't you stay?" to use when the guests are preparing to leave.
*   **The Comical Disasters:** Mary is confident but severely lacks tact and social awareness. When the guests arrive, she takes her mother's advice too literally and applies it at entirely the wrong times:
    *   **The Banker Blunder:** Attempting to make conversation, Mary makes uninformed and rude comments about bankers, completely unaware that Mrs. Lee's brother is actually a banker.
    *   **The Stray Dog Incident:** Trying to be helpful, Mary makes fun of a scruffy dog she assumes is a stray, only to embarrassingly discover that the dog belongs to the guests.
*   **The Final Mistake:** The climax of the comedy occurs when Mrs. Harding and Mrs. Lee finally prepare to leave. Mary tries to use the polite phrase her mother taught her, but she mixes up the words entirely. Instead of urging them to stay, she accidentally says, "Must you stay? Can't you go?"—effectively kicking the guests out and completing her string of social disasters.

---

## Central Themes

### 1. Think Before You Speak
The central lesson of the play is that words have power. Speaking without considering the context or the feelings of others can lead to embarrassment and unintentionally hurt people, as demonstrated by Mary's blunders about the banker and the dog.

### 2. True Etiquette vs. Scripted Manners
The play highlights that genuine politeness cannot simply be memorized from a script. True etiquette requires empathy, social awareness, and understanding the appropriate context. Mary's failure stems from memorizing phrases without understanding when or how to use them.

### 3. The Humor of Miscommunication
The play uses situational comedy and miscommunication to entertain the reader. Mary’s mistakes show how good intentions can lead to disastrous and hilarious social outcomes when one lacks tact.

## Textbook Questions and Answers

### II. Analytical and Reflective Questions

**1. What does Mary’s comment about Mr. Best’s routine and profession reveal about her viewpoint on bankers and their way of life?**
**Ans:** Mary's blunt comments reveal she views a banker's life as exceedingly dull, repetitive, and unimaginative. By highlighting his monotonous daily routine, she strips away the prestige of the profession, showing she values excitement over rigid structure.

**2. What can we infer about Mary’s understanding of polite conversation based on her interactions with the guests?**
**Ans:** We can infer that Mary possesses absolutely no understanding of the tact or diplomacy required for polite society. She operates purely on unfiltered, literal truth, failing to realize that polite conversation often relies on white lies and avoiding sensitive topics.

**3. What do you think the repeated stage direction ‘no one speaks’ indicates about the atmosphere in the room when Mary speaks?**
**Ans:** The repeated "no one speaks" powerfully indicates an atmosphere of profound, uncomfortable, and embarrassed silence. It shows that Mary's comments violate accepted social norms so drastically that the adult guests are left completely shocked and speechless.

**4. How does Mary’s attitude towards the guests change throughout the play?**
**Ans:** Initially, Mary's attitude towards the guests is somewhat eager and observant, as she actively tries to engage them in conversation, even though her methods are clumsy. However, as the play progresses and her attempts at conversation repeatedly result in awkward silences and offended reactions, her attitude shifts towards confusion, frustration, and eventual despair. By the end, she is entirely defeated, wishing she could simply disappear rather than face the complex, confusing demands of adult social interaction.

**5. What do Mrs. Harding’s and Mrs. Lee’s responses to Mary’s blunders during their visit reveal about their characters?**
**Ans:** Their responses reveal they are typical members of polite society who prioritize appearances and decorum over addressing uncomfortable truths. They react with shocked silence or forced smiles to avoid conflict, showing they are easily offended by breaches of etiquette.

**6. In what way does the play show the importance of knowing how to engage in polite conversation?**
**Ans:** The play humorously but clearly demonstrates the importance of polite conversation by showing the catastrophic social consequences of lacking it. Through Mary's series of blunders, it illustrates that absolute, unfiltered honesty is often cruel and disruptive in social settings, leading to hurt feelings and ruined visits. It shows that polite conversation—which includes tact, empathy, and sometimes withholding the literal truth—is the essential "glue" that allows diverse people to interact smoothly and comfortably in adult society.
