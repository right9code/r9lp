## Complete Poem Text

'Tis a lesson you should heed,
Try, try again;
If at first you don't succeed,
Try, try again;
Then your courage should appear,
For if you will persevere,
You will conquer, never fear;
Try, try again.

Once or twice, though you should fail,
If you would at last prevail,
Try, try again;
If we strive, 'tis no disgrace,
Though we do not win the race;
What should you do in the case?
Try, try again.

If you find your task is hard,
Time will bring you your reward,
Try, try again;
All that other folks can do,
Why, with patience, should not you?
Only keep this rule in view:
Try, try again.

**— Eliza Cook** *(Originally attributed to T.H. Palmer)*

---

## Detailed Summary

"Try Again" is an inspirational poem by Eliza Cook that emphasizes the vital importance of perseverance, determination, and optimism when faced with failure. It uses a historical legend to teach a timeless moral lesson.

### Stanza-wise Overview

*   **A Defeated King:** The poem narrates the story of King Bruce of Scotland. After failing repeatedly in battle to achieve a noble goal for his people and free his land, the King is in a state of deep despair and hopelessness. Feeling completely defeated, he considers giving up his quest entirely.
*   **The Tiny Teacher:** While resting and lost in his gloomy thoughts, the King notices a tiny spider attempting to climb up to its web on the ceiling. 
*   **The Spider's Struggle:** The King watches as the spider struggles immensely. It climbs a little, but slips and falls back down. It tries again, but falls again. This happens multiple times (nine failed attempts), yet the tiny creature persistently resumes its climb without hesitation.
*   **Success at Last:** After numerous failures, the spider bravely gathers its strength, tries one more time, and finally succeeds in reaching its web.
*   **A Royal Revelation:** Witnessing this incredible display of grit and patience from such a small creature deeply inspires King Bruce. He realizes that if a tiny spider can overcome such immense obstacles through sheer persistence, he, a King, must not give up on his own people. Regaining his courage, the King makes another attempt at his task and, like the spider, eventually succeeds.

---

## Central Themes

### 1. Perseverance and Persistence
The core message of the poem is captured in the famous proverb: "If at first you don't succeed, try, try again." It teaches that success is rarely achieved on the first attempt and that consistent effort is required to overcome challenges.

### 2. Failure as a Stepping Stone
The poem highlights that failure is not the end of the journey; rather, it is a normal part of the process. Every time the spider fell, it didn't quit; it just started over. Failure should be viewed as a learning experience that eventually leads to success.

### 3. Inspiration from Unexpected Places
The poem shows that valuable life lessons and great inspiration can come from observing the smallest, most ordinary things in nature, provided one is willing to look and learn.

## Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

**Rhyme Scheme:** ABAB / AABCCB pattern with variations. The consistent rhyme creates a song-like, easy-to-memorize rhythm, perfect for its didactic (teaching) purpose.

**1. Refrain / Repetition**
The most prominent device. *"Try, try again"* is repeated at the end of nearly every section:
*   Stanza 1: appears 3 times.
*   Stanzas 2 & 3: appears 2 times each.
*   **Effect:** Creates a chant-like, motivational rhythm. The repetition drills the central message into the reader's mind, making it unforgettable.

**2. Metaphor**
*   *"win the race"* — life's challenges are compared to a race. Even if you don't finish first, the act of striving (*"'tis no disgrace"*) is itself honourable.
*   *"Time will bring you your reward"* — time is compared to a generous giver, implying that patience and persistence are eventually rewarded.

**3. Alliteration**
*   ***c***ourage... ***c***onquer
*   ***t***ry... ***t***ry
*   ***t***ask... ***t***ime
*   **Effect:** Reinforces key words and adds rhythmic emphasis.

**4. Rhetorical Question**
*   *"All that other folks can do, / Why, with patience, should not you?"* — the poet asks this not to get an answer but to challenge the reader: if others can succeed, so can you.
*   *"What should you do in the case?"* — the answer is already embedded in the refrain.

**5. Personification**
*   *"your courage should appear"* — courage is given the human ability to "appear," as if it is a companion that arrives when needed.
*   *"Time will bring you your reward"* — time is personified as a deliverer of rewards.

**6. Direct Address (Second Person)**
The poem directly addresses the reader with *"you"* throughout:
*   *"'Tis a lesson you should heed," "If at first you don't succeed," "Why… should not you?"*
*   **Effect:** Creates a personal, mentor-to-student tone. The poem feels like direct advice.

**7. Tone**
The tone is **motivational, encouraging, and gently didactic.** It is optimistic throughout, never harsh or critical. The poet reassures the reader that failure is normal and persistence is the key to success.

## Textbook Questions and Answers

### II. Analytical and Reflective Questions

**1. How does the first stanza help in setting the mood of the poem?**
**Ans:** The first stanza sets a gloomy, melancholic mood by describing the king in "low despair" with a sinking heart. This highlights the heavy psychological burden of failure, contrasting sharply with his ultimate triumph.

**2. Describe how King Bruce's attitude changes from the beginning towards the end of the poem.**
**Ans:** At the beginning of the poem, King Bruce is in a state of absolute surrender and profound depression, feeling completely defeated by his repeated failures and declaring, "I'll give it all up." However, by the end of the poem, his attitude undergoes a radical transformation. After witnessing the spider's relentless perseverance, he casts off his gloom, shouts "Bravo!", and embraces the challenge with renewed vigor, believing that if an insect can conquer obstacles, a king certainly can too.

**3. The poet describes every action of the spider in great detail. What does this tell us about his attitude towards the spider?**
**Ans:** The poet's detailed description of the spider's slips, falls, and climbs reveals a deep respect for its quiet resilience. The poet elevates the tiny insect into a heroic figure and a master teacher of perseverance.

**4. How does the spider inspire us to overcome despair and not give up?**
**Ans:** The spider inspires us by demonstrating that repeated failure is simply a natural part of the process of achieving a difficult goal, rather than a reason to surrender. Despite falling "nine" times and becoming dizzy, it never stops to complain or wallow in self-pity; it simply steadies itself and climbs again. This proves that physical weakness or environmental challenges are irrelevant as long as the internal will to succeed remains unbroken, teaching us that victory belongs to those who refuse to accept defeat.

**5. The poem teaches us that failures are stepping stones to success. Explain.**
**Ans:** The poem illustrates that success is rarely instant; it usually requires surviving multiple failures. Each of the spider's falls built the necessary momentum for its final climb, just as the king learned from his previous defeats.

**6. The spider's journey tells us that anyone can be a source of inspiration in our lives. Elaborate.**
**Ans:** King Bruce, a powerful monarch, learned his greatest lesson from a tiny, seemingly insignificant insect. This shows that true wisdom is universal and that we can find profound inspiration in the humblest parts of nature if we pay attention.
