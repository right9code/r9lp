# A Funny Man - Class 7 Comprehensive Notes

**Poet:** Natalie Joan
**(From the textbook *Poorvi*)**

---

## Complete Poem Text

One day a funny kind of man
Came walking down this street.
He wore a shoe upon his head
And hats upon his feet.

He raised the shoe and smiled at me,
His manners were polite;
But never had I seen before
Such a funny sounding sight.

He said, “Allow me to present
Your Highness with a rose.”
And taking out a currant bun
He held it to my nose.

I staggered back against the wall
And then I answered, “Well!
I never saw a rose with such
A funny looking smell.”

He then began to sing a song,
And sat down on the ground;
You never heard in all your life
Such a funny feeling sound.

“My friend, why do you wear two hats
Upon your feet?” I said.
He turned the other way about,
And hopped home on his head.

**— Natalie Joan**

---

## Detailed Summary

"A Funny Man" by Natalie Joan is a highly imaginative, light-hearted, and nonsensical poem. It describes a whimsical encounter between the poet and a very peculiar man who completely ignores everyday logic and social norms through his bizarre appearance and actions.

### Stanza-wise Overview

*   **The Bizarre Encounter:** The poem begins with the poet describing an encounter with a very strange, "funny" man on the street. His appearance is entirely unconventional and absurd.
*   **Upside-Down Fashion:** The man’s sense of fashion is completely reversed. Instead of wearing a hat on his head, he wears a shoe. Conversely, instead of wearing shoes on his feet, he wears hats on his toes.
*   **Peculiar Politeness:** Despite his ridiculous attire, the man is surprisingly well-mannered and polite. When he meets the poet, he acts like a gentleman, but instead of tipping his hat in greeting, he politely raises the shoe from his head.
*   **Nonsensical Actions:** The man then playfully offers the poet a currant bun, but bizarrely pretends that the bun is a beautiful rose. 
*   **The Absurd Exit:** When the poet playfully questions his strange behavior and odd fashion choices, the man does not offer any logical explanation. Instead, he simply ignores the question and hops away down the street on his head, completing the picture of total absurdity.

---

## Central Themes

### 1. Celebrating Uniqueness and Individuality
The poem cheerfully encourages readers not to judge others for being different. The funny man is strange, but he is also polite and harmless. The poem suggests that being unconventional is simply a fun way to express one's unique personality.

### 2. The Power of Nonsense and Imagination
Through its absurd imagery (wearing shoes on heads, calling buns roses, hopping on heads), the poem celebrates the pure joy of a child-like imagination, where the strict rules of the real world do not apply.

### 3. Light-heartedness
The poem's central message is to avoid taking life too seriously. It reminds the reader to appreciate silly moments and find humor in the unexpected and the bizarre.

## Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

**Rhyme Scheme:** ABCB in each quatrain (*man/street/head/feet, me/polite/before/sight, present/rose/bun/nose, wall/well/such/smell, song/ground/life/sound, hats/said/about/head*). The predictable rhyme gives the poem a nursery-rhyme quality, perfect for its humorous, child-friendly tone.

**1. Irony (Situational)**
The central device — the man's behaviour is entirely backwards, yet he is described as perfectly polite:
*   He wears a *shoe* on his *head* and *hats* on his *feet* — complete reversal of normal.
*   He *"raised the shoe and smiled at me, / His manners were polite"* — the absurd gesture is performed with gentlemanly grace.
*   **Effect:** The contradiction between his bizarre appearance and proper manners creates gentle, delightful humour.

**2. Oxymoron / Synesthesia (Mixed Senses)**
The poet deliberately mixes sensory experiences to create paradoxical phrases:
*   *"funny sounding sight"* — blends sound ("sounding") with vision ("sight").
*   *"funny looking smell"* — blends vision ("looking") with smell.
*   *"funny feeling sound"* — blends touch ("feeling") with hearing ("sound").
*   **Effect:** Mirrors the man's own sense-defying logic. Everything about him confuses the normal order.

**3. Imagery (Visual)**
Absurd but vivid pictures dominate the poem:
*   *"He wore a shoe upon his head / And hats upon his feet"* — instantly creates a comic visual.
*   *"He turned the other way about, / And hopped home on his head"* — the reader can picture the ridiculous exit.

**4. Repetition**
The word *"funny"* is used repeatedly (in the title, and in stanzas 1, 2, 4, 5):
*   **Effect:** Reinforces the dominant mood of absurdity and playfulness. The word becomes a leitmotif (recurring theme).

**5. Hyperbole (Exaggeration)**
*   *"You never heard in all your life / Such a funny feeling sound"* — exaggerates the uniqueness of the man's song.
*   *"hopped home on his head"* — exaggerates his unconventional exit to an impossible degree.

**6. Dialogue**
The poem uses direct speech to add life and narrative energy:
*   *"‘Allow me to present Your Highness with a rose.’"* — the man's formal, courtly language contrasts with his absurd offering (a currant bun).
*   *"‘My friend, why do you wear two hats upon your feet?’"* — the speaker's reasonable question is met with an unreasonable exit.

**7. Tone**
The tone is **whimsical, playful, and cheerfully absurd.** The poet celebrates nonsense and encourages the reader to enjoy the sheer silliness of the encounter without needing a logical explanation.

## Textbook Questions and Answers

### II. Analytical and Reflective Questions

**1. Which character trait of the funny man was most appealing to you? Why?**
**Ans:** The most appealing trait is his absolute, unbothered comfort in his own eccentricities. He embraces his backwards, upside-down world with cheerful nonchalance, living life entirely according to his own bizarre rules without seeking anyone's approval.

**2. The funny man does unusual things in the poem. How does it affect the overall mood of the poem?**
**Ans:** The funny man's unusual actions create a lighthearted, whimsical, and deeply comical mood. By completely flipping everyday logic upside down, the poet constructs a nonsensical world that invites laughter and playful imagination, free from any seriousness.

**3. What alternative title would you suggest for the poem? Give reason(s) for your choice.**
**Ans:** An alternative title could be "The Upside-Down Gentleman." This perfectly captures the core theme of a man who navigates life by reversing the natural order of things, while the word "Gentleman" adds a touch of ironic formality to his ridiculous behavior.

**4. Why do you think the poet has included dialogues in the poem?**
**Ans:** The dialogues make the funny man's bizarre logic feel more interactive and alive. By giving him a voice to explain his absurd rationale, the poet transforms him from a simple caricature into an engaging character, adding to the poem's playful rhythm.

**5. What does the poet wish to convey by highlighting the unusual behaviour of the funny man?**
**Ans:** By highlighting the funny man's highly unusual behavior, the poet wishes to convey the simple joy of nonsense and the liberating power of imagination. The poem serves as a gentle reminder that life does not always have to be lived according to strict, logical rules. It subtly celebrates individuality, suggesting that it is perfectly acceptable—and often quite entertaining—to be entirely different from the norm, encouraging readers to look at the world from fresh, "upside-down" perspectives.
