# Travel - Class 7 Comprehensive Notes

**Poet:** Edna St. Vincent Millay
**(From the textbook *Poorvi*)**

## Complete Poem Text

The railroad track is miles away,
And the day is loud with voices speaking,
Yet there isn't a train goes by all day
But I hear its whistle shrieking.

All night there isn't a train goes by,
Though the night is still for sleep and dreaming,
But I see its cinders red on the sky,
And hear its engine steaming.

My heart is warm with the friends I make,
And better friends I'll not be knowing;
Yet there isn't a train I wouldn't take,
No matter where it's going.

**— Edna St. Vincent Millay**

---

## Detailed Summary

"Travel" by Edna St. Vincent Millay is a short, reflective, and deeply evocative poem that captures the speaker's intense longing for adventure, exploration, and the irresistible pull of the unknown. 

### Stanza-wise Overview

*   **The Power of Imagination (Daytime):** The poem begins by establishing that the speaker does not live close to a railway station; the railroad track is "miles away." Despite this physical distance, and despite the fact that the day is loud and busy with the voices of people talking, the speaker's mind is elsewhere. Through the sheer power of imagination, she can vividly "hear" the loud, shrieking whistle of a passing train cutting through the noise of her daily life.
*   **Persistent Longing (Nighttime):** The second stanza shifts to the nighttime. When the world is completely quiet and still, the speaker’s longing for travel does not fade; it grows stronger. She visualizes the train moving through the darkness, imagining the red cinders (glowing sparks) falling from the engine lighting up the sky. She can hear the engine "steaming" as it forcefully makes its way along the tracks.
*   **The Irresistible Call:** In the final stanza, the poet acknowledges her current, settled life. She states that her heart is warm and she deeply cherishes her close friends, indicating that she is not unhappy with her home. However, her wanderlust and desire for discovery are so overwhelmingly strong that, if given the chance, she would board any train, no matter where it is going, simply for the joy and thrill of the journey itself.

---

## Central Themes

### 1. Wanderlust and the Desire to Explore
The central theme of the poem is "wanderlust"—a strong, innate desire to travel and explore the world. The speaker’s constant thoughts about trains represent a deep longing to break away from routine and embrace new experiences.

### 2. The Symbolism of the Train
In the poem, the train acts as a powerful symbol of freedom, movement, and escape. The sounds of the train (the whistle, the engine) serve as a literal "call to adventure" that the speaker finds impossible to ignore.

### 3. Balancing Contentment and Restlessness
The poem highlights a complex human emotion: the ability to value one's current life while still yearning for something else. The speaker loves her friends and feels "warm," yet she still possesses a restless spirit that wishes to leave on any train, showing that one can be content but still adventurous.

## Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

**Rhyme Scheme:** ABAB in each quatrain (*away/day, speaking/shrieking; by/sky, dreaming/steaming; make/take, knowing/going*). The alternating rhyme creates a rhythmic, rolling momentum that mirrors the movement of a train.

**1. Auditory Imagery**
The poem is dominated by the sounds of the train:
*   *"the day is loud with voices speaking"* — the noise of everyday life.
*   *"I hear its whistle shrieking"* — the piercing, irresistible call of the train cutting through the noise.
*   *"hear its engine steaming"* — the powerful, mechanical sound of the locomotive at night.
*   **Effect:** Even though the speaker never physically sees or boards a train, the sounds fill her imagination, making the train's presence inescapable.

**2. Visual Imagery**
*   *"its cinders red on the sky"* — a vivid image of glowing sparks trailing against the dark night sky. This single image conveys the romance and power of the train.

**3. Symbolism**
*   **The Train:** Symbolizes freedom, adventure, and the endless possibilities of the unknown world.
*   **The Railroad Track:** Symbolizes the path to new experiences, always present but "miles away."
*   **Cinders red on the sky:** Symbolize the burning desire and passion for travel that glows even in darkness.

**4. Contrast / Antithesis**
The poem is structured around contrasts:
*   **Day vs. Night:** *"the day is loud"* (stanza 1) vs. *"the night is still"* (stanza 2). In both, the train dominates her thoughts.
*   **Contentment vs. Restlessness:** *"My heart is warm with the friends I make"* vs. *"there isn't a train I wouldn't take"* — she loves her life yet yearns to leave.
*   **Near vs. Far:** The track is *"miles away,"* yet its presence feels immediate and close.

**5. Parallelism (Structural Repetition)**
Stanzas 1 and 2 mirror each other:
*   *"there isn't a train goes by all day"* || *"All night there isn't a train goes by"*
*   **Effect:** Shows the constancy of her wanderlust — it doesn't fade with time of day.

**6. Paradox**
*   *"My heart is warm with the friends I make, / And better friends I'll not be knowing; / Yet there isn't a train I wouldn't take"* — She is deeply happy yet would leave on any train. This paradox captures the complex human ability to be content and restless simultaneously.

**7. Tone**
The tone is **wistful, yearning, and bittersweet.** There is no sadness — the speaker is not unhappy. Instead, there is a deep, romantic longing for adventure that coexists peacefully with her love for home and friends.

## Textbook Questions and Answers

### II. Analytical and Reflective Questions

**1. What does the phrase ‘cinders red on the sky’ tell us about the train at night?**
**Ans:** The phrase paints a fiery image of a classic steam locomotive burning coal intensely, causing bright, glowing embers to shoot out and illuminate the black sky. This highlights the train's raw mechanical power and the dramatic visual spectacle it creates in the dark.

**2. What is the significance of the repeated references to the train’s whistle?**
**Ans:** The repeated references to the train's whistle—such as hearing it shriek "night and day"—serve as a constant, haunting auditory motif that symbolizes the irresistible call of adventure. For the speaker, the whistle is not just a mechanical noise; it is a seductive, emotional trigger that pierces through the mundane reality of her current location and stirs a deep, restless longing within her soul. It acts as a continuous reminder of all the unknown places she hasn't yet seen, fueling her passionate wanderlust.

**3. What does the line ‘no matter where it’s going’ reveal about the speaker’s attitude towards travel?**
**Ans:** It reveals that the speaker's attitude is driven by an absolute love for the journey itself, rather than a desire to reach a specific destination. It highlights a deeply romantic mindset where the unknown possibilities of the open road are incredibly appealing.

**4. Do you think the poet actually sees and hears the train? Why or why not?**
**Ans:** While she certainly hears the shrieking whistle, it is highly likely she doesn't actually see the train physically. Her vivid descriptions read more like the romantic, idealized daydreams of someone sitting indoors, visualizing the train intensely in her imagination as a symbol of escape.
