# Diamante Poem Writing

## 📌 What is a Diamante Poem?
A **Diamante poem** (pronounced dee-uh-mahn-tay) is a seven-line poem that is shaped like a diamond. It does not need to rhyme. Instead, it follows a strict grammatical structure using specific parts of speech (nouns, adjectives, verbs). It usually describes a transition from one topic at the top to an opposite (or related) topic at the bottom.

## 📝 Format / Structure

The poem must have exactly **7 lines**, forming a diamond shape:

- **Line 1**: 1 Noun (The starting subject)
- **Line 2**: 2 Adjectives (Describing the subject in Line 1)
- **Line 3**: 3 "ing" Verbs (Action words related to Line 1)
- **Line 4**: 4 Nouns (2 relating to Line 1, 2 relating to Line 7. This is the transition line!)
- **Line 5**: 3 "ing" Verbs (Action words related to the new subject in Line 7)
- **Line 6**: 2 Adjectives (Describing the new subject in Line 7)
- **Line 7**: 1 Noun (The ending subject, usually an antonym or synonym of Line 1)

## ⚖️ Types of Diamante Poems
1. **Synonym Diamante**: The first noun and last noun mean the same thing (e.g., Monster -> Creature).
2. **Antonym Diamante**: The first noun and last noun are opposites (e.g., Day -> Night, Winter -> Summer).

## 💡 How to Write a Diamante Poem (Step-by-Step)
- **Step 1**: Choose your two nouns (Start and End). Make sure they are strong opposites or clear synonyms.
- **Step 2**: Brainstorm adjectives (describing words) for both nouns.
- **Step 3**: Brainstorm verbs ending in "-ing" for both nouns.
- **Step 4**: Pick the best words and arrange them in the 7-line diamond structure.

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Using the wrong parts of speech (e.g., using a verb in the adjective line).
- Forgetting the "-ing" on the verbs in Lines 3 and 5.
- Losing the diamond shape. (Center your text so it visually looks like a diamond).
