# Conversation (Dialogue) Writing

## 📌 What is Conversation Writing?
**Conversation or Dialogue writing** is a written exchange of spoken words between two or more characters. The goal is to make the conversation sound natural, revealing the characters' personalities and the context of the situation without writing long paragraphs of explanation.

## 📝 Format / Structure 

1. **Setting the Scene (Optional but helpful)**: A brief line in brackets setting up where the characters are (e.g., *[Rahul and Amit meet at the bus stop]*).
2. **Speaker Tags**: The name of the speaker followed by a colon ( : ).
3. **The Dialogue**: The exact words spoken by the character. No quotation marks are needed in this format.
4. **Stage Directions (Optional)**: Actions or emotions written in brackets to add flavor (e.g., *(smiling)* or *(angrily)*).

**Example format:**
**Rahul**: Hey Amit! Where are you going?
**Amit**: *(panting)* I am rushing to the library.

## ⚖️ CBSE Marking Scheme (Typically 4-5 Marks)
- **Format / Structure**: 1 Mark
- **Content (Logical flow, relevance to topic)**: 2 Marks
- **Expression (Natural language, punctuation, grammar)**: 1-2 Marks

## 💡 Language Tips & Key Rules
- **Keep it Natural**: People use contractions in real speech (e.g., use "I'm", "don't", "can't" instead of "I am", "do not").
- **Avoid Long Monologues**: A conversation is a back-and-forth exchange. Don't let one character speak 5 sentences in a row while the other just says "Yes."
- **Stay on Topic**: Ensure the conversation addresses the prompt given in the question.
- **Use Punctuation**: Question marks, exclamation points, and commas are crucial to convey the right tone of voice.

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Writing dialogue like a formal essay. Speech should be casual and situational.
- Forgetting the colon (:) after the speaker's name.
- Using direct/indirect speech rules (e.g., writing *Rahul asked Amit where he was going* instead of actual dialogue).

## 🌟 Model Example

**Q: Write a conversation between a doctor and a patient who is suffering from a severe stomach ache.**

*(In the doctor's clinic)*

**Patient**: Good morning, doctor.  
**Doctor**: Good morning. Please have a seat. What seems to be the problem today?  
**Patient**: *(holding his stomach)* I have been having a severe stomach ache since last night.  
**Doctor**: I see. Where exactly does it hurt? Is it a sharp pain?  
**Patient**: Yes, it's very sharp, mostly on the lower right side. I also feel a little nauseous.  
**Doctor**: Did you eat anything unusual outside yesterday?  
**Patient**: Well, I did have some street food near the station on my way home from work.  
**Doctor**: Ah, that might be the culprit. Let me examine you on the bed. Please lie down.  
*(After examination)*  
**Doctor**: It looks like a case of food poisoning. I am prescribing some antibiotics and painkillers. You must drink plenty of fluids and eat only bland food for two days.  
**Patient**: Thank you, doctor. I will make sure to take the medicines on time.  
**Doctor**: You're welcome. Come back if the pain doesn't subside by tomorrow.
