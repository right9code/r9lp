# Determiners

**Determiners** are words placed before a noun to clarify what the noun refers to. They "determine" or limit the meaning of the noun. A determiner tells us whether the noun is specific or general, singular or plural, and indicates ownership or quantity.

*Note: A determiner always comes before a noun. If there is an adjective, the determiner comes before the adjective (e.g., **The** red car).*

---

## Types of Determiners

Determiners are broadly classified into the following categories:

### 1. Articles (A, An, The)
Articles define whether the noun is specific or unspecific.

- **Definite Article (The):** Used before a specific noun (singular or plural) that is already known to the speaker and the listener.
  - *I saw **the** boy who stole your bag.* (Specific boy)
  - ***The** Sun is a star.* (Unique object)
- **Indefinite Articles (A, An):** Used before general, non-specific, singular countable nouns.
  - **A** is used before consonant sounds: ***a** car, **a** university* (starts with 'yoo' sound).
  - **An** is used before vowel sounds: ***an** apple, **an** hour* (h is silent, starts with 'o' sound).

⚠️ **EXCEPTION:** Do not use "a" or "an" with uncountable nouns (like water, air, advice) or plural nouns. We also say *a one-eyed man* because "one" starts with a consonant "w" sound.

### 2. Demonstratives (This, That, These, Those)
Demonstratives indicate the physical proximity (distance) of the noun from the speaker.

- **This:** Singular noun, close to the speaker. (*I like **this** book.*)
- **That:** Singular noun, far from the speaker. (*Look at **that** bird.*)
- **These:** Plural noun, close to the speaker. (***These** flowers are beautiful.*)
- **Those:** Plural noun, far from the speaker. (***Those** boys are playing football.*)

### 3. Possessives (My, Our, Your, His, Her, Its, Their)
Possessives show ownership or belonging.

- ***My** father is a doctor.*
- *She forgot **her** keys at home.*
- *The dog wagged **its** tail.*

⚠️ **EXCEPTION:** Do not confuse the determiner **its** (belonging to it) with **it's** (it is). *Its collar is tight* vs *It's a nice day*.

### 4. Quantifiers (Some, Any, Much, Many, Few, Little, All, Both, etc.)
Quantifiers indicate the quantity or amount of the noun.

- **Some & Any:**
  - **Some** is used in affirmative sentences and polite requests. (*I want **some** milk. Could you lend me **some** money?*)
  - **Any** is used in negative and interrogative sentences. (*I don't have **any** pens. Do you have **any** questions?*)
- **Much & Many:**
  - **Much** is used with uncountable nouns. (*There isn't **much** water left.*)
  - **Many** is used with plural countable nouns. (*There are **many** students in the class.*)
- **Little vs. A little (Uncountable Nouns):**
  - **Little:** Means almost none (negative meaning). (*He has **little** knowledge of the subject.*)
  - **A little:** Means some, a small amount (positive meaning). (*I need **a little** sugar for the tea.*)
- **Few vs. A few (Countable Nouns):**
  - **Few:** Means almost none (negative meaning). (*He has **few** friends, so he is lonely.*)
  - **A few:** Means some, a small number (positive meaning). (*I have **a few** books on history.*)
- **All & Both:**
  - **All** refers to the whole number or amount. (***All** the students passed the exam.*)
  - **Both** refers to two people or things. (***Both** my parents are teachers.*)

### 5. Distributives (Each, Every, Either, Neither)
Distributives refer to individual members of a group.

- **Each:** Used for two or more persons/things, considered individually. (***Each** boy was given a pen.*)
- **Every:** Used for more than two persons/things, referring to the whole group without exception. (***Every** citizen should vote.*)
- **Either:** Means any of the two. (*You can take **either** bus; they both go to the station.*)
- **Neither:** Means not the one nor the other of the two. (***Neither** answer is correct.*)

### 6. Interrogatives (What, Which, Whose)
Used to ask questions about the noun.

- ***What** subject do you like most?*
- ***Which** book are you reading?*
- ***Whose** pen is this?*

---

## Important Rules for Exams

1. **Do not use double determiners:** You cannot use an article and a possessive together.
   - *Incorrect:* The my book is lost.
   - *Correct:* My book is lost.
2. **Less vs. Fewer:** 
   - *Less* is used with uncountable nouns (e.g., *I have **less** money than him.*)
   - *Fewer* is used with countable nouns (e.g., *There are **fewer** cars on the road today.*)
3. **Enough:** Comes before a noun.
   - *We have **enough** food for the trip.*

# PRACTICE QUESTIONS

## I. Fill in the blanks with suitable determiners.

**A.**
1. He gave me ………… (an/a) one-rupee note.
2. Is there ………… (some/any) news about the results?
3. ………… (Every/Each) of the two boys gets a prize.
4. I have read ………… (many/much) books on this topic.
5. There is ………… (little/a little) hope of his recovery; he is very sick.
6. She bought ………… (these/this) apples from the market.
7. I need ………… (some/any) money to buy a ticket.
8. ………… (Whose/Which) car is parked outside our house?
9. I can write with ………… (either/neither) hand.
10. Do you have ………… (much/many) work to do today?

**B.**
1. Only ………… (a few/few) people attended the meeting because of the storm.
2. We have ………… (less/fewer) students in our class than last year.
3. ………… (All/Both) my brothers are engineers.
4. Let's listen to ………… (the/a) music being played on the radio.
5. Are there ………… (some/any) messages for me?

## II. Identify and correct the error in the use of determiners in each sentence.

**A.**
1. I don't have some money.
2. He is a honest man.
3. This is the my bag.
4. There is many sugar in the tea.
5. She gave me an useful advice.

---

## Answers

**I. A.**
1. a
2. any
3. Each
4. many
5. little
6. these
7. some
8. Whose
9. either
10. much

**I. B.**
1. a few
2. fewer
3. Both
4. the
5. any

**II. A.**
1. I don't have **any** money.
2. He is **an** honest man.
3. This is **my** bag. (Or: This bag is mine.)
4. There is **much** sugar in the tea.
5. She gave me **some** useful advice. (Or: She gave me **a piece of** useful advice. 'Advice' is uncountable).
