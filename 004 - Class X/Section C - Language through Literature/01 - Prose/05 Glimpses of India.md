# Glimpses of India - Class 10 Comprehensive Notes

**Authors:** Lucio Rodrigues (Part I), Lokesh Abrol (Part II), Arup Kumar Datta (Part III)
**(From the textbook *First Flight*)**

## Detailed Summary

### Part I: A Baker from Goa
The story is a nostalgic trip to the Portuguese days in Goa, focusing on the traditional baker or *pader*. The author recalls his childhood when the baker used to visit twice a day, making a musical entry with his bamboo staff. While the elders were fond of the bread loaves, the children loved the bread-bangles. Baking was a profitable profession, and the baker was considered a friend and guide. Bread was an essential part of Goan culture, mandatory for marriages (sweet bread called *bol*), engagements (sandwiches), and Christmas (cakes and *bolinhas*). The age-old furnaces still exist, keeping the tradition alive.

### Part II: Coorg
Coorg (Kodagu) is the smallest district of Karnataka, known as a "piece of heaven" located between Mysore and Mangalore. It is famous for its evergreen rainforests, spices, and coffee plantations. The Kodavus (Coorgis) are a proud, martial race believed to be of Greek or Arabic descent. They wear a traditional coat called *Kuppia*, similar to the Arab *Kuffia*. Coorgis are known for their hospitality and tales of valor. The river Kaveri flows through the region, offering adventure sports like river rafting. The area also hosts the Brahmagiri hills and a large Tibetan settlement at Bylakuppe.

### Part III: Tea from Assam
Rajvir and Pranjol are traveling to Assam, Pranjol's hometown. During the train journey, Rajvir shares facts about tea, noting that over 80 crore cups are consumed daily. As the landscape changes to a "sea of tea bushes," Rajvir recounts legends about the origin of tea: a Chinese legend where tea leaves accidentally fell into boiling water, and an Indian legend involving a Buddhist ascetic, Bodhidharma, whose severed eyelids grew into tea plants. They arrive at Dhekiabari Tea Estate, managed by Pranjol's father, during the second-flush season (May to July), which yields the best tea.

## Character Analysis

### Part I: A Baker from Goa
*   **The Pader (The Baker)**: A living link to the Portuguese era. He traditionally wore the *kabai* (a single-piece long frock) and later a shirt and trousers shorter than full length. His plump, "jackfruit-like" physique indicated his prosperity, as baking was a lucrative profession. He was a friendly figure to the children.

### Part II: Coorg
*   **The Kodavus**: A proud race of martial men and beautiful women. They are the only people in India permitted to carry firearms without a license. General Cariappa, the first Chief of the Indian Army, was a Coorgi.

### Part III: Tea from Assam
*   **Rajvir**: A student from Delhi who is enthusiastic and well-prepared. He represents the curious traveler who does his research before visiting a place, impressing even the locals with his knowledge of tea legends and seasons.
*   **Pranjol**: A local Assamese boy. Since he grew up surrounded by tea gardens, he is less fascinated by the scenery than Rajvir, representing the familiarity of the locals.

## Central Themes & Literary Devices

### Central Themes
1.  **Cultural Heritage**: All three stories celebrate the unique cultural heritage of different regions of India—the Portuguese influence in Goa, the martial and Greek/Arabic ancestry of Coorg, and the colonial/ancient history of tea in Assam.
2.  **Nature and Landscape**: Coorg and Assam heavily feature the beauty of the Indian landscape, from misty hills and rainforests to endless green tea gardens.
3.  **Tradition vs. Modernity**: The survival of the paders in Goa despite the Portuguese leaving highlights how traditions persist through time.

### Literary Devices
*   **Onomatopoeia**: "Thud and jingle" (Goa).
*   **Simile**: "Appearance like a jackfruit" (Goa).
*   **Metaphor**: "Sea of tea bushes" (Assam); "Piece of heaven" (Coorg).
*   **Personification**: "The river Kaveri obtains its water..." (Giving the river an active role).

## Exam-Style Value Points (6-Mark Questions)

1.  **The "Unextinguished Fire" (Goa)**: The furnaces still exist, proving that essential traditions survive political changes. Bread in Goa is not just food; it is a social requirement for religious and familial ceremonies.
2.  **Cultural Hybridity (Coorg)**: The *Kuppia/Kuffia* connection is a crucial factual point highlighting the diverse ancestry of the Coorgi people.
3.  **Knowledge as Preparation (Assam)**: Rajvir’s character highlights that "doing one's homework" enhances the experience of travel and shows respect for the local culture.
4.  **Second-Flush Significance (Assam)**: The May-July window is the most profitable and high-quality period for tea production.

## Question & Answers (NCERT Solutions)

### Part I: A Baker from Goa

**1. What are the elders in Goa nostalgic about?**
**Ans:** In Goa, the elders are nostalgic about the good old Portuguese days and their famous loaves of bread.

**2. Is bread-making still popular in Goa? How do you know?**
**Ans:** Yes, bread-making is still popular in Goa. The makers of bread, the mixers, the moulders, and the age-old, time-tested furnaces still exist. The thud and jingle of the traditional baker's bamboo can still be heard.

**3. What is the baker called?**
**Ans:** The baker is popularly known as a 'pader' in Goa.

**4. When would the baker come every day? Why did the children run to meet him?**
**Ans:** The baker would come twice a day—once in the morning when he set out to sell his bread, and later when he returned after emptying his basket. The children ran to meet him not for the loaves, but to choose their favorite bread-bangles or sweet bread.

**5. Match the following.**
(i) As marriage gifts? – sweet bread called bol
(ii) For a party or a feast? – bread
(iii) For a daughter’s engagement? – sandwiches
(iv) For Christmas? – cakes and bolinhas

**6. What did the bakers wear? (i) in the Portuguese days? (ii) when the author was young?**
**Ans:** 
(i) In the Portuguese days, the bakers wore a peculiar dress known as the *kabai*. It was a single-piece long frock reaching down to the knees.
(ii) When the author was young, he saw bakers wearing a shirt and trousers that were shorter than full-length ones and longer than half pants.

**7. Who invites the comment “he is dressed like a pader”? Why?**
**Ans:** Anyone who wears a half-pant that reaches just below the knees invites the comment that "he is dressed like a pader." This is because the traditional bakers (paders) of Goa used to wear such distinctive trousers.

**8. Where were the monthly accounts of the baker recorded?**
**Ans:** The monthly accounts of the baker were recorded on some wall in pencil.

**9. What does a ‘jackfruit-like appearance’ mean?**
**Ans:** A 'jackfruit-like appearance' means having a plump, healthy, and well-fed physique, similar to the shape of a jackfruit. It implies that the person is prosperous and never starves, much like the bakers who ran a profitable business.

### Part II: Coorg

**1. Where is Coorg?**
**Ans:** Coorg, or Kodagu, is the smallest district of Karnataka. It is situated midway between Mysore and the coastal town of Mangalore.

**2. What is the story about the Kodavu people’s descent?**
**Ans:** There are two stories about the Kodavu people's descent. One story suggests they are of Greek origin, stating that a part of Alexander's army moved south and settled there when return became impractical. They married amongst the locals. The other theory suggests Arabic descent, supported by the traditional long, black coat with an embroidered waist-belt worn by the Kodavus, known as *kuppia*, which resembles the *kuffia* worn by the Arabs and the Kurds.

**3. What are some of the things you now know about:**
**(i) The people of Coorg?**
**Ans:** They are a proud martial race, known for their fierce independence, hospitality, and tales of bravery. They are the only people in India allowed to carry firearms without a license.
**(ii) The main crop of Coorg?**
**Ans:** Coffee is the main crop of Coorg. The air breathes of invigorating coffee, and coffee estates and colonial bungalows stand tucked under tree canopies.
**(iii) The sports it offers to tourists?**
**Ans:** It offers high-energy adventure sports like river rafting, canoeing, rappelling, rock climbing, and mountain biking.
**(iv) The animals you are likely to see in Coorg?**
**Ans:** One is likely to see Macaques, Malabar squirrels, langurs, slender lorises, wild elephants, kingfishers, and the Mahaseer fish in Coorg.
**(v) Its distance from Bangalore, and how to get there?**
**Ans:** Coorg is around 250-260 kilometers from Bangalore. One can get there by road or by train (via Mysore, Mangalore, or Hassan) and then by bus or taxi.

### Part III: Tea from Assam

**1. Why was Rajvir excited to see the tea garden?**
**Ans:** Rajvir was a city boy from Delhi who had never seen a tea garden before. He was excited to see the vast, seemingly endless expanse of green tea bushes that looked like a "sea" against the backdrop of wooded hills.

**2. Why was Pranjol not as excited as Rajvir?**
**Ans:** Pranjol was born and brought up on a tea plantation in Assam. Because he had been surrounded by tea gardens his entire life, the sight was ordinary to him, and he did not share Rajvir's extraordinary excitement.

**3. What are the legends related to the discovery of tea?**
**Ans:** There are two legends. 
**The Chinese Legend:** A Chinese emperor always boiled water before drinking it. One day, a few leaves from the twigs burning under the pot fell into the water, giving it a delicious flavor. It is said they were tea leaves.
**The Indian Legend:** Bodhidharma, an ancient Buddhist ascetic, cut off his eyelids because he felt sleepy during meditations. Ten tea plants grew out of his eyelids. The leaves of these plants, when put in hot water and drunk, banished sleep.

**4. What information did Rajvir share about the global consumption of tea?**
**Ans:** Rajvir shared that tea is a hugely popular beverage globally, and over eighty crore (800 million) cups of tea are consumed every single day throughout the world. He also shared that tea came to Europe only in the sixteenth century and was initially drunk more as medicine than as a beverage.inking water made from tea leaves helped them stay awake and fresh.
