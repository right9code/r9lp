# The Proposal - Class 10 Comprehensive Notes

**Author:** Anton Chekhov
**(From the textbook *First Flight*)**

---

## Detailed Summary

This one-act play is a farce that satirizes the landed gentry of 19th-century Russia. It highlights how economic interests and petty ego often override romance and emotional logic, exposing the absurd contradictions in the characters' behavior and the repetitive nature of their conflicts.

**Phase I: The Formal Arrival (The Exposition)**
At Chubukov’s country house, Ivan Vassilevitch Lomov enters in formal evening dress. After some rambling and nervous stalling, Lomov reveals to Chubukov that he has come to ask for his daughter Natalya’s hand in marriage. Chubukov is delighted, kisses Lomov, and calls for Natalya, though he doesn't tell her the real reason for Lomov's visit.

**Phase II: The First Argument (The Inciting Incident)**
Lomov tries to lead up to the proposal by mentioning the long-standing friendly relations between their families. However, he casually mentions "my Oxen Meadows," which touch the Chubukovs' Birchwoods. Natalya interrupts, claiming the Meadows belong to her family. The proposal is entirely forgotten as they argue fiercely over ownership, legal deeds, and their grandmothers' histories.

**Phase III: The Hysteria (Rising Action)**
After a heated exchange involving Chubukov, where they trade insults (calling each other "pettifoggers" and "swindlers"), Lomov leaves in a fit of palpitations. Chubukov then casually mentions that the "fool" came to propose. Natalya is shocked. She screams, "Bring him back! I'm dying!" Her territorial pride instantly shifts to a desperate need to get married.

**Phase IV: The Second Argument (The Climax)**
Lomov returns, exhausted. Natalya tries to be civil and concedes the Meadows. However, the conversation shifts to their dogs—Lomov’s *Guess* and Natalya’s *Squeezer*. They argue over which dog is superior in speed, jaw structure, and pedigree. Chubukov joins in again, and Lomov eventually collapses from a "nervous breakdown" or fainting spell.

**Phase V: The "Proposal" (Falling Action)**
Chubukov and Natalya think Lomov is dead. They panic, realizing the marriage opportunity is slipping away. Lomov regains consciousness. Chubukov hurriedly puts Lomov’s hand into Natalya’s and shouts, "He consents!" and "Hurry up and get married!"

**Phase VI: The Eternal Conflict (Resolution)**
They are officially engaged, but within seconds, they resume their argument about the dogs. Chubukov calls for "Champagne!" to drown out the noise, realizing that this marriage will be a lifetime of shouting and bickering.

## Character Analysis

### A. Stepan Stepanovitch Chubukov (The Father)
*   **Hypocritical & Opportunistic**: He is initially suspicious of Lomov (thinking he’s come to borrow money) but becomes "extremely glad" once he realizes a proposal is coming, as it secures his daughter's future.
*   **Hot-Headed**: He quickly joins the arguments between Lomov and Natalya, escalating the tension rather than acting as a mediator.
*   **Endearingly Fake**: Frequently uses terms of endearment like "my darling," "my beauty," and "angel," even when he is about to hurl insults.

### B. Ivan Vassilevitch Lomov (The Suitor)
*   **Hypochondriac**: A 35-year-old bachelor who is "full of palpitations," "excitable," and constantly worried about his sleep and "twitches."
*   **Formal & Pedantic**: He approaches the proposal like a legal transaction, dressed in a dress-jacket and white gloves, obsessed with proving the legality of his property.
*   **Socially Awkward**: He lacks the social grace to steer a conversation toward romance, instead falling into traps of pride and territorial disputes.

### C. Natalya Stepanovna (The Daughter)
*   **Efficient & Domineering**: She is an excellent housekeeper but possesses a "shrewish" temperament. 
*   **Contradictory**: She fights Lomov tooth and nail over the "Oxen Meadows," but as soon as she hears he came to propose, she falls into hysterics and demands he be brought back.
*   **Stubborn**: Like Lomov, her pride in her family’s "possessions" (land and dogs) far outweighs her desire for a peaceful marriage.

## Central Themes

### 1. Marriage as an Economic Contract
Chekhov portrays marriage not as a union of souls, but as a merger of properties. The characters are more interested in land, wealth, and dogs than in each other's personalities.

### 2. Petty Pride and Ego
The characters are willing to sabotage their own happiness to prove they are "right" about trivial matters. Their egos prevent them from communicating effectively.

### 3. Hypocrisy of the Elite
The thin veneer of "civilized" behavior (evening dress, formal language) quickly vanishes, revealing a crude, aggressive, and petty nature beneath.

## Exam-Style Value Points (6-Mark Questions)

1.  **Satire on the Landed Gentry**: The play mocks the upper classes of 19th-century Russia, showing them as obsessed with property and lineage over human connection.
2.  **The Significance of Oxen Meadows & The Dogs**: These act as physical symbols of the characters' absurd pride and materialism.
3.  **Lomov's Physical Ailments**: His palpitations and fainting reflect his internal emotional instability and serve as a physical manifestation of his anxiety over the "business" of marriage.
4.  **Natalya’s Dual Nature**: Her rapid shift from a fierce, arguing landowner to a weeping, desperate bride highlights the societal pressure on women to marry.
5.  **Farce and Irony**: The ending is highly ironic; they achieve the "happy ending" of engagement, but it guarantees a future of endless quarreling.

## Question & Answers (NCERT Solutions)

### I. Thinking About the Play

**1. What does Chubukov at first suspect that Lomov has come for? Is he sincere when he later says. “And I’ve always loved you, my angel, as if you were my own son”? Find reasons for your answer from the play.**
**Ans:** Chubukov at first suspects that Lomov has come to borrow money from him. He is not sincere when he later says that he has always loved him like his own son. He treats Lomov rudely, and even harshly over trivial matters like the ownership of the worthless Oxen Meadows or the superiority of their dogs. He abuses him, curses him, and calls him a double-faced intriguer, villain, scarecrow, blind hen, and turnip ghost. If he truly meant what he said about loving him like a son, he would not have initially decided he wouldn't give Lomov money, nor would he have insulted him so viciously.

**2. Chubukov says of Natalya: “...as if she won’t consent! She’s in love; egad, she’s like a lovesick cat ....” Would you agree? Find reasons for your answer.**
**Ans:** I agree that she acts like she is desperate to marry, but it is debatable if it is true "love." The play supports her desperation: she is happy to see Lomov, and when she learns he came to propose, she hysterically demands her father bring him back. When Lomov faints, she repeats, “A doctor! A doctor! He’s dead,” showing concern. However, another perspective is that Chubukov knew Lomov was a good marriage prospect and simply claimed she was in love to seal the deal. Natalya does not seem genuinely "in love" with Lomov at any point; she is more attached to her land, meadows, and dogs. The way they constantly argue over trivialities suggests they are driven by societal pressure and economics, not romantic love.

**3. (a) Find all the words and expressions in the play that the characters use to speak about each other, and the accusations and insults they hurl at each other.**
**Ans:**
- **Lomov describing Natalya**: an excellent housekeeper, not bad-looking, well-educated.
- **Natalya insulting Lomov**: Impudent, a rascal, Monster ("first takes our land then has the impudence to abuse us"), questioning his ability as a hunter ("You ought to go and lie on the kitchen oven and catch black beetles..."), claiming he argues the most but knows the least.
- **Lomov insulting Chubukov**: not a neighbour but a grabber, old rat, intriguer.
- **Chubukov insulting Lomov**: pettifogger, villain, scarecrow, blind hen, turnip ghost, stuffed sausage, malicious, double-faced intriguer, fool, sick, "you’re under the slipper of your housekeeper."

**(b) Then think of five adjectives or adjectival expressions of your own to describe each character in the play.**
**Ans:**
- **Lomov**: Weak-hearted, nervous, pedantic, argumentative, socially awkward.
- **Natalya**: Domineering, materialistic, sharp-tongued, desperate, touchy.
- **Chubukov**: Opportunistic, hot-tempered, hypocritical, loud, status-conscious.

**(c) Can you now imagine what these characters will quarrel about next?**
**Ans:** Given their history, it is easy to imagine that these characters will quarrel next about the quality or brand of the champagne Chubukov has just called for, or perhaps about where they will live after the wedding.
