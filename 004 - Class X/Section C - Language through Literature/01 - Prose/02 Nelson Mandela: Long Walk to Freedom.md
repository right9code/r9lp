**Author:** Nelson Rolihlahla Mandela

## Detailed Summary

This chapter is an extract from the autobiography of Nelson Mandela, the first Black President of South Africa. It focuses on the historic occasion of the inauguration ceremony and Mandela's reflections on freedom and oppression.

**Phase 1: The Exposition (May 10, 1994)**
The setting is a "bright and clear" morning at the Union Buildings amphitheatre in Pretoria. Leaders and dignitaries from over 140 countries gathered to witness the birth of a democratic South Africa, ending its "international pariah" status under the brutal apartheid regime.

**Phase 2: The Swearing-In (The Inciting Incident)**
The order of ceremony saw Mr. de Klerk sworn in as 2nd Deputy President, then Thabo Mbeki as 1st Deputy President, and finally Nelson Mandela as President. Mandela pledges to obey and uphold the Constitution and devote himself to the well-being of the Republic and its people.

**Phase 3: The Rising Action (The Spectacle & History)**
A spectacular military display follows, with South African jets leaving a smoke trail of the new national flag: Black, Red, Green, Blue, and Gold. Mandela reflects on the past, recalling the Anglo-Boer War (early 1900s) which led to the creation of Apartheid—one of the harshest systems of racial domination ever known.

**Phase 4: The Climax (The Definition of Freedom)**
Mandela reflects on how his "boyhood freedom" (running in fields, roasting mealies) was merely an illusion. His desire for personal freedom evolved into a "greater hunger" for the freedom of his people to live with dignity and self-respect.

**Phase 5: Falling Action (The Twin Obligations)**
Mandela explains the social conflict that every man has "twin obligations"—one to his family and one to his community/country. In South Africa, a man of color who attempted to fulfill his duty to his people was inevitably "ripped from his family" and forced to live a life of secrecy and rebellion.

**Phase 6: The Final Resolution (The Moral Lesson)**
Mandela concludes that the oppressor is a "prisoner of hatred," locked behind bars of prejudice. To be truly free, one must live in a way that enhances the freedom of others, liberating both the oppressor and the oppressed.

## Character Analysis

### A. Nelson Mandela
*   **Resilient & Courageous**: He defines courage not as the absence of fear, but the "triumph over it." He believes a brave man is one who conquers fear.
*   **Statesman & Visionary**: He dreams of a society where "never, never, and never again" will the land experience the oppression of one by another.
*   **Empathetic Philosopher**: He recognizes that the "oppressor must be liberated just as surely as the oppressed," as both are robbed of their humanity.
*   **Selfless Patriot**: He transitioned from a "family-loving husband" to a "man without a home" to serve the greater cause of his people's freedom.

### B. The Freedom Fighters (The "Comrades")
*   **Men of Extraordinary Courage**: Mandela cites individuals like Oliver Tambo, Walter Sisulu, Chief Luthuli, Yusuf Dadoo, Bram Fischer, and Robert Sobukwe. 
*   **The "True Wealth"**: He describes these men as the nation's greatest assets—"finer and truer than the purest diamonds."
*   **Products of Oppression**: Mandela notes that it was the "brutality" of the Apartheid system that unintentionally produced such "heights of character."

### C. Zenani
*   **Symbol of Continuity**: Mandela’s daughter who stood by him on the podium. Her presence represents the healing of familial bonds that were severed by his long years of imprisonment.

## Central Themes

### 1. The Indivisibility of Freedom
Mandela asserts that "the chains on any one of my people were the chains on all of them." Freedom is a collective state, not a personal luxury.

### 2. The Triumph of Human Spirit
The theme explores how "depths of oppression" create "heights of character." Hardship is seen as a forge for greatness.

### 3. Reconciliation & Humanity
The inclusion of two national anthems ('Nkosi Sikelel’ iAfrika' and 'Die Stem') symbolizes the merging of different races into one unified nation, emphasizing that love comes more naturally to the human heart than hate.

## Question & Answers (NCERT Solutions)

### I. Oral Comprehension Check

**1. Where did the ceremonies take place? Can you name any public buildings in India that are made of sandstone?**
**Ans:** The ceremonies took place in the lovely sandstone amphitheatre formed by the Union Buildings in Pretoria. In India, the Parliament House, the Rashtrapati Bhavan, and the Red Fort are made of sandstone.

**2. Can you say how 10th May is an 'autumn day' in South Africa?**
**Ans:** Geographically, South Africa is in the southern hemisphere, so the seasons are reversed compared to the northern hemisphere, making May an autumn month. Politically, the day symbolizes the shedding of the "leaves" of slavery and oppression that lasted for over 300 years.

**3. At the beginning of his speech, Mandela mentions "an extraordinary human disaster". What does he mean by this? What is the "glorious... human achievement" he speaks of at the end?**
**Ans:** By 'an extraordinary human disaster', he means the brutal practice of apartheid and the oppression of black people by white people that lasted too long. The 'glorious human achievement' is the liberation of black people from bondage, poverty, deprivation, and discrimination, resulting in the establishment of a democratic, non-racial government.

**4. What does Mandela thank the international leaders for?**
**Ans:** Mandela thanks the international leaders for having come to witness and take possession with the people of South Africa of what is a common victory for justice, for peace, and for human dignity.

**5. What ideals does he set out for the future of South Africa?**
**Ans:** He sets out the ideal that South Africa will never again experience the oppression of one by another. The people will be liberated from poverty, deprivation, suffering, and all forms of discrimination.

**6. What did the military generals do? How did their attitude change, and why?**
**Ans:** The military generals saluted Mr. Mandela and pledged their loyalty to democracy and the newly elected government. Previously, under the apartheid regime, they had arrested him. Their attitude changed because the system changed, and Mr. Mandela was now the democratically elected President of a new South Africa.

**7. Why were two national anthems sung?**
**Ans:** Two national anthems were sung to symbolize equality and the coming together of two previously divided communities. The white people sang 'Nkosi Sikelel’ iAfrika' and the black people sang 'Die Stem', showing mutual respect and the birth of a unified, non-racial nation.

**8. How does Mandela describe the system of government in his country (i) in the first decade, and (ii) in the final decade of the twentieth century?**
**Ans:** 
(i) In the first decade of the 20th century, the white-skinned people patched up their differences and erected a harsh, inhumane system of racial domination (apartheid) against the dark-skinned peoples of their own land.
(ii) In the final decade, that previous system had been overturned forever and replaced by one that recognized the rights and freedoms of all peoples, regardless of the color of their skin.

**9. What does courage mean to Mandela?**
**Ans:** To Mandela, courage does not mean the absence of fear, but the triumph over it. The brave man is not he who does not feel afraid, but he who conquers that fear.

**10. Which does he think is natural, to love or to hate?**
**Ans:** He thinks that love comes more naturally to the human heart than hate.

**11. What 'twin obligations' does Mandela mention?**
**Ans:** The 'twin obligations' Mandela mentions are: first, the obligations to his family, parents, wife, and children; and second, the obligations to his people, community, and country.

**12. What did being free mean to Mandela as a boy, and as a student? How does he contrast these "transitory freedoms" with "the basic and honourable freedoms"?**
**Ans:** As a boy, being free meant running in the fields, swimming in the clear stream, and roasting mealies under the stars. As a student, freedom meant the ability to stay out at night, read what he pleased, and go where he chose. He contrasts these "transitory freedoms" (which were temporary and only for himself) with "the basic and honourable freedoms" (which meant achieving his potential, earning his keep, and ensuring his entire people could live with dignity and self-respect).

**13. Does Mandela think the oppressor is free? Why / Why not?**
**Ans:** No, Mandela does not think the oppressor is free. He believes a man who takes away another man's freedom is a "prisoner of hatred," locked behind bars of prejudice and narrow-mindedness. The oppressor, like the oppressed, is robbed of their humanity.

### II. Thinking About The Text

**1. Why did such a large number of international leaders attend the inauguration? What did it signify the triumph of?**
**Ans:** A large number of international leaders attended the inauguration to confer glory and hope to the newborn liberty of South Africa, marking the end of its status as an international pariah. It signified the triumph of justice, peace, human dignity, and the global rejection of apartheid.

**2. What does Mandela mean when he says he is 'simply the sum of all those African patriots' who had gone before him?**
**Ans:** By this, Mandela means that his achievements were built upon the immense sacrifices of the thousands of brave freedom fighters who came before him. He learned the meaning of courage from these patriots. He feels he is the culmination of their struggles and sorrows, carrying forward the legacy they started.

**3. Would you agree that the 'depths of oppression' create 'heights of character'? How does Mandela illustrate this? Can you add your own examples to this argument?**
**Ans:** Yes, I agree that the 'depths of oppression' can create 'heights of character.' Mandela illustrates this by stating that the decades of profound brutality and oppression under apartheid produced men of extraordinary courage, wisdom, and generosity, such as Oliver Tambo, Walter Sisulu, and Chief Luthuli. Similarly, in India, the intense oppression of British colonial rule produced leaders of monumental character like Mahatma Gandhi, Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel, and Bhagat Singh.

**4. How did Mandela's understanding of freedom change with age and experience?**
**Ans:** In his boyhood, Mandela thought he was born free, viewing freedom purely as physical liberties (running, swimming). As a student, he recognized he lacked "transitory freedoms" to make his own choices. Finally, as a young man in Johannesburg, he realized that not only was his own freedom curtailed, but the freedom of everyone who looked like him was non-existent. His understanding evolved from a selfish desire for personal liberty to a profound hunger for the "basic and honourable freedoms" of his entire people.

**5. How did Mandela's 'hunger for freedom' change his life?**
**Ans:** Mandela's 'hunger for the freedom of his people' completely transformed his life. It changed a frightened young man into a bold one; it drove a law-abiding attorney to become a criminal in the eyes of the apartheid state; it turned a family-loving husband into a man without a home; and it forced a life-loving man to live like a monk. He sacrificed his personal life and comforts entirely for the greater cause of his nation's liberation.
