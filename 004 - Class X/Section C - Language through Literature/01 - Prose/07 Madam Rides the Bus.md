# Madam Rides the Bus - Class 10 Comprehensive Notes

**Author:** Vallikkannan
**(From the textbook *First Flight*)**

---

## Detailed Summary

This chapter is a sensitive and charming portrayal of a young girl's first independent interaction with the adult world, focusing on her meticulous planning, her emotional transition from joy to solemnity, and the vivid imagery of the South Indian landscape.

**Phase I: The Desire (The Exposition)**
In a small village in South India, an eight-year-old girl named Valliammai (Valli) has no playmates her age. Her favorite pastime is watching the bus that travels between her village and the nearest town. Watching the new set of passengers every hour creates a "tiny wish" in her to ride the bus just once, which grows into an "overwhelming desire."

**Phase II: The Preparation (Rising Action)**
Valli listens to neighbors' conversations and asks discreet questions, learning the town is six miles away, the trip takes forty-five minutes, and the fare is thirty paise one way. She saves every stray coin, stifling her desires for sweets and toys until she has sixty paise. She plans to take the one o'clock afternoon bus, calculating she will be back by two-forty-five while her mother is napping.

**Phase III: The Journey Outward (Rising Action)**
On a spring afternoon, Valli flags down the bus. The conductor, amused by her confidence, jokingly calls her "Madam." Valli stands on her seat to see over the window blinds, enjoying the view of the canal, palm trees, mountains, and blue sky. She laughs joyously at a young cow running wildly in front of the bus.

**Phase IV: The Town (The Climax)**
The bus reaches the town, and everyone gets off except Valli. The conductor offers to buy her a cold drink, but she firmly refuses, sticking to her budget and dignity. She stays on the bus, waiting for the return trip.

**Phase V: The Return Journey (Falling Action)**
On the way back, Valli sees the same cow she had laughed at earlier, now lying dead by the roadside after being struck by a fast-moving vehicle. The "lovable, beautiful creature" has turned into a "horrible, frightening" sight. The memory haunts her, and she loses her enthusiasm to look outside.

**Phase VI: The Silent Return (Resolution)**
She reaches home at three-forty-five and finds her mother awake, chatting with an aunt. Her mother and aunt discuss how there are many things happening outside without their knowledge. Valli smiles to herself, knowing she has just secretly experienced one of those "outside" things firsthand.

## Character Analysis

### A. Valliammai (Valli)
*   **Independent & Self-Respecting**: An eight-year-old who dislikes being called "child" or "Madam" condescendingly. She insists on paying her own way and standing on her own feet.
*   **Meticulous Planner**: Exhibits wisdom beyond her years by calculating the fare, timing the journey, and saving money by resisting the temptation of peppermints, toys, and merry-go-rounds.
*   **Highly Observant**: Her favorite pastime is standing in her front doorway, watching the "limitless" new experiences the street offers.
*   **Sensitive**: The sight of the dead cow profoundly moves her, shifting her mood from exuberant excitement to a quiet contemplation about life and death.

### B. The Bus Conductor
*   **Jolly & Good-Natured**: A "jolly sort," fond of joking. He mockingly but affectionately addresses Valli as "Madam" to acknowledge her self-importance and helps her enjoy the ride.
*   **Observant & Kind**: He notices Valli's confidence, handles her with amusement and respect, and even offers to buy her a drink when she refuses to get off in the town.

---

## Central Themes

### 1. The Loss of Innocence
Valli’s journey begins as a quest for fun and ends with a mature, solemn understanding of the fleeting nature of life and the suddenness of death.

### 2. Self-Reliance and Discipline
The story highlights how a child can achieve a significant goal through sheer willpower, financial planning, and self-control, without adult help.

### 3. The Mystery of the Outside World
Valli realizes that the "adult world" is full of experiences—both beautiful and tragic—that one can only understand by stepping out of one's comfort zone.

---

## Exam-Style Value Points (6-Mark Questions)

1.  **Valli's Planning and Execution**: Her ability to save sixty paise by suppressing all temptations shows a level of maturity, discipline, and determination unusual for an eight-year-old.
2.  **The Symbolism of the Cow**: The living cow represents the joy and comedy of life, while the dead cow symbolizes the harsh reality of death and the end of Valli's childhood innocence.
3.  **The Conductor's Role**: The conductor acts as a benevolent guide in the adult world, respecting Valli's boundaries while ensuring her safety.
4.  **The Significance of "Madam"**: The title "Madam" highlights Valli's assertive nature and her desire to be treated as an equal, independent passenger rather than a helpless child.
5.  **The Final Irony**: The ending conversation between Valli's mother and aunt is dramatic irony; they speak of unknown events in the outside world, unaware that Valli has just completed a major journey into that very world.

## Question & Answers (NCERT Solutions)

### I. Oral Comprehension Check

**1. What was Valli’s favourite pastime?**
**Ans:** Valli’s favourite pastime was standing in the front doorway of her house, watching what was happening in the street outside. It was every bit as enjoyable as any of the elaborate games other children played and gave her many unusual new experiences.

**2. What was a source of unending joy for Valli? What was her strongest desire?**
**Ans:** A source of unending joy for Valli was the sight of the bus, filled each time with a new set of passengers. Her strongest desire was to ride the bus just once.

**3. What did Valli find out about the bus journey? How did she find out these details?**
**Ans:** Valli found out that the town was six miles from her village, the fare was thirty paise one way, the trip took forty-five minutes, and she could return home on the same bus. She found out these details by carefully listening to conversations between her neighbours and regular bus users. She also asked a few discreet questions here and there.

**4. What do you think Valli was planning to do?**
**Ans:** Valli was planning to undertake her first bus ride. She planned to take the one o’clock afternoon bus, reach the town at one forty-five, and be back by about two forty-five while her mother was asleep.

**5. Why does the conductor call Valli ‘madam’?**
**Ans:** The conductor called Valli ‘madam’ because of her commanding behaviour and confident replies. When she shouted, ‘Stop the bus!’ and the conductor tried to help her board, she curtly replied, ‘Never mind, I can get on by myself.’ He addressed her as 'madam' jokingly to match her self-important, mature demeanor.

**6. Why does Valli stand up on the seat? What does she see now?**
**Ans:** Valli stood up on the seat because her view outside was cut off by a canvas blind that covered the lower part of the window. Standing up allowed her to peer over the blind. She saw the canal, palm trees, grasslands, distant mountains, and the blue sky on one side, and a deep ditch followed by acres of green fields on the other.

**7. What does Valli tell the elderly man when he calls her a child?**
**Ans:** When the elderly man called Valli a child, she haughtily told him that there was no one on the bus who was a child. She stated that she had paid her thirty paise like everyone else, making it clear she didn’t like being patronized.

**8. Why didn’t Valli want to make friends with the elderly woman?**
**Ans:** Valli didn’t want to make friends with the elderly woman because she found her absolutely repulsive. The woman had big holes in her earlobes with ugly earrings, and the way she was chewing betel nut—with the juice threatening to spill over her lips—made Valli feel it was impossible to be sociable with her.

**9. How did Valli save up money for her first journey? Was it easy for her?**
**Ans:** Valli saved up money by resisting every temptation to buy peppermints, toys, balloons, and similar items. She saved every coin she received. On the day of the village fair, she resolutely stifled a strong desire to ride the merry-go-round. This was not an easy task for an eight-year-old child.

**10. What did Valli see on her way that made her laugh?**
**Ans:** Valli saw a young cow with its tail high in the air, running very fast in the middle of the road right in front of the bus. The more the bus driver honked, the more frightened the cow became, running even faster. This sight was so funny to Valli that she clapped her hands and laughed until tears came to her eyes.

**11. Why didn’t she get off the bus at the bus station?**
**Ans:** She didn’t get off the bus at the bus station because she was afraid to wander around a strange town all by herself. Furthermore, she only had enough money to buy her return ticket and had accomplished her sole goal: riding the bus.

**12. Why didn’t Valli want to go to the stall and have a drink? What does this tell you about her?**
**Ans:** Valli didn’t want to go to the stall for a drink because she didn’t have enough money, and she also refused the cold drink offered by the conductor. This tells us that she was self-respecting, cautious, and possessed the strong willpower to resist temptations and avoid obligations from strangers.

**Ans:** The conductor calls Valli 'madam' as a playful acknowledgement of her confident, assertive, and unusually mature behavior. She demands to be treated like a paying adult, pays her fare independently, and refuses help getting on the bus. The conductor is amused by her serious, authoritative tone and plays along by treating her like a dignified lady.

**6. Why does Valli refuse to look out of the window on her way back?**
**Ans:** On her way back, Valli sees the same young cow that had entertained her earlier lying dead on the side of the road, struck by a vehicle. The sight of the dead, bloodied animal is a harsh shock. The memory of the dead cow haunts her, replacing her previous joy with fear and sadness, making her refuse to look out the window anymore.

**7. What does Valli mean when she says, “I was just agreeing with what you said about things happening without our knowledge.”**
**Ans:** Valli's mother and aunt were discussing how many things happen in the outside world without their knowledge. Valli casually agrees, secretly referring to her own recent adventure. She means that she, an eight-year-old, had just taken a bus ride to the town and back entirely without her mother's knowledge, perfectly proving their point.
