# From the Diary of Anne Frank - Class 10 Comprehensive Notes

**Author:** Anne Frank
**(From the textbook *First Flight*)**

---

## Detailed Summary

This text is a profound look at the "inner life" of a teenager caught in the machinery of the Holocaust. It is an excerpt from "The Diary of a Young Girl," highlighting Anne's school life before her family went into hiding.

**Phase 1: The Rationale (The Exposition)**
Anne decides to write a diary because she feels she has no one to truly confide in. She names the diary "Kitty" to make it feel like a real friend rather than a mere notebook. She provides a brief background of her family: her parents' marriage, her birth in Frankfurt, and their move to Holland.

**Phase 2: The Classroom Anxiety (The Inciting Incident)**
On June 20, 1942, the entire class is "shaking in its boots" because of the upcoming teachers' meeting to decide who moves to the next form. Anne finds the betting between her friends amusing and believes half the class are "dummies" who should be held back.

**Phase 3: The Conflict with Mr. Keesing (Rising Action)**
Mr. Keesing, the math teacher, is annoyed by Anne’s constant talking. As a punishment, he assigns her an extra essay on the topic: "A Chatterbox." Anne writes three pages arguing that talking is a student’s trait and that she cannot cure it because her mother talked as much as she did.

**Phase 4: The Escalation (Rising Action)**
After she continues to talk, he assigns a second essay: "An Incorrigible Chatterbox." When that fails to stop her, he assigns a third, ridiculous title: "Quack, Quack, Quack, Said Mistress Chatterbox."

**Phase 5: The Masterstroke (The Climax)**
With the help of her friend Sanne (who is good at poetry), Anne writes the third essay in verse. She writes a story about a mother duck and a father swan with three baby ducklings who were bitten to death by the father because they quacked too much.

**Phase 6: The Resolution**
Mr. Keesing takes the joke in the right spirit. He reads the poem to Anne’s class and other classes. Anne is finally allowed to talk in class, and Mr. Keesing stops giving her extra homework, even starting to make jokes himself.

## Character Analysis

### A. Anne Frank (The Protagonist)
*   **Introspective & Lonely**: Despite having "thirty people" she could call friends, she felt a lack of a "true friend," leading her to treat her diary as her soulmate.
*   **Witty & Talkative**: Her "chatterbox" nature is a central trait. She is quick-witted, as seen in her ability to turn Mr. Keesing’s punishments into clever jokes.
*   **Perceptive & Mature**: She understands the complexities of human nature, noting that "paper has more patience than people."
*   **Assertive**: She doesn't shy away from defending her nature, arguing that talking is a "female trait" inherited from her mother.

### B. Mr. Keesing (The Antagonist/Mentor Figure)
*   **Strict but Humorous**: Initially presented as an "old fogey" who is annoyed by Anne’s talking, he eventually shows a sense of humor.
*   **Fair-Minded**: He recognizes Anne’s wit and eventually allows her to talk in class after being "defeated" by her clever third essay.

### C. Margot Frank & Parents
*   **Margot**: Anne’s older sister, whom Anne feels is often favored or seen as more "proper."
*   **Otto Frank**: Anne describes him as the "most adorable father," who migrated to Holland to provide for the family.

## Central Themes

### 1. Loneliness in a Crowd
The central irony that one can be surrounded by family and friends yet feel completely alone, highlighting the need for genuine emotional connection.

### 2. The Power of Expression
Writing serves as a therapeutic outlet for Anne, proving the adage "Paper has more patience than people."

### 3. Adolescent Identity
The struggle of a young girl trying to fit into a rigid school system while maintaining her unique, vibrant personality.

---

## Exam-Style Value Points (6-Mark Questions)

1.  **"Paper has more patience than people"**: This explains Anne’s psychological state—people are often judgmental or bored, but a diary is a silent, non-judgmental confidant.
2.  **Inherited Traits**: Anne’s defense of her talkative nature as a "hereditary trait" shows her logical mind and her refusal to be shamed for her personality.
3.  **The Evolution of Mr. Keesing**: His character arc shows that creativity and wit can bridge the gap between strict authority (teachers) and students.
4.  **The Significance of "Kitty"**: Giving the diary a name transforms a writing exercise into a relationship, highlighting Anne's deep need for emotional intimacy.
5.  **Classroom Dynamics**: The description of the "shaking" class provides a realistic glimpse into the universal anxieties of students regarding academic results.

## Question & Answers (NCERT Solutions)

### I. Oral Comprehension Check

**1. What makes writing in a diary a strange experience for Anne Frank?**
**Ans:** Anne had never written anything before. Her thought that later on, neither she nor anyone else would be interested in the musings of a thirteen-year-old schoolgirl made it a strange experience for her.

**2. Why does Anne want to keep a diary?**
**Ans:** Anne wants to keep a diary because she has no close, true friend to confide in. Since this situation is not going to change, the diary will help her get all kinds of things off her chest.

**3. Why did Anne think she could confide more in her diary than in people?**
**Ans:** Anne thought she could confide more in her diary than in people because she believed in the saying that "paper has more patience than people." It would listen silently without judging her. Moreover, she never found a real friend to confide in deeply.

**4. Why does Anne provide a brief sketch of her life?**
**Ans:** Anne provides a brief sketch of her life so that everybody can understand every word of her stories to 'Kitty'. Without the brief background of her family and life, it would be difficult for anyone to understand her diary entries.

**5. What tells you that Anne loved her grandmother?**
**Ans:** Anne's birthday passed with little celebration when her Grandma fell ill in 1941 and had to have an operation. When Grandma died, on Anne's birthday in 1942, Grandma's candle was also lit along with the rest. She wrote that no one knew how often she thought of her and still loved her. These actions tell us that Anne loved her grandmother deeply.

**6. Why was Mr. Keesing annoyed with Anne? What did he ask her to do?**
**Ans:** Mr. Keesing was annoyed with Anne because she talked too much in his math class. As a punishment, he asked her to write an essay on the topic 'A Chatterbox', then another on 'An Incorrigible Chatterbox', and finally a third one on '"Quack, Quack, Quack," Said Mistress Chatterbox'.

**7. How did Anne justify her being a chatterbox in her essay?**
**Ans:** She gave two arguments to justify her being a 'Chatterbox': one, that talking is a student's trait, and two, that nothing can be done about inherited traits, as her mother talked just as much as she did, if not more.

**8. Do you think Mr. Keesing was a strict teacher?**
**Ans:** Mr. Keesing was a strict teacher, but not rigidly so. He expected discipline and silence in his class, which is acceptable. He punished Anne by assigning her extra essays. However, he had a good sense of humor; when Anne wrote a convincing and witty essay, he received it with a good laugh. When she finally countered his third punishment with a brilliant poem, he took the joke in the right spirit, read it to the class, and stopped punishing her. Therefore, he was strict but also fair and fun-loving.

**9. What made Mr. Keesing allow Anne to talk in class?**
**Ans:** Anne was able to justify her talkative nature every time she was punished by Mr. Keesing. On three occasions, he gave her essay topics as punishment. However, on each occasion, he was impressed and amused by the clever manner in which she presented her arguments, especially her final poem about the ducklings. Finally, Mr. Keesing accepted her wit and the fact that Anne would always be talkative. Hence, she was allowed to talk in class.

### II. Thinking About the Text

**1. Was Anne right when she said that the world would not be interested in the musings of a thirteen-year-old girl?**
**Ans:** No, Anne was not right when she said so. Her diary, "The Diary of a Young Girl," has become one of the most widely read books in the world. It has been translated into numerous languages. Moreover, it is an incredibly important historical document of daily life and the condition of Jews under Nazi occupation.

**2. There are some examples of diary or journal entries in the 'Before You Read' section. Compare these with what Anne writes in her diary. What language was the diary originally written in? In what way is Anne's diary different?**
**Ans:** Anne's diary was originally written in the Dutch language. Anne's diary is different because it is written in the form of a letter addressed to a friend named "Kitty." While it has the features of a traditional journal detailing daily events, it goes much deeper, describing intimate feelings, fears, and profound thoughts, making it a highly personal and emotional account.

**3. Why does Anne need to give a brief sketch about her family? Does she treat 'Kitty' as an insider or an outsider?**
**Ans:** Anne needs to give a brief sketch about her family because she thinks that no one would understand a word of her stories to 'Kitty' without knowing her background. She treats 'Kitty' as an ultimate insider. She considers 'Kitty' to be her closest, most patient friend and confides everything in her to get all kinds of burdens off her chest.

**4. How does Anne feel about her father, her grandmother, Mrs. Kuperus and Mr. Keesing? What do these tell you about her?**
**Ans:** Anne felt that her father was the "most adorable father" she had ever seen. She loved her grandmother deeply and remembered her often even after her death. In the sixth form at the Montessori school, she was very attached to her teacher and headmistress, Mrs. Kuperus; at the end of the year, they both had a heartbreaking farewell in tears. She found Mr. Keesing annoying at first because he punished her, but she engaged with his punishments creatively, eventually winning him over. All these relationships show that Anne was a lovable, sensitive, and smart girl who formed deep emotional attachments and could use her intelligence to resolve conflicts.

**5. What does Anne write in her first essay?**
**Ans:** In her first essay, titled 'A Chatterbox', Anne wanted to come up with convincing arguments to prove the necessity of talking. She wrote three pages and argued that talking was a student's trait and that she would do her best to keep it under control. She further argued that she would never be able to cure herself of the habit completely since her mother talked as much as she did, and there was not much one could do about inherited traits. Mr. Keesing had a good laugh reading her clever arguments.

**6. Anne says teachers are most unpredictable. Is Mr. Keesing unpredictable? How?**
**Ans:** Anne felt that teachers were the most unpredictable creatures on earth because nobody could guess who would pass and who would fail in the upcoming exams. Mr. Keesing can indeed be termed unpredictable. Usually, a teacher would lose his temper with a student who constantly talks. However, after several warnings, all Mr. Keesing did was assign her an extra essay as a joke. Each time she countered his jokes with witty writing. One could not have predicted that a strict math teacher would take a joke played on him in the right spirit. Finally, when she wrote a poem making fun of a strict father, he accepted it beautifully, allowed her to talk, and even started making jokes himself. This change of heart was highly unpredictable.

**7. What do these statements tell you about Anne Frank as a person?**
**(i) We don't seem to be able to get any closer and that's the problem. May be it's my fault that we don't confide in each other.**
**(ii) I don't want to jot down the facts in this diary the way most people would but I want the diary to be my friend.**
**(iii) Margot went to Holland in December and I followed in February, when I was plunked down on the table as a birthday present for Margot.**
**(iv) If you ask me, there are so many dummies that about a quarter of the class should be kept back, but teachers are the most unpredictable creatures on earth.**
**(v) Anyone could ramble on and leave big space between the words, but the trick was to come up with convincing arguments to prove the necessity of talking.**

**Ans:** 
**(i)** This shows that Anne had no true friend to confide in, despite having many acquaintances. It also shows her maturity, as she is introspective enough to put the blame on herself and admit her own shortcomings.
**(ii)** This shows Anne's deep need for a true companion. She was creative and imaginative, elevating a mere notebook to the status of a trusted confidant named Kitty, rather than just using it as a logbook.
**(iii)** This statement highlights Anne's fun-loving nature and witty sense of humor. She knew how to present an ordinary event (her arrival) in a funny and dramatic way ("plunked down... as a birthday present").
**(iv)** This shows she had a strong, critical opinion on everything and was quite confident in her own intelligence compared to the "dummies" in her class. It also shows her observant nature regarding the unpredictable behavior of adults.
**(v)** This shows that Anne was an intelligent and prideful writer. She didn't want to just complete a punishment lazily; she wanted to intellectually challenge her teacher by using her wit to prove her point convincingly.
