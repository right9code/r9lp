# A Tiger in the Zoo – Class 10 Comprehensive Summary & Poetic Devices

### Complete Poem Text
He stalks in his vivid stripes
The few steps of his cage,
On pads of velvet quiet,
In his quiet rage.

He should be lurking in shadow,
Sliding through the long grass
Near the water hole
Where plump deer pass.

He should be snarling around houses
At the jungle’s edge,
Baring his white fangs, his claws,
Terrorising the village!

But he’s locked in a concrete cell,
His strength behind bars,
Stalking the length of his cage,
Ignoring visitors.

He hears the last voice at night,
The patrolling cars,
And stares with his brilliant eyes
At the brilliant stars.
— **Leslie Norris**

---

### Detailed Summary (Stanza-wise)

#### First Stanza Analysis
The poem opens with the tiger pacing the limited space of its cage. Its stripes are "vivid" and bright, a sharp contrast to the dull environment. It moves silently on paws as soft as "velvet," suppressing a "quiet rage." This imagery captures the tiger’s restrained fury and the helplessness of a majestic creature in captivity.

#### Second Stanza Analysis
The poet imagines the tiger’s rightful life in the wild: lurking in the shadows and sliding through long grass near a water hole to ambush "plump deer." This stanza emphasizes the tiger’s natural predatory instincts, which are completely denied in the zoo.

#### Third Stanza Analysis
In the wild, the tiger would be a force to be reckoned with at the "jungle's edge." It would snarl near houses and terrorize villagers by showing its white fangs and sharp claws. This depicts the tiger as an apex predator asserting its dominance, a stark contrast to the powerless exhibit it has become.

#### Fourth Stanza Analysis
The reality returns to the "concrete cell." The tiger’s immense strength is now useless "behind bars." It no longer roars or hunts; it simply stalks the length of its cage, completely ignoring the visitors who come to gawp at it, showing its total indifference to human presence.

#### Fifth Stanza Analysis
As night falls, the tiger is surrounded by the sounds of "patrolling cars." In the final poignant image, the tiger looks up at the "brilliant stars" with its "brilliant eyes." This suggests a silent connection between the trapped tiger and the vast, free heavens, highlighting its longing for liberty.

---

### Central Themes

1.  **Freedom vs. Captivity**
    The poem creates a powerful contrast between the wild vitality of the jungle and the suffocating imprisonment of the zoo. It shows that captivity causes deep, quiet suffering.
2.  **Loss of Natural Instincts**
    Confinement alters a creature's essence. The tiger stops hunting and roaring, becoming a passive, hollow version of its true self.
3.  **The Ethics of Zoos**
    The poet urges the reader to reflect on the cruelty of turning majestic animals into spectacles for human entertainment.

### Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

*   **Rhyme Scheme:** **ABCB** (in every stanza). This creates a rhythmic, steady pace that mimics the tiger’s repetitive steps in the cage.
*   **Personification:** The poet refers to the tiger as "**He**" rather than "it," attributing human-like emotions such as "rage" and the choice to "ignore" others.
*   **Oxymoron:** "**Quiet rage**." It combines the silence of the tiger's movement with the intense fury building inside it.
*   **Metaphor:** 
    *   "**Pads of velvet quiet**": The tiger's soft paws are compared to velvet.
    *   "**Concrete cell**": Represents the prison-like nature of the enclosure.
*   **Imagery:** 
    *   **Visual:** "Vivid stripes," "white fangs," "brilliant stars."
    *   **Auditory:** "Snarling," "the last voice at night."
*   **Alliteration:** Repetition of sounds in "He **h**ears the last voice at night" and "**p**lump deer **p**ass."
*   **Assonance:** Repetition of the 'i' sound in "v**i**v**i**d str**i**pes" and "qu**i**et r**a**ge."
*   **Consonance:** Repetition of the 's' sound in "He stalk**s** in hi**s** vivid stripe**s**."
*   **Enjambment:** When a sentence continues into the next line without punctuation (e.g., "Sliding through the long grass / Near the water hole"). This reflects the fluid motion of the tiger.

---

### Word Meanings & Vocabulary
*   **Stalks:** To follow or move stealthily.
*   **Vivid:** Producing powerful feelings or strong, clear images in the mind.
*   **Lurking:** Remaining hidden so as to wait in ambush for someone or something.
*   **Snarling:** Making an aggressive growl with bared teeth.
*   **Baring:** Uncovering or exposing to view.
*   **Patrolling:** Keeping watch over an area by regularly walking or traveling around it.

---

### Key Exam Points (Memorize These)
*   **The Contrast:** Always contrast the **Cage** (rage, ignored visitors, concrete) with the **Wild** (lurking, water hole, terrorizing).
*   **Symbolism of Stars:** The stars symbolize freedom and hope—something the tiger can see but never reach.
*   **The "Quiet Rage":** This is the most important phrase; it describes the tiger’s internal state—furious but unable to act.
*   **Message:** Wild animals belong in their natural habitat where they can fulfill their biological roles.

### I. Thinking about the Poem

**1. Read the poem again, and work in pairs or groups to do the following tasks.**
**(i) Find the words that describe the movements and actions of the tiger in the cage and in the wild. Arrange them in two columns.**
**Ans:**

| In the Cage | In the Wild |
| :--- | :--- |
| Stalks | Lurking in shadow |
| Quiet rage | Sliding through long grass |
| Ignoring visitors | Snarling around houses |
| Hears the last voice | Baring his white fangs, his claws |
| Stares at the brilliant stars | Terrorising the village |

**(ii) Find the words that describe the two places, and arrange them in two columns.**
**Ans:**

| The Cage | The Wild |
| :--- | :--- |
| Few steps of his cage | Shadow |
| Locked in a concrete cell | Long grass |
| Behind bars | Water hole |
| Patrolling cars | Plump deer |
| Brilliant stars | Jungle's edge |

**2. Notice the use of a word repeated in lines such as these:**
**(i) On pads of velvet quiet, / In his quiet rage.**
**(ii) And stares with his brilliant eyes / At the brilliant stars.**
**What do you think is the effect of this repetition?**
**Ans:** The poet uses repetition to create a deeper impact and emphasize the tiger's tragic situation. 
In the first instance, "quiet" highlights the contrast between the absolute silence of his velvet paws and the suppressed, silent intensity of his "quiet rage" inside the cage. He is furious but helpless.
In the second instance, the word "brilliant" emphasizes the stark contrast between his majestic, shining eyes (full of life and longing) and the distant, shining stars. It brings out his yearning for freedom, looking at the open sky which he cannot reach.
