# The Tale of Custard the Dragon - Class 10 Comprehensive Summary & Poetic Devices

## Complete Poem Text
Belinda lived in a little white house,  
With a little black kitten and a little grey mouse,  
And a little yellow dog and a little red wagon,  
And a realio, trulio, little pet dragon.  

Now the name of the little black kitten was Ink,  
And the little grey mouse, she called him Blink,  
And the little yellow dog was sharp as Mustard,  
But the dragon was a coward, and she called him Custard.  

Custard the dragon had big sharp teeth,  
And spikes on top of him and scales underneath,  
Mouth like a fireplace, chimney for a nose,  
And realio, trulio daggers on his toes.  

Belinda was as brave as a barrel full of bears,  
And Ink and Blink chased lions down the stairs,  
Mustard was as brave as a tiger in a rage,  
But Custard cried for a nice safe cage.  

Belinda tickled him, she tickled him unmerciful,  
Ink, Blink and Mustard, they rudely called him Percival,  
They all sat laughing in the little red wagon  
At the realio, trulio, cowardly dragon.  

Belinda giggled till she shook the house,  
And Blink said Weeck! which is giggling for a mouse,  
Ink and Mustard asked him his age,  
When Custard cried for a nice safe cage.  

Suddenly, suddenly they heard a nasty sound,  
And Mustard growled, and they all looked around.  
Meowch! cried Ink, and ooh! cried Belinda,  
For there was a pirate, climbing in the winda.  

Pistol in his left hand, pistol in his right,  
And he held in his teeth a cutlass bright,  
His beard was black, one leg was wood;  
It was clear that the pirate meant no good.  

Belinda paled, and she cried Help! Help!  
But Mustard fled with a terrified yelp,  
Ink trickled down to the bottom of the household,  
And little mouse Blink strategically mouseholed.  

But up jumped Custard, snorting like an engine,  
Clashed his tail like irons in a dungeon,  
With a clatter and a clank and a jangling squirm,  
He went at the pirate like a robin at a worm.  

The pirate gaped at Belinda’s dragon,  
And gulped some grog from his pocket flagon,  
He fired two bullets, but they didn’t hit,  
And Custard gobbled him, every bit.  

Belinda embraced him, Mustard licked him,  
No one mourned for his pirate victim.  
Ink and Blink in glee did gyrate  
Around the dragon that ate the pirate.  

But presently up spoke little dog Mustard,  
I’d have been twice as brave if I hadn’t been flustered.  
And up spoke Ink and up spoke Blink,  
We’d have been three times as brave, we think,  
And Custard said, I quite agree  
That everybody is braver than me.  

Belinda still lives in her little white house,  
With her little black kitten and her little grey mouse,  
And her little yellow dog and her little red wagon,  
And her realio, trulio little pet dragon.  

Belinda is as brave as a barrel full of bears,  
And Ink and Blink chase lions down the stairs,  
Mustard is as brave as a tiger in a rage,  
But Custard keeps crying for a nice safe cage.  

**Ogden Nash**

## Detailed Summary

### Introduction of Characters
Belinda lives in a little white house with her four pets: a black kitten named **Ink**, a grey mouse named **Blink**, a yellow dog named **Mustard**, and a pet dragon named **Custard**. The poet introduces Custard as a terrifying creature with spikes, scales, a mouth like a fireplace, and dagger-like toes. However, ironically, Custard is a complete coward who constantly cries for a "nice safe cage."

### The Illusion of Bravery
The other characters constantly boast about their bravery. Belinda claims to be as brave as a "barrel full of bears." Ink and Blink claim they are brave enough to chase lions. Mustard boasts he is as fierce as an angry tiger. They all mercilessly tease and tickle Custard for his cowardice, calling him "Percival" (a knight of King Arthur's court who was considered brave but ran away).

### The Real Test (The Pirate)
One day, the peace of the house is shattered when a heavily armed pirate climbs through the window. He has two pistols, a cutlass in his mouth, a black beard, and a wooden leg. Faced with real danger, the "brave" characters instantly expose their cowardice. Belinda cries for help and turns pale. Mustard the dog runs away yelping. Ink hides in the basement, and Blink runs into his mousehole. 

### Custard's Heroism
While everyone else flees, the supposedly cowardly Custard jumps up to face the pirate. He snorts like an engine, clashes his heavy tail like iron chains in a dungeon, and attacks the pirate as swiftly as a robin catching a worm. The terrified pirate shoots two bullets, but they miss. Custard then swallows the pirate whole ("gobbled him, every bit").

### The Aftermath and Hypocrisy
Immediately after the dragon saves them, Belinda embraces him, and the others dance around him in joy. However, their ego soon takes over. Mustard, Ink, and Blink make up excuses for running away, claiming they would have been twice or three times as brave as Custard if they hadn't been "flustered." Custard, being humble and non-confrontational, simply agrees with them. 

### Conclusion
The poem ends by repeating the first and fourth stanzas. The situation has returned to normal. Belinda, Ink, Blink, and Mustard continue to boast about their false bravery, while the true hero, Custard, continues to ask for a "nice safe cage."

---

## Central Themes

### 1. Appearance vs. Reality
The poem teaches that true bravery is not about loud boasting or outward appearances. Those who boast the most (Belinda, Ink, Blink, Mustard) are often the biggest cowards in a crisis. The one who appears cowardly and quiet (Custard) is the one who steps up and acts heroically when it truly matters.

### 2. Humility
Even after single-handedly saving everyone, Custard does not boast about his bravery. He humbly accepts the others' excuses and agrees that everyone is braver than him. This highlights that true heroes do not need to brag about their actions.

## Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

*   **Rhyme Scheme:** AABB (couplets). This gives the poem a musical, nursery-rhyme-like rhythm.
*   **Simile:** Comparing using 'like' or 'as'.
    *   *Mouth like a fireplace*
    *   *Brave as a barrel full of bears*
    *   *Snorting like an engine*
    *   *Clashed his tail like irons in a dungeon*
*   **Metaphor:**
    *   *Chimney for a nose* (nose compared to a chimney).
*   **Poetic License / Coined Words:** The poet deliberately changes spellings and invents words to maintain the rhyme and rhythm.
    *   *Realio, trulio* (instead of real and true).
    *   *Winda* (instead of window, to rhyme with Belinda).
*   **Alliteration:**
    *   *B*elinda was as *b*rave as a *b*arrel full of *b*ears.
    *   *g*ulped some *g*rog
*   **Onomatopoeia:** Use of sound words.
    *   *Giggled, Weeck, Meowch, Clatter, Clank, Squirm, Yelping.*
*   **Allusion:** *Percival* - referring to the legendary Knight of the Round Table.

## Word Meanings & Vocabulary

*   **Daggers:** Sharp knives (referring to his claws).
*   **Unmerciful:** Cruelly, without mercy.
*   **Cutlass:** A short sword with a slightly curved blade.
*   **Grog:** An alcoholic drink (often associated with sailors/pirates).
*   **Flagon:** A large container for drinking, typically made of metal or pottery.
*   **Flustered:** Agitated, confused, or nervous.
*   **Gyrate:** To dance or move around in circles.

## Key Exam Points (Memorize These)
1.  **The Ballad Form:** The poem is written in the style of a ballad—a song or poem that tells a story. However, it is a parody (a humorous imitation) of a traditional ballad because instead of a handsome knight, the hero is a cowardly pet dragon.
2.  **Why 'Percival'?** They call him Percival to mock him. Percival was a brave knight, but here it is used sarcastically because Custard cries for a cage.
3.  **The Final Irony:** The biggest irony is the ending. Even after the dragon proves his bravery, the pets return to their old boasting, and Custard returns to his humble, "cowardly" persona.

## Question & Answers (NCERT Solutions)

### I. Thinking about the Poem

**1. Who are the characters in this poem? List them with their pet names.**
**Ans:** The characters in the poem are:
1.  **Belinda:** A little girl who lives in a white house.
2.  **Ink:** A little black kitten.
3.  **Blink:** A little grey mouse.
4.  **Mustard:** A little yellow dog.
5.  **Custard:** A pet dragon.
6.  **The Pirate:** An intruder who climbs through the window.

**2. Why did Custard cry for a nice safe cage? Why is the dragon called "cowardly dragon"?**
**Ans:** Custard cried for a nice safe cage because he was timid, peace-loving, and easily frightened. He preferred the safety of a cage over the dangers of the outside world. He is called a "cowardly dragon" because his behavior drastically contrasts with the fierce, aggressive physical appearance typical of dragons (spikes, scales, fire-breathing mouth). Instead of being brave, he constantly demands protection.

**3. "Belinda tickled him, she tickled him unmerciful..." Why?**
**Ans:** Belinda tickled Custard unmercifully to mock him and make fun of his cowardly nature. While dragons are supposed to be terrifying and fearless beasts, Custard was so soft and sensitive that he would cry for a safe cage. Tickling him was a way for Belinda and the other pets to establish their "superiority" and tease him for not living up to his fierce physical appearance.

**4. The poet has employed many poetic devices in the poem. For example: "Clashed his tail like irons in a dungeon" — the poetic device here is a simile. Can you, with your partner, list some more such poetic devices used in the poem?**
**Ans:** Yes, the poem is rich in poetic devices:
*   **Simile:** "Mouth like a fireplace," "as brave as a barrel full of bears," "snorting like an engine," "went at the pirate like a robin at a worm."
*   **Metaphor:** "Chimney for a nose" (nose is compared to a chimney emitting smoke).
*   **Alliteration:** "Belinda was as brave as a barrel full of bears" (repetition of 'b' sound).
*   **Onomatopoeia:** Use of sound words like "clatter," "clank," "weeck," and "meowch."
*   **Poetic License:** "realio, trulio" (for real and true) and "winda" (for window).

**5. Read stanza three again to know how the poet describes the appearance of the dragon.**
**Ans:** In stanza three, the poet describes Custard as a terrifying creature. He has big, sharp teeth, spikes on his back, and hard scales underneath his belly. His mouth is described as a "fireplace" (implying he breathes fire), and his nose is like a "chimney" (emitting smoke). His toes possess sharp, dagger-like claws. His physical appearance is fierce, which is highly ironic given his timid personality.

**6. Can you find out the rhyme scheme of two or three stanzas of the poem?**
**Ans:** The rhyme scheme of the poem is highly regular. Every stanza follows the **AABB** rhyme scheme, which consists of rhyming couplets.
For example, in stanza 1: house (A) / mouse (A) / wagon (B) / dragon (B).
In stanza 2: Ink (A) / Blink (A) / Mustard (B) / Custard (B).

**7. Writers use words to give us a picture or image without actually saying what they mean. Can you trace some images used in the poem?**
**Ans:** The poet uses vivid visual and auditory imagery throughout the poem:
*   **Visual Imagery:** "pistol in his left hand, pistol in his right," "his beard was black, one leg was wood," "mouth like a fireplace." These paint a clear picture of the terrifying pirate and the fierce-looking dragon.
*   **Auditory Imagery (Sound):** "snorting like an engine," "clashed his tail like irons in a dungeon," "clatter and a clank." These words help the reader "hear" the violent and noisy attack of Custard on the pirate.

**8. Do you find The Tale of Custard the Dragon to be a serious or a light-hearted poem? Give reasons to support your answer.**
**Ans:** *The Tale of Custard the Dragon* is a highly light-hearted and humorous poem. The humor stems from several elements:
1.  **Irony:** A fierce-looking dragon is a coward, while tiny animals like a mouse and kitten boast of chasing lions.
2.  **Rhyme and Rhythm:** The AABB rhyme scheme and coined words like "realio, trulio" and "winda" give it a musical, nursery-rhyme quality.
3.  **Hypocrisy:** The humor peaks at the end when the "brave" pets make silly excuses for running away from the pirate, and the humble dragon easily agrees that they are braver than him, despite being the only one who actually fought.
