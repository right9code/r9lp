# Fog - Class 10 Comprehensive Summary & Poetic Devices

## Complete Poem Text
The fog comes  
on little cat feet.  

It sits looking  
over harbour and city  
on silent haunches  
and then moves on.  

**Carl Sandburg**

---

## Detailed Summary

This short poem is a vivid observation of nature, specifically the arrival and departure of fog over a city and its harbor. The poet compares the fog to a cat. 

In the first stanza, the poet states that the fog arrives quietly and stealthily, just like a cat walks silently on its "little cat feet." You do not hear the fog coming; it just suddenly appears.

In the second stanza, the poet describes what the fog does once it arrives. Just as a cat sits on its haunches (hind legs) and observes its surroundings silently, the fog settles over the harbor and the city, covering them completely. It "looks over" the city in absolute silence. After a while, just as unpredictably and silently as a cat decides to walk away, the fog lifts and "moves on," clearing the view.

---

## Central Themes

### 1. The Transience of Nature
The poem highlights the temporary, ever-changing state of nature. The fog arrives, stays for a while, and then leaves. It serves as a reminder that natural phenomena are transient and cannot be controlled by humans.

### 2. The Silent Power of Nature
Despite having no physical form or voice, the fog has a commanding presence. It quietly engulfs an entire city and harbor, demonstrating the silent, overwhelming power of nature over human-made environments.

---

## Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

*   **Free Verse:** The poem has no rhyme scheme and no regular meter. This unstructured form mirrors the free, flowing, and unpredictable nature of both the fog and a cat.
*   **Metaphor:** The entire poem is an extended metaphor. The poet does not use "like" or "as", but directly compares the fog to a living cat.
    *   *on little cat feet* (The quiet arrival of fog is compared to a cat's silent steps).
    *   *on silent haunches* (The way fog settles over the city is compared to a cat sitting on its hind legs).
*   **Enjambment:** Sentences continue onto the next line without punctuation.
    *   *It sits looking / over harbour and city*
*   **Personification/Animalification:** Giving the non-living fog the characteristics of a living animal (a cat) by saying it "comes on feet" and "sits looking."

---

## Word Meanings & Vocabulary

*   **Fog:** A thick cloud of tiny water droplets suspended in the atmosphere at or near the earth's surface.
*   **Harbour:** A place on the coast where vessels may find shelter.
*   **Haunches:** The back legs and thighs of an animal, used for sitting or squatting.

---

## Key Exam Points (Memorize These)
1.  **The Three Similarities:** The poet compares the fog to a cat based on three distinct actions:
    *   **Arrival:** Both arrive silently and stealthily (on little cat feet).
    *   **Presence:** Both sit silently and observe their surroundings (on silent haunches).
    *   **Departure:** Both move on unpredictably and quietly.
2.  **Brevity of the Poem:** It is only six lines long. The shortness of the poem perfectly matches the fleeting, short-lived nature of fog.

## Question & Answers (NCERT Solutions)

### I. Thinking about the Poem

**1. (i) What does Sandburg think the fog is like?**
**(ii) How does the fog come?**
**(iii) What does 'it' in the third line refer to?**
**(iv) Does the poet actually say that the fog is like a cat? Find three things that tell us that the fog is like a cat.**
**Ans:** (i) Sandburg thinks the fog is like a cat.
(ii) The fog comes very quietly and stealthily, without making any noise, just like a cat walking on its soft, little paws.
(iii) The word 'it' in the third line refers to the fog.
(iv) No, the poet does not explicitly use the words "like a cat" (which would be a simile). Instead, he uses an extended metaphor to describe the fog through a cat's actions. The three things that tell us the fog is like a cat are:
1.  It comes silently "on little cat feet."
2.  It settles down to observe the city by sitting "on silent haunches."
3.  Just like a cat that gets up and leaves quietly, the fog eventually "moves on."

**2. Does this poem have a rhyme scheme? Poetry that does not have an obvious rhythm or rhyme is called 'free verse'.**
**Ans:** No, this poem does not have a rhyme scheme or an obvious rhythm. Therefore, it is written in 'free verse'. This style is perfectly suited for the poem because it mirrors the fluid, unstructured, and unpredictable movement of the fog as it drifts in and out of the city.
