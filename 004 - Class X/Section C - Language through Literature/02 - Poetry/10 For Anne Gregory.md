# For Anne Gregory - Class 10 Comprehensive Summary & Poetic Devices

## Complete Poem Text
"Never shall a young man,  
Thrown into despair  
By those great honey-coloured  
Ramparts at your ear,  
Love you for yourself alone  
And not your yellow hair."  

"But I can get a hair-dye  
And set such colour there,  
Brown, or black, or carrot,  
That young men in despair  
May love me for myself alone  
And not my yellow hair."  

"I heard an old religious man  
But yesternight declare  
That he had found a text to prove  
That only God, my dear,  
Could love you for yourself alone  
And not your yellow hair."  

**William Butler Yeats**

---

## Detailed Summary

### Stanza 1: The Speaker's Assertion
The poem begins as a conversation between the poet (or a speaker) and a young woman named Anne Gregory. The speaker tells Anne that no young man will ever love her purely for her inner beauty or personality ("for yourself alone"). Instead, the young men are thrown into despair (madly in love) because of her external beauty, specifically her beautiful "honey-coloured" blonde hair that falls around her ears like "ramparts" (protective walls of a castle). The speaker insists that physical attraction will always be the primary reason a man loves her.

### Stanza 2: Anne Gregory's Rebuttal
Anne Gregory responds to the speaker. She refuses to accept that she can only be loved for her physical appearance. She argues that her yellow hair is superficial and can easily be changed. She states that she could dye her hair brown, black, or even bright orange ("carrot"). She believes that if she makes herself physically less attractive or changes the very thing men are drawn to (her yellow hair), then young men will be forced to look past her exterior and love her for her true self ("myself alone").

### Stanza 3: The Speaker's Final Argument
The speaker replies to Anne's optimistic view by quoting an authority figure. He mentions that just the previous night ("yesternight"), he heard an old religious man declare that he had found a religious text to prove a profound truth. According to this text, human beings are inherently superficial. Only God has the capacity to look past external beauty and love someone purely for their soul, character, and inner self. Therefore, the speaker concludes that it is impossible for any human man to love Anne without being influenced by her yellow hair.

---

## Central Themes

### 1. Internal vs. External Beauty
The poem explores the conflict between inner character and outer appearance. It highlights the human tendency to be captivated by physical beauty (yellow hair) rather than appreciating a person's true essence and soul.

### 2. The Nature of Human Love vs. Divine Love
The poem draws a sharp contrast between human love, which is often rooted in physical attraction and superficiality, and divine love. It asserts that only God has the pure, unconditional capacity to love a person for their inner self alone, regardless of their physical appearance.

---

## Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

*   **Rhyme Scheme:** The poem follows an **ABCBDB** rhyme scheme in each stanza (despair/ear/hair; there/despair/hair; declare/dear/hair).
*   **Metaphor:** 
    *   *Honey-coloured Ramparts:* Anne's hair falling around her ears is compared to the ramparts (defensive walls) of a fort. Just as a fort's walls protect the castle and trap enemies, her hair traps young men's hearts and prevents them from looking inside at her true character.
*   **Alliteration:** 
    *   *Y*our *y*ellow hair
    *   *S*et *s*uch colour
*   **Repetition:** The phrase "Love you for yourself alone / And not your yellow hair" is repeated at the end of every stanza to emphasize the core theme of the poem.
*   **Dialogue Structure:** The poem is written as a conversational debate. Stanza 1 is the speaker, Stanza 2 is Anne, and Stanza 3 is the speaker again.

---

## Word Meanings & Vocabulary

*   **Despair:** Complete loss of hope (here, used to mean hopelessly and madly in love).
*   **Ramparts:** The high, wide walls built around a castle or fort for defense.
*   **Yesternight:** Last night / the previous night.
*   **Text:** A religious book or scripture.
*   **Carrot:** Here, referring to an orange-red hair color.

---

## Key Exam Points (Memorize These)
1.  **The Ramparts Metaphor:** Anne's hair acts as a barrier. The men are so captivated by the "walls" of her physical beauty that they never bother to "enter the castle" to know her inner self.
2.  **Anne's Solution:** Anne suggests dyeing her hair an unattractive or common color so that men stop focusing on her exterior and start loving her for who she is inside.
3.  **The Religious Man's Text:** The religious text proves that human love is flawed and superficial. Only God is capable of unconditional, purely spiritual love.

## Question & Answers (NCERT Solutions)

### I. Thinking about the Poem

**1. What does the young man mean by "great honey-coloured / Ramparts at your ear"? Why does he say that young men are "thrown into despair" by them?**
**Ans:** By "great honey-coloured ramparts at your ear," the speaker means Anne Gregory's beautiful, thick, blonde hair that falls around her face and ears. He uses a metaphor comparing her hair to the protective walls (ramparts) of a fort. He says young men are "thrown into despair" by them because her physical beauty is so captivating that men fall madly and hopelessly in love with her appearance, feeling despair over wanting to win her affection. However, these "ramparts" also block them from looking past her beauty to see her true inner self.

**2. What colour is the young woman's hair? What does she say she can change it to? Why would she want to do so?**
**Ans:** The young woman's hair is "honey-coloured" or yellow (blonde). She says she can change it to brown, black, or carrot (orange) using a hair dye. She wants to do this because she is tired of men only being attracted to her external beauty. She hopes that by changing her beautiful yellow hair to a different or less striking color, men will stop focusing on her appearance and finally love her for her true personality and inner self ("for myself alone").

**3. Objects have qualities which make them desirable to others. Can you think of some objects (a car, a phone, a dress...) and say what qualities make one object more desirable than another? Imagine you were trying to sell an object: what qualities would you emphasize?**
**Ans:** Objects are generally considered desirable based on their external appearance, functionality, brand value, and durability. For example, a car is desirable if it has a sleek design, high speed, and good mileage. A phone is desirable based on its camera quality, processing speed, and aesthetics. If I were trying to sell an object, such as a phone, I would emphasize its unique features, sleek design, camera capabilities, and long battery life to make it appealing to the buyer's superficial desires and practical needs.

**4. What about people? Do we love others because we like their qualities, whether physical or mental? Or is it possible to love someone "for themselves alone"? Are some people 'more lovable' than others? Discuss this question in pairs or groups, considering points like the following.**
**(i) A parent or caregiver's love for a newborn baby, for a mentally or physically challenged child, for a clever child or a prodigy.**
**(ii) The public's love for a film star, a sportsperson, a politician, or a social worker.**
**(iii) Your love for a friend, or brother or sister.**
**(iv) Your love for a pet, and the pet's love for you.**
**Ans:** Human love is complex and often influenced by physical and mental qualities. While it is difficult for humans to love someone entirely "for themselves alone" without being influenced by external factors, certain types of love come close to this ideal:
*(i)* A parent's love for a newborn, or a mentally/physically challenged child, is the closest humans come to unconditional love. It is not based on the child's physical beauty or intellectual achievements (like a prodigy) but is an instinctual, selfless bond.
*(ii)* The public's love for a film star or sportsperson is largely superficial, based on physical attractiveness, talent, or public image. It is conditional and fades if the person loses their charm or skill.
*(iii)* Love for a sibling or a close friend is usually a mix. It starts with shared experiences and compatible mental qualities but often evolves into a deep, unconditional bond that looks past physical appearances.
*(iv)* A pet's love for its owner is arguably the purest form of earthly love. A dog, for instance, loves its owner purely "for themselves alone," completely disregarding the owner's wealth, physical appearance, or social status.
