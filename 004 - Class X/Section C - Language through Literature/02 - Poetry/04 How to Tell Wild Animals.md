# How to Tell Wild Animals - Class 10 Comprehensive Summary & Poetic Devices

## Complete Poem Text
If ever you should go by chance  
To jungles in the east;  
And if there should to you advance  
A large and tawny beast,  
If he roars at you as you’re dyin’  
You’ll know it is the Asian Lion...  

Or if some time when roaming round,  
A noble wild beast greets you,  
With black stripes on a yellow ground,  
Just notice if he eats you.  
This simple rule may help you learn  
The Bengal Tiger to discern.  

If strolling forth, a beast you view,  
Whose hide with spots is peppered,  
As soon as he has leapt on you,  
You’ll know it is the Leopard.  
’Twill do no good to roar with pain,  
He’ll only lep and lep again.  

If when you’re walking round your yard  
You meet a creature there,  
Who hugs you very, very hard,  
Be sure it is a Bear.  
If you have any doubts, I guess  
He’ll give you just one more caress.  

Though to distinguish beasts of prey  
A novice might nonplus,  
The Crocodile you always may  
Tell from the Hyena thus:  
Hyenas come with merry smiles;  
But if they weep they’re Crocodiles.  

The true Chameleon is small,  
A lizard sort of thing;  
He hasn’t any ears at all,  
And not a single wing.  
If there is nothing on the tree,  
’Tis the chameleon you see.  

**Carolyn Wells**

## Detailed Summary

### Stanza 1: The Asian Lion
The poet suggests that if you ever visit the jungles of the East and encounter a large, yellowish-brown (tawny) beast that advances towards you, you might be in danger. If the beast roars so loudly and fiercely that you feel you are dying of fear, then you can be absolutely certain that you have encountered an Asian Lion.

### Stanza 2: The Bengal Tiger
If you are roaming around and are "greeted" by a noble wild beast with black stripes on a yellow background, you should observe its behavior. The poet humorously states that if this beast eats you, this "simple rule" will help you identify it as the Bengal Tiger. The dark humor lies in the fact that the identification is of no use once you are eaten.

### Stanza 3: The Leopard
While strolling, if you see a beast whose skin is covered (peppered) with dark spots, and it immediately leaps upon you, you will know it is a leopard. The poet warns that crying out in pain won't help, because the leopard will continuously "lep and lep" (leap) on you to attack.

### Stanza 4: The Bear
If you are walking in your yard and meet a creature that gives you a very tight, bone-crushing hug, you should know it is a Bear. If you still have doubts about its identity, the bear will give you "just one more caress" (another tight hug) to confirm it—which is a humorous way of saying it will squeeze you to death.

### Stanza 5: Hyena vs. Crocodile
The poet states that a beginner (novice) might get confused (nonplus) when trying to distinguish between beasts of prey. However, the rule is simple: a Hyena always approaches with a "merry smile" (referring to the sound it makes), whereas a Crocodile sheds tears (crocodile tears) while swallowing its victim.

### Stanza 6: The Chameleon
A true chameleon is a small creature that looks like a lizard. It lacks ears and wings. The defining characteristic of a chameleon is its ability to camouflage. Therefore, if you look at a tree and see absolutely nothing on it, it means a chameleon is sitting there, having blended perfectly with its surroundings.

## Central Themes

### 1. Humour and Irony
The primary theme of the poem is dark humor. The poet suggests highly dangerous and fatal ways to identify wild animals. The irony is that the "rules" of identification are usually learned at the cost of one's life.

### 2. Wildlife Recognition
Underneath the humorous tone, the poet does outline the distinct physical characteristics and behavioral traits of various wild animals (lion's roar, tiger's stripes, leopard's spots, bear's hug, chameleon's camouflage).

## Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

*   **Rhyme Scheme:** The poem follows a consistent rhyme scheme of **ABABCC** in each stanza.
*   **Enjambment:** Continuation of a sentence without a pause beyond the end of a line.
    *   *If ever you should go by chance / To jungles in the east;*
*   **Alliteration:** Repetition of consonant sounds.
    *   **r**oaming **r**ound
    *   **w**ho hugs you **v**ery, **v**ery hard
    *   **n**ovice might **n**onplus
*   **Poetic License:** The poet deliberately changes the spelling of words to maintain the rhythm and rhyme of the poem.
    *   *dyin'* instead of dying (to rhyme with lion)
    *   *lep* instead of leap (to maintain rhythm)
    *   *nonplus* used as a verb instead of a noun/adjective.
*   **Irony / Sarcasm:** 
    *   Calling the tiger a "noble" beast right before stating it will eat you.
    *   Calling a bear's deadly squeeze a "caress."
    *   Saying "just notice if he eats you" as a "simple rule" for identification.
*   **Oxymoron:** "Merry smiles" of a deadly hyena.

## Word Meanings & Vocabulary

*   **Tawny:** Yellowish-brown color.
*   **Noble:** Belonging to a high rank; here, used ironically for a majestic but deadly animal.
*   **Discern:** To recognize or identify.
*   **Peppered:** Covered with spots.
*   **Caress:** A gentle, loving touch (used ironically here for a deadly hug).
*   **Novice:** A beginner; someone new to a job or situation.
*   **Nonplus:** To confuse or puzzle.

## Key Exam Points (Memorize These)
1.  **The Humorous Tone:** The humor arises from the fact that the poet suggests identifying dangerous animals through fatal encounters. 
2.  **Tiger vs. Leopard:** The tiger has black stripes on a yellow ground and eats you quietly. The leopard has spots and continuously leaps on you.
3.  **Hyena vs. Crocodile:** Hyenas deceive with a smile; crocodiles deceive with tears.
4.  **The Chameleon's trait:** It can camouflage, which is why if you see nothing on the tree, you are actually seeing the chameleon.

## Question & Answers (NCERT Solutions)

### I. Thinking about the Poem

**1. Does 'dyin' really rhyme with 'lion'? Can you say it in such a way that it does?**
**Ans:** No, the correct spelling "dying" does not strictly rhyme with "lion." However, the poet uses **poetic license** to change the spelling and pronunciation to "dyin'," dropping the 'g' sound. When read aloud in a conversational or slightly rustic tone, "dyin'" and "lion" produce a matching rhythmic sound, making them rhyme within the context of the poem.

**2. How does the poet suggest that you identify the lion and the tiger? When can you do so, according to him?**
**Ans:** The poet suggests that you can identify an Asian Lion by its large, tawny appearance and its terrifying roar that makes you feel like you are dying of fear. You can identify a Bengal Tiger by its noble appearance and the black stripes on its yellow body. According to the poet, you can do this when you are roaming in the jungles of the East and these beasts advance towards you or, humorously, right before the tiger eats you.

**3. Do you think the words 'lep' and 'lep' in the third stanza are spelt correctly? Why does the poet spell them like this?**
**Ans:** No, the words 'lep' and 'lep' are not spelled correctly; the correct spelling is "leap." The poet spells them this way intentionally, using **poetic license**. This is done to maintain the rhythm of the poem and to create a sense of fast-paced, repetitive action, emphasizing how the leopard will continuously bounce and attack its prey without pause.

**4. Do you know what a 'bearhug' is? It's a friendly and strong hug — such as bears are thought to give, as they attack you! Again, hyenas are thought to laugh, and crocodiles to weep ('crocodile tears') as they swallow their victims. Are there similar expressions and popular ideas about wild animals in your own language(s)?**
**Ans:** Yes, the poem plays on popular English idioms. A "bearhug" is generally a tight, affectionate embrace among humans, but the poet uses it literally to describe how a bear crushes its prey. "Crocodile tears" refers to false, insincere sorrow, derived from the myth that crocodiles weep while eating. "Laughing like a hyena" refers to loud, hysterical laughter, based on the sound hyenas make. *(Students can add equivalent idioms from Hindi or their regional languages here, such as 'Magarmach ke aansu' which means the exact same as crocodile tears).*

**5. Look at the line "A novice might nonplus". How would you write this correctly? Why is the poet's incorrect line better in the poem?**
**Ans:** Written correctly according to standard grammar, the line should be: "A novice might *be nonplussed*." The poet uses the word 'nonplus' incorrectly as a verb in the active voice. However, the poet's incorrect line is better in the poem because it maintains the strict meter and allows the word "nonplus" to perfectly rhyme with "thus" in the next line, preserving the rhyme scheme (ABABCC) and the playful, humorous tone of the verse.
