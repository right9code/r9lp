# Amanda! - Class 10 Comprehensive Summary & Poetic Devices

## Complete Poem Text
Don’t bite your nails, Amanda!  
Don’t hunch your shoulders, Amanda!  
Stop that slouching and sit up straight,  
Amanda!  

*(There is a languid, emerald sea,*  
*where the sole inhabitant is me—*  
*a mermaid, drifting blissfully.)*  

Did you finish your homework, Amanda?  
Did you tidy your room, Amanda?  
I thought I told you to clean your shoes,  
Amanda!  

*(I am an orphan, roaming the street.*  
*I pattern soft dust with my hushed, bare feet.*  
*The silence is golden, the freedom is sweet.)*  

Don’t eat that chocolate, Amanda!  
Remember your acne, Amanda!  
Will you please look at me when I’m speaking to you,  
Amanda!  

*(I am Rapunzel, I have not a care;*  
*life in a tower is tranquil and rare;*  
*I’ll certainly never let down my bright hair!)*  

Stop that sulking at once, Amanda!  
You’re always so moody, Amanda!  
Anyone would think that I nagged at you,  
Amanda!  

**Robin Klein**

---

## Detailed Summary

### Stanza 1 & 2 (The Mermaid)
**The Parent's Voice:** The poem opens with a parent (likely the mother) constantly instructing a young girl named Amanda. She is told not to bite her nails, not to hunch her shoulders, and to sit up straight. The repetition of her name emphasizes the constant nagging.
**Amanda's Escape:** Instead of listening, Amanda retreats into her imagination. The brackets indicate her inner thoughts. She imagines herself as a mermaid living in a calm, green (emerald) sea. As a mermaid, she is the only inhabitant there, drifting happily and peacefully away from the constant scolding.

### Stanza 3 & 4 (The Orphan)
**The Parent's Voice:** The nagging continues. The mother asks Amanda if she has finished her homework, cleaned her room, and polished her shoes. 
**Amanda's Escape:** Amanda ignores the questions and imagines herself as an orphan roaming the streets. It is highly unusual for a child to wish to be an orphan, but Amanda desires it because an orphan has no parents to nag them. She imagines walking barefoot in the soft dust. For her, the silence of being alone is "golden," and the freedom from constant instructions is "sweet."

### Stanza 5 & 6 (Rapunzel)
**The Parent's Voice:** The mother forbids Amanda from eating chocolate, reminding her that it causes acne. The mother becomes irritated because Amanda is ignoring her and demands that Amanda look at her while she is speaking.
**Amanda's Escape:** Amanda imagines herself as Rapunzel, the fairy tale princess locked in a tower. She feels that life in a tall tower would be peaceful (tranquil) and rare, far away from her nagging mother. However, unlike the real Rapunzel who let her hair down for the prince to climb up, Amanda resolves *never* to let her hair down; she wants no visitors and wishes to remain entirely alone.

### Stanza 7 (The Final Scolding)
**The Parent's Voice:** In the final stanza, the mother notices Amanda's sulking and moody behavior (caused by the constant scolding). Ironically, the mother tells her to stop sulking because people might think that she (the mother) is nagging her. The mother cares more about what society thinks of her parenting than understanding her daughter's mental state. Amanda retreats entirely into silence; there is no bracketed stanza after this, indicating she has stopped imagining or has completely shut down.

## Central Themes

### 1. Parenting vs. Over-parenting
The poem highlights the negative effects of constant nagging and over-parenting. While the mother's intentions (teaching manners, discipline, hygiene) might be good, her method restricts Amanda's freedom and creativity, pushing the child into a state of silent rebellion and depression.

### 2. Escapism and Imagination
Children naturally use their imagination as a coping mechanism against harsh realities. Amanda uses escapism (imagining herself as a mermaid, an orphan, and Rapunzel) to find the peace and freedom she is denied in real life.

### 3. The Desire for Freedom
Amanda's extreme desires—to be alone in the sea, to be an orphan on the street, or locked in a tower—show how desperately she craves freedom and silence.

## Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

*   **Anaphora:** Repeating words at the beginning of consecutive lines.
    *   *Don't* bite your nails... / *Don't* hunch your shoulders...
    *   *Did you* finish your homework... / *Did you* tidy your room...
*   **Epiphora / Repetition:** The name "Amanda!" is repeated at the end of almost every instruction to show the mother's frustration and the constant nagging.
*   **Metaphor:**
    *   *Emerald sea:* The sea is compared to a green emerald.
    *   *Silence is golden:* Silence is compared to something highly valuable.
    *   *Freedom is sweet:* Freedom is compared to a sweet taste.
*   **Allusion:** Referencing well-known cultural or literary figures.
    *   *Mermaid:* A mythical sea creature.
    *   *Rapunzel:* A famous fairy-tale character from the Brothers Grimm.
*   **Alliteration:**
    *   *S*top that *s*louching and *s*it up *s*traight
    *   *L*ife in a tower is tranquil and *l*et down my bright hair (L sound).
*   **Visual Imagery:** "pattern soft dust with my hushed, bare feet", "languid, emerald sea".

## Word Meanings & Vocabulary

*   **Hunch:** To bend one's back and shoulders forward.
*   **Slouching:** Sitting or standing in a lazy, drooping way.
*   **Languid:** Relaxed, slow, and peaceful.
*   **Emerald:** A bright green color.
*   **Sole:** Only; single.
*   **Drifting:** Moving slowly with the water.
*   **Tranquil:** Calm, peaceful, and quiet.
*   **Sulking:** Being silent and bad-tempered out of annoyance or disappointment.
*   **Nagged:** Constantly harassed or scolded.

## Key Exam Points (Memorize These)
1.  **The Brackets:** The stanzas in brackets represent Amanda's inner thoughts, daydreams, and her escape from reality. They are not spoken aloud.
2.  **Why an Orphan?** Amanda wishes to be an orphan not because she hates her parents, but because an orphan has no one to constantly dictate rules. It represents her ultimate craving for freedom.
3.  **The Rapunzel Twist:** Unlike the fairy tale, Amanda says she will *never* let down her hair. She doesn't want a prince to rescue her; she wants to be left completely alone in the tower.

## Question & Answers (NCERT Solutions)

### I. Thinking about the Poem

**1. How old do you think Amanda is? How do you know this?**
**Ans:** Amanda is likely a young teenager or a pre-teen, around 11 to 14 years old. We can infer this from the instructions she receives: she is doing "homework," struggling with "acne" (which typically starts in early puberty), and has a habit of biting her nails and slouching, which are common traits among children entering their teenage years.

**2. Who do you think is speaking to her?**
**Ans:** A parent, most likely her mother, is speaking to her. The instructions about cleaning her room, polishing her shoes, finishing homework, sitting up straight, and not eating chocolate (to prevent acne) are typical of a mother who is overly concerned about her daughter's discipline, appearance, and social manners.

**3. Why are Stanzas 2, 4 and 6 given in brackets?**
**Ans:** Stanzas 2, 4, and 6 are given in brackets to separate Amanda's inner thoughts from the mother's spoken instructions. The brackets represent Amanda's imagination and her mental escape from the constant nagging of her mother. While the unbracketed stanzas are loud, commanding, and harsh, the bracketed stanzas are silent, peaceful, and poetic.

**4. Who is the speaker in Stanzas 2, 4 and 6? Do you think this speaker is listening to the speaker in Stanzas 1, 3, 5, and 7?**
**Ans:** The speaker in Stanzas 2, 4, and 6 is Amanda herself (in her mind). No, Amanda is not listening to the speaker (her mother) in Stanzas 1, 3, 5, and 7. She is completely lost in her daydreams to escape the scolding. This is why her mother gets irritated and asks, "Will you please look at me when I'm speaking to you, Amanda!"

**5. What could Amanda do if she were a mermaid?**
**Ans:** If Amanda were a mermaid, she would live alone in the beautiful, calm, and green (emerald) sea. As the sole inhabitant, she would drift blissfully and peacefully with the waves, completely free from the restrictive rules, instructions, and constant nagging of her mother.

**6. Is Amanda an orphan? Why does she say so?**
**Ans:** No, Amanda is not an orphan; she lives with her parent(s). She says so because she is frustrated with being constantly controlled and scolded. She imagines herself as an orphan because orphans do not have parents to nag them or restrict their movements. She desires the absolute freedom an orphan has to roam the streets barefoot and enjoy the "golden" silence.

**7. Do you know the story of Rapunzel? Why does she want to be Rapunzel?**
**Ans:** Yes, Rapunzel is a fairy tale character who was locked in a tall tower by a witch and would let down her long golden hair for a prince to climb up and visit her. Amanda wants to be Rapunzel because life in a tall, isolated tower would be calm, peaceful ("tranquil"), and rare. However, she states she will never let down her bright hair, showing that unlike the original Rapunzel, she wants no visitors and wishes to remain entirely alone.

**8. What does the girl yearn for? What does this poem tell you about Amanda?**
**Ans:** The girl yearns for absolute freedom, peace, and silence. She wants to be left alone without constant instructions dictating her every move. The poem tells us that Amanda is a highly imaginative child who feels suffocated by the strict discipline and over-parenting in her house. Instead of arguing, she uses escapism to cope with her frustration, leading her to become moody and sulky in reality.

**9. Read the last stanza. Do you think Amanda is sulking and is moody?**
**Ans:** Yes, Amanda is likely sulking and appearing moody, but not without reason. The constant barrage of instructions and scolding has depressed her and forced her into a shell. When her daydreams are interrupted, she reacts with silence and a sullen expression. Her mother notices this moodiness but selfishly worries more about people thinking she "nagged" Amanda, rather than trying to understand her daughter's emotional state.
