# The Trees - Class 10 Comprehensive Summary & Poetic Devices

## Complete Poem Text
The trees inside are moving out into the forest,  
the forest that was empty all these days  
where no bird could sit  
no insect hide  
no sun bury its feet in shadow  
the forest that was empty all these nights  
will be full of trees by morning.  

All night the roots work  
to disengage themselves from the cracks  
in the veranda floor.  
The leaves strain toward the glass  
small twigs stiff with exertion  
long-cramped boughs shuffling under the roof  
like newly discharged patients  
half-dazed, moving  
to the clinic doors.  

I sit inside, doors open to the veranda  
writing long letters  
in which I scarcely mention the departure  
of the forest from the house.  
The night is fresh, the whole moon shines  
in a sky still open  
the smell of leaves and lichen  
still reaches like a voice into the rooms.  

My head is full of whispers  
which tomorrow will be silent.  
Listen. The glass is breaking.  
The trees are stumbling forward  
into the night. Winds rush to meet them.  
The moon is broken like a mirror,  
its pieces flash now in the crown  
of the tallest oak.  

**Adrienne Rich**

---

## Detailed Summary

### Stanza 1: The Empty Forest
The poet observes a strange phenomenon: the trees that were kept inside the house as decorative indoor plants are moving out into the forest. The poet reflects on the forest outside, which had been empty and barren for days. Because there were no trees in the forest, no birds could sit, no insects could hide, and the sunlight couldn't cast any shadows. The poet predicts that by morning, this empty forest will be full of trees again.

### Stanza 2: The Struggle for Freedom
The poet describes the immense physical struggle of the trees trying to escape their artificial confinement. All through the night, the roots work hard to break free from the cracks in the veranda floor. The leaves push against the glass windows, and the small twigs become stiff from the effort. The large branches (boughs), cramped under the roof for so long, shuffle towards the exit. The poet compares them to newly discharged hospital patients—half-dazed and confused, walking unsteadily towards the clinic doors after a long illness.

### Stanza 3: The Poet's Detachment
The poet sits inside her house with the doors to the veranda open, writing long letters. Strangely, she barely mentions the massive "departure of the forest" from her house in her letters. The atmosphere outside is described: the night is fresh, and a full moon shines in an open sky. Even though the poet ignores the physical movement of the trees in her writing, the smell of leaves and lichen reaches into the room like a voice, constantly reminding her of their silent rebellion.

### Stanza 4: The Escape
The poet's head is full of "whispers"—the sounds of the leaves and roots moving, or perhaps her own thoughts about their escape. She notes that by tomorrow, these whispers will be silent because the trees will have left. Suddenly, she asks the reader to "Listen." The glass window breaks. The trees stumble forward into the night, and the winds rush to greet them like old friends. As the tallest oak tree stands free, its branches obscure the moon, making it look like a broken mirror whose pieces are flashing like a crown on the tree's head.

## Central Themes

### 1. Conflict between Man and Nature
The poem highlights how humans have brought nature (trees) inside their homes for decoration, confining them in unnatural environments. The trees' violent struggle to break the glass and floor represents nature rebelling against human dominance and returning to its rightful place.

### 2. Feminism / Women's Liberation
Adrienne Rich was a radical feminist. The "trees" inside the house are heavily read as a metaphor for women confined within the four walls of domesticity. The roots breaking the floor and leaves smashing the glass represent women breaking patriarchal barriers and moving out into the world (the forest) to claim their rightful freedom.

### 3. The Urge for Freedom
Whether literal (nature) or metaphorical (women), the poem emphasizes that the desire for freedom is an unstoppable, destructive, and ultimately triumphant force. Confinement leads to stiffness and illness ("discharged patients"), while freedom brings power and majesty ("crown of the tallest oak").

## Detailed Poetic Devices Analysis

*   **Free Verse:** The poem does not follow a strict rhyme scheme or meter, reflecting the theme of breaking rules and seeking freedom.
*   **Personification:** Giving human qualities to the trees and nature.
    *   *sun bury its feet* (sunlight given human feet)
    *   *roots work to disengage themselves*
    *   *twigs stiff with exertion*
    *   *smell of leaves... reaches like a voice*
*   **Simile:** Comparing two different things using 'like' or 'as'.
    *   *like newly discharged patients* (comparing the shuffling branches to weak patients)
    *   *still reaches like a voice* (comparing the smell to a voice)
    *   *moon is broken like a mirror*
*   **Enjambment:** Sentences spilling over from one line to the next without punctuation. (e.g., *the forest that was empty all these days / where no bird could sit*).
*   **Metaphor:** The entire poem is an extended metaphor. The "trees" represent women, and the "house" represents the restrictive domestic sphere.
*   **Alliteration:** *l*ong *l*etters, *f*orest *f*rom

## Word Meanings & Vocabulary

*   **Exertion:** Physical or mental effort.
*   **Boughs:** Large branches of a tree.
*   **Cramped:** Restricted in space; uncomfortably small.
*   **Discharged:** Allowed to leave (a hospital or institution).
*   **Dazed:** Unable to think or react properly; bewildered.
*   **Lichen:** A slow-growing plant that forms a crust-like or branching growth on rocks or walls.
*   **Stumbling:** Tripping or losing balance while walking.
*   **Crown:** The top part of a tree.

## Key Exam Points (Memorize These)
1.  **Why was the forest empty?** Because humans had chopped down the trees or brought them indoors to decorate their houses.
2.  **The 'Discharged Patient' Simile:** The trees are compared to patients because they have been "sick" and cramped inside the house. Like patients leaving a hospital, they are weak, dazed, and unsteady as they step into freedom.
3.  **The Broken Moon Image:** As the tall oak tree moves outside, its branches cross in front of the full moon. Through the leaves and branches, the moon looks as if it has been shattered into pieces, which sit like a glowing crown on the tree's head.

## Question & Answers (NCERT Solutions)

### I. Thinking about the Poem

**1. (i) Find, in the first stanza, three things that cannot happen in a treeless forest.**
**(ii) What picture do these words create in your mind: "... sun bury its feet in shadow..."? What could the poet mean by the sun's 'feet'?**
**Ans:** (i) The three things that cannot happen in a treeless forest are: no bird can sit, no insect can hide, and the sun cannot bury its feet in shadow.
(ii) The picture created is of a hot, scorching landscape where there is no shade to provide relief from the bright sunlight. By the sun's "feet," the poet personifies the sun, referring to the sunrays that reach the earth. In a forest with trees, these rays are blocked by leaves, creating shadows on the ground where the sun's "feet" (rays) are buried or hidden.

**2. (i) Where are the trees in the poem?**
**(ii) What do their roots, their leaves, and their twigs do?**
**Ans:** (i) The trees in the poem are confined inside the poet's house, acting as indoor decorative plants.
(ii) The roots work all night to break free from the cracks in the veranda floor. The leaves strain and push hard against the glass windows to get out. The small twigs become stiff with the sheer physical exertion of trying to free themselves from their indoor confinement.

**3. (i) How does the poet describe the moon: (a) at the beginning of the third stanza, and (b) at its end? What causes this change?**
**(ii) What happens to the house when the trees move out of it?**
**(iii) Why do you think the poet does not mention "the departure of the forest from the house" in her letters?**
**Ans:** (i) (a) At the beginning of the third stanza, the poet describes the moon as whole and shining fully in an open sky. (b) At the end of the poem, the moon is described as "broken like a mirror" with its pieces flashing like a crown on the tallest oak tree. This change is caused because the trees have escaped the house. The tall oak tree stands outside, and its thick branches block the view of the full moon, making it appear shattered through the gaps in the leaves.
(ii) When the trees move out, the glass of the windows breaks, and the smell of the leaves and lichen reaches into the rooms like a voice. The house, which had imprisoned them, is now shattered and left behind.
(iii) The poet may not mention the departure in her letters because she represents the patriarchal or human establishment that has taken nature/women for granted. Acknowledging their massive, rebellious escape would mean acknowledging her own complicity in keeping them confined. Alternatively, she might be too shocked or in denial to write about this surreal, overwhelming rebellion happening right in front of her.

**4. Now that you have read the poem in detail, we can begin to ask what the poem might mean. Here are two suggestions. Can you think of others?**
**(i) Does the poem present a conflict between man and nature? Compare it with A Tiger in the Zoo. Is the poet suggesting that plants and trees, used for 'interior decoration' in cities while forests are cut down, are 'imprisoned', and need to 'break out'?**
**(ii) On the other hand, Adrienne Rich has been known to use trees as a metaphor for human beings: this is a recurrent image in her poetry. What new meanings emerge from the poem if you take its trees to be symbolic of this particular meaning?**
**Ans:** (i) Yes, the poem strongly presents a conflict between man and nature. Just like *A Tiger in the Zoo*, where the majestic tiger is locked in a concrete cell for human entertainment, the trees here are uprooted from their natural habitat and confined in pots inside houses for decoration. The poet suggests that nature cannot be subdued forever; it feels suffocated and will inevitably rebel, break the glass (barriers), and reclaim its rightful space in the wild.
(ii) If we view the poem through Adrienne Rich's feminist perspective, the "trees" symbolize women who have been confined to the domestic sphere (the house) for centuries by a patriarchal society. The "roots" and "leaves" breaking the floor and glass represent the struggles and feminist movements fighting to break the glass ceiling. The "newly discharged patients" metaphor highlights how patriarchy has weakened them, but their march towards the "forest" (the outside world, professional fields, independence) is unstoppable. When they finally achieve freedom, they are crowned with glory (the moon flashing like a crown).
