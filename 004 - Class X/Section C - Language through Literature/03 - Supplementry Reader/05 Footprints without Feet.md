# Footprints without Feet - Class 10 Comprehensive Notes

**Author:** H.G. Wells
**(From the textbook *Footprints without Feet*)**

---

## Detailed Summary

**Phase 1: The Discovery in London**
*   **The Invisible Man:** Griffin swallows certain rare drugs and becomes as transparent as a sheet of glass. 
*   **The First Crime:** After his landlord tries to eject him, Griffin sets fire to the house in revenge. To escape without being seen, he has to remove all his clothes.
*   **The Muddy Tracks:** Two boys in London are fascinated by "fresh muddy imprints of a pair of bare feet" appearing from nowhere. They follow the prints until they grow fainter and disappear.

**Phase 2: Seeking Shelter**
*   **The Big Store:** Shivering in the January cold, Griffin slips into a London store. He eats cold meat, drinks coffee, and sleeps on a pile of quilts. 
*   **The Narrow Escape:** In the morning, he is almost caught by the store assistants. He is forced to strip naked again to escape into the freezing air.
*   **The Theatrical Company:** He robs a shop in Drury Lane, taking clothes, dark glasses, a false nose, and bandages to hide his "emptiness" and look like a bandaged man.

**Phase 3: The Arrival at Iping**
*   **The Village Inn:** Seeking solitude, Griffin takes a train to the village of Iping and rents two rooms at the Coach and Horses inn.
*   **The Suspicious Guest:** His strange appearance (bandages and goggles) and his desire for solitude make the villagers suspicious. He claims he had an "accident" that affected his face.

**Phase 4: The Burglary and the "Spirits"**
*   **The Clergyman’s House:** Griffin runs out of money. One morning, a clergyman and his wife hear the chink of money being taken from their desk, but they see nobody in the room.
*   **The Strange Room:** Mrs. Hall finds Griffin’s door open and enters. Suddenly, a chair "becomes alive" and charges at her, pushing her out of the room. She believes the furniture is possessed.

**Phase 5: The Final Confrontation**
*   **The Reveal:** Mrs. Hall confronts Griffin about the strange happenings. In a rage, Griffin removes his bandages and nose. The people in the bar are horrified to see a **headless man**.
*   **Mr. Jaffers:** The village constable, Jaffers, arrives to arrest him. He is surprised to find he has to arrest a man without a head.
*   **The Disappearing Act:** Griffin starts shedding his clothes one by one. As he becomes invisible, he lands blows on the constable and the villagers. The story ends with Griffin escaping into the air, leaving the people "shaken and bewildered."

---

## Character Analysis

### A. Griffin (The Scientist/Protagonist)
*   **Brilliant but Lawless:** He is a gifted scientist who discovers how to make the human body invisible, but he uses this discovery for personal gain and revenge rather than the benefit of society.
*   **Irritable and Short-tempered:** He has a "fiery" temper. He becomes easily annoyed by people and often resorts to violence or theft when disturbed.
*   **Homeless Wanderer:** Because he has to be naked to stay invisible, he becomes a "homeless wanderer without clothes, without money, and quite invisible" in the cold London winter.
*   **Misguided Genius:** His brilliance is overshadowed by his lack of conscience. He is a "lawless person" who views others only as obstacles.

### B. Mrs. Hall (The Landlady)
*   **Business-minded:** She is the owner of the inn at Iping. She is prepared to tolerate Griffin’s strange habits and appearance because he paid her in advance.
*   **Social and Friendly:** She makes every effort to be friendly with her guest, but is repeatedly rebuffed by Griffin’s cold behavior.
*   **Superstitious:** When the furniture in Griffin's room begins to move on its own, she becomes convinced the room is "haunted by spirits."

---

## Central Themes

*   **Science without Soul:** Science is a powerful tool, but without a moral compass, it can turn a human into a monster.
*   **Isolation and its Effects:** Griffin’s invisibility physically separates him from society, which eventually leads to his mental and moral degradation.
*   **The Misuse of Power:** The story warns that having power (like invisibility) without accountability leads to lawlessness and chaos.

---

## Exam-Style Value Points (6-Mark Questions)
1. **Griffin's True Character:** While a brilliant scientist, Griffin is fundamentally a lawless and selfish person, using his invisibility for theft, assault, and escaping the consequences of his actions.
2. **The Burden of Invisibility:** The story highlights the practical disadvantages of invisibility, showing how Griffin suffers from the cold, lacks shelter, and is alienated from human society.
3. **The Contrast with Anil (The Thief's Story):** Unlike the transformative power of trust shown in *The Thief's Story*, Griffin represents a complete absence of empathy or moral development despite his high intellect.

## Question & Answers (NCERT Solutions)

### I. Read and Find out

**1. How did the invisible man first become visible?**
**Ans:** Griffin was completely invisible until he happened to step in some mud, which caused him to leave footprints as he walked. His footprints were seen by two boys, who followed him as long as the prints were visible. On getting rid of them, he went into a big London store to put on some warm clothes. After the store was shut, he got dressed by putting on shoes, an overcoat and a wide-brimmed hat. This made him visible to the people who came to work at the store the next day.

**2. Why was he wandering the streets?**
**Ans:** Griffin was a lawless person. His landlord disliked him and tried to get rid of him. In revenge, he set fire to the house. He had to remove his clothes to get away without being seen. This was why he had become a homeless wanderer-without clothes and money.

**3. Why does Mrs Hall find the scientist eccentric?**
**Ans:** The arrival of a stranger at an inn in winter was in itself a strange occurrence. In addition, the stranger had an uncommon appearance. In spite of Mrs Hall's attempts to be friendly, he would respond in a cold manner. He rebuffed her by saying that he had no desire to talk and that all he wanted was solitude. He did not wish to be disturbed in his work. For all these reasons she regarded him as an eccentric scientist.

**4. What curious episode occurs in the study?**
**Ans:** A clergyman and his wife were awakened by noises in their study very early in the morning. Creeping downstairs, they heard the chink of money being taken from the clergyman's desk. However, when they entered the study, they did not find anybody there. He and his wife looked under the desk and behind the curtains, and even up the chimney. There wasn't a sign of anybody. Yet the desk had been opened and the housekeeping money was missing. This was very curious.

**5. What other extraordinary things happen at the inn?**
**Ans:** The landlord and his wife were surprised to see the scientist's door open. Usually, it is shut and locked and he becomes furious if anyone enters his room. The opportunity seems too good to be missed. They peep round the door, see nobody and decide to investigate. The bedclothes were cold, showing that the scientist must have been up for some time. All of a sudden, Mrs. Hall heard a sniff close to her ear. A moment later, the hat on the bedpost leapt up and dashed itself into her face. Then the bedroom chair became alive. Springing into the air it charged straight at her, legs foremost. As she and her husband turned away in terror, the extraordinary chair pushed them both out of the room and then appeared to slam and lock the door after them. Mrs Hall almost fell down the stairs in hysterics. She was convinced that the room was haunted by spirits and the stranger had somehow caused these to enter into the furniture.

### II. Think About It

**1. "Griffin was rather a lawless person." Comment.**
**Ans:** Griffin was not bothered if he had harmed anybody in the fulfilment of what he wanted. He set his landlord's house on fire because the landlord tried to make him leave. Then his robberies at shops and later in the village indicate that he was a lawless person. When he encountered the landlady of the inn, he threw a chair at her and her husband. Lawless persons like Griffin never think about the safety and well-being of others. They are only concerned about themselves.

It is rightly said that Griffin was a lawless person. He was not a law-abiding citizen. A person who follows law and order of the country cannot think of damaging other's property. Griffin was not bothered if he had harmed anybody in the fulfilment of what he wanted. He set his landlord's house on fire because the landlord tried to make him leave and became a homeless wanderer. His robberies at shops and later in the village indicate that he was a lawless person. He robbed a shopkeeper of all the money he could find. He stole the housekeeping money from the clergyman's desk. Griffin also attacked Mrs Hall when she entered his room. Lawless persons like Griffin never think about the safety and well-being of others. They are only concerned about themselves. All these activities and instances prove that he was really a lawless person.

**2. How would you assess Griffin as a scientist?**
**Ans:** Griffin was a brilliant scientist, as he discovered how to make himself invisible. But he seemed to enjoy the feeling of power which he got out of his invisibility. The power to hurt anybody without getting noticed can give sadistic pleasure to some people. A true scientist should make discoveries for the larger benefit of society, not just for his own benefit. After repeated experiments, Griffin had discovered how to make the human body transparent. This was a big achievement. However, he misused his discovery for personal gains and for hurting others. Thus, though he can be termed as a brilliant scientist, he was not a noble one.
